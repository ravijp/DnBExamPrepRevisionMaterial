1. Describe the anatomical subdivisions of the larynx and the deep laryngeal spaces. How do imaging findings determine the T-stage of laryngeal carcinoma? [4+6]

2. Discuss the relative roles of CT and MRI in assessing cartilage invasion in laryngeal carcinoma, including the imaging criteria and the pitfalls of each. [5+5]

3. A 45-year-old presents with a cystic level II cervical node and no obvious primary on examination. Outline your imaging approach to the unknown primary and the relevance of HPV-associated oropharyngeal carcinoma. [4+6]

4. Describe the imaging features of nasopharyngeal carcinoma, with emphasis on skull base and foraminal/perineural spread, and state the modality of choice. [6+4]

5. Discuss the role of imaging in cervical nodal assessment and in post-treatment follow-up of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, including the role of PET-CT. [5+5]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — Laryngeal subdivisions and T-staging [4+6]

The larynx is divided craniocaudally into three regions. The **supraglottis** extends from the epiglottic tip to (but excluding) the laryngeal ventricle, and includes the epiglottis, aryepiglottic folds, arytenoids and false cords; its rich lymphatics mean early and often bilateral nodal spread. The **glottis** comprises the true vocal cords with the anterior and posterior commissures and a short subjacent extent (about 1 cm; verify exact value); its sparse lymphatics give early glottic cancer a low nodal rate. The **subglottis** runs from the lower glottis to the lower border of the cricoid. Two deep fat-filled spaces govern spread: the **pre-epiglottic space** (anterior to the epiglottis) and the **paraglottic space** (deep to the cords, the conduit for transglottic and submucosal spread); the two communicate.

Imaging, principally contrast CT with MRI for problem-solving, upstages tumour by demonstrating deep extension that endoscopy cannot see. Key T-stage drivers are: **obliteration of pre-epiglottic or paraglottic fat**, **impaired or fixed cord mobility** (correlating with deep paraglottic invasion), **transglottic spread** across the ventricle, **subglottic extension**, **cartilage invasion** (thyroid, cricoid, arytenoid), and **extralaryngeal spread** through the thyrohyoid membrane or cricothyroid space into adjacent soft tissue, strap muscles or thyroid gland. Anterior commissure involvement is a clue to possible cartilage invasion. Each of these progressively raises the T category within the AJCC framework (confirm edition-specific numbers).

### Answer 2 — CT versus MRI for cartilage invasion [5+5]

Cartilage invasion upstages laryngeal carcinoma and may change management from voice-conserving radiotherapy/partial surgery to total laryngectomy, so accurate assessment is critical. The fundamental difficulty is that laryngeal cartilage ossifies **non-uniformly and unpredictably**, so non-ossified cartilage has soft-tissue density/signal similar to tumour.

On **CT**, the reliable sign of invasion is **extralaryngeal tumour soft tissue** seen beyond the cartilage, which is specific. **Sclerosis** of cartilage is sensitive but not specific (it also occurs reactively). CT cannot easily separate non-ossified cartilage and peritumoural inflammation from true invasion, so early erosion may be missed or over-called; however CT is fast and robust against motion.

On **MRI**, non-ossified cartilage is **low signal on both T1 and T2**, while ossified cartilage shows high-T1 fatty marrow. Tumour replaces these with **intermediate T2 signal and enhancement matching the primary**. MRI is highly sensitive but its specificity is limited because **peritumoural oedema/inflammation** is bright on T2 and enhances, causing over-staging; using intermediate (rather than very high) T2 signal and low ADC similar to tumour improves specificity (verify thresholds). MRI is more motion-prone.

In practice CT is first-line for gross disease and MRI is the problem-solver, and the two are complementary; correlation reduces both over- and under-staging.

### Answer 3 — Cystic neck node, unknown primary, HPV [4+6]

A cystic level II node in an adult should be regarded as **metastatic squamous cell carcinoma until proven otherwise**, classically from an **HPV/p16-positive oropharyngeal primary** (tonsil or tongue base), not a branchial cleft cyst, particularly in a younger non-smoker. HPV-associated oropharyngeal SCC tends to have a **small or occult primary** with disproportionately large, often **cystic/necrotic nodes**, and carries a substantially better prognosis, which is why it has a **separate, downstaged TNM staging system**.

Imaging approach to the unknown primary: begin with **contrast CT of the neck (skull base to thoracic inlet)** and/or **MRI of the oropharynx/nasopharynx** to scrutinise the tonsillar fossae, tongue base, nasopharynx (fossa of Rosenmuller) and pyriform sinuses for asymmetry, an enhancing mucosal/submucosal mass, or focal fullness. **FDG PET-CT** is particularly valuable to localise an occult primary and to detect distant metastasis or a synchronous second primary. The cystic node itself is characterised by size, necrosis and irregular margins suggesting extranodal extension. Tissue diagnosis is obtained by **US-guided FNAC/core biopsy** of the node with **p16/HPV testing**, and directed (often image-guided or examination-under-anaesthesia with tonsillectomy) biopsy of the suspected primary site. The combined anatomical and metabolic imaging plus HPV status directs staging and treatment.

### Answer 4 — Nasopharyngeal carcinoma imaging [6+4]

Nasopharyngeal carcinoma (NPC) most commonly arises in the **fossa of Rosenmuller** and frequently presents late with nodal disease (including retropharyngeal nodes) or cranial nerve symptoms because of deep and skull base spread. **MRI is the modality of choice** owing to superior soft-tissue contrast and assessment of marrow, perineural and intracranial extension; CT is complementary for cortical bone erosion.

Imaging features: an enhancing soft-tissue mass centred on the lateral nasopharyngeal recess with **asymmetry/obliteration of the fossa of Rosenmuller**, deep extension into the **parapharyngeal and masticator spaces**, and **skull base invasion** seen as loss of the normal high-T1 fatty marrow signal of the clivus and petrous apex. Spread through the **skull base foramina** is characteristic: **foramen ovale** (V3, with perineural spread to the cavernous sinus), **foramen lacerum**, and the **jugular foramen**, producing foraminal enlargement, loss of fat and abnormal enhancement. Intracranial and cavernous sinus extension and involvement of cranial nerves are best shown on post-contrast fat-suppressed T1 sequences.

Nodal spread, including bilateral and retropharyngeal nodes, is common. NPC has its **own dedicated staging system** distinct from other mucosal head and neck SCC. PET-CT supplements MRI for distant metastases and surveillance.

### Answer 5 — Nodal assessment and post-treatment follow-up [5+5]

**Nodal assessment** uses CT/MRI for anatomical mapping by neck level (I-VII) and US for targeted evaluation with FNAC. Imaging features favouring malignant nodes are: **rounded shape with loss of the normal oval morphology**, **loss of the fatty hilum**, **internal necrosis/cystic change**, **heterogeneous enhancement**, peripheral/chaotic Doppler vascularity, and a short-axis size above the site-specific threshold (commonly above about 1 cm, lower for retropharyngeal and level II nodes; verify exact value). **Irregular, ill-defined or infiltrating margins indicate extranodal extension (ENE)**, an adverse feature incorporated into current N-staging for most non-NPC, non-HPV sites. Number, size and laterality also determine the N category.

**Post-treatment follow-up** distinguishes expected post-therapy change (oedema, fibrosis, distorted anatomy) from residual or recurrent tumour, which is difficult on CT/MRI alone. **FDG PET-CT** is the key tool: performed at an adequate interval after chemoradiotherapy (commonly around 12 weeks; verify exact value) it has a **high negative predictive value** for residual nodal disease, allowing many patients to avoid planned neck dissection. Imaging too early risks **false-positive inflammatory uptake**; physiological uptake is another pitfall. PET-CT also detects distant metastasis and second primaries. Suspicious residual masses or new enhancing/FDG-avid soft tissue prompt biopsy. A combined anatomical (CT/MRI) and metabolic (PET-CT) strategy gives the most reliable surveillance.
