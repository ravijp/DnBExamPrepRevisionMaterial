## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — Le Fort fractures (CT) [6+4]

Le Fort fractures are transverse fracture patterns of the midface, unified by the fact that all three involve the **pterygoid plates** — confirming pterygoid plate disruption is the single most useful diagnostic check.

**Le Fort I (Guerin, "floating palate")** is a horizontal fracture passing above the tooth apices through the maxillary antra and lower nasal septum, freeing the alveolus and hard palate. **Le Fort II (pyramidal)** runs through the nasal bones, frontal process of maxilla, medial and inferior orbital walls and rims and the zygomaticomaxillary buttress, mobilising a central pyramid of nose and maxilla; the infraorbital nerve is at risk. **Le Fort III (craniofacial disjunction, "floating face")** separates the entire facial skeleton from the skull base through the nasofrontal suture, medial and lateral orbital walls and zygomatic arches. Pure patterns are uncommon; injuries are frequently mixed and asymmetric.

**CT approach (10 marks split):** thin-section multidetector CT with bone and soft-tissue windows and **multiplanar (axial, coronal, sagittal) plus 3D surface reconstructions** is the standard; plain films are insensitive and superseded. The radiologist should: identify the level(s) by tracing the classic fracture lines; **specifically confirm pterygoid plate fractures**; and then actively exclude associated injuries — **haemosinus** and air-fluid levels, orbital floor or medial wall blowout with fat/muscle herniation and possible **entrapment**, globe and optic nerve injury, orbital emphysema, and (especially in Le Fort II/III and NOE) **skull base fracture, intracranial injury and CSF leak**. Displacement, comminution and the relationship to the dental occlusion guide surgical fixation. A clear, structured CT report mapping each buttress is what scores marks.

### Answer 2 — Odontogenic cysts and tumours [5+5]

The first-line tool for these lesions is the **dental radiograph** — orthopantomogram (OPG) and periapical views — with **CBCT/MDCT** for cortical and three-dimensional assessment and **MRI** as a problem-solver for soft-tissue or marrow extent.

A **radicular (periapical) cyst** is the commonest jaw cyst, inflammatory in origin, arising at the **apex of a non-vital carious tooth** as a small well-corticated unilocular periapical lucency. A **dentigerous (follicular) cyst** is developmental and surrounds the **crown of an unerupted tooth** (often a third molar or canine), attaching at the cemento-enamel junction as a unilocular pericoronal lucency. The **odontogenic keratocyst (OKC)** is a developmental but aggressive lesion that grows **along the marrow space** of the mandible with relatively little buccolingual expansion, may be uni- or multilocular, and has a **high recurrence rate**; multiple OKCs should prompt a search for **naevoid basal cell carcinoma (Gorlin) syndrome**. **Ameloblastoma** is a locally aggressive benign tumour, classically an expansile **multilocular "soap-bubble"/"honeycomb"** lucency in the **posterior mandible/ramus**, with cortical thinning or breach and characteristic **knife-edge root resorption**.

A useful comparison: radicular sits at the apex (non-vital tooth), dentigerous hugs the crown (unerupted tooth), OKC spreads through marrow, and ameloblastoma is the multilocular expansile soap-bubble. On CT/CBCT one assesses locularity, cortical expansion or perforation, relationship to the inferior alveolar canal, and root resorption. MRI helps separate a simple fluid-filled cyst from a solid enhancing tumour and defines soft-tissue extent before surgery. Recurrence risk (high for OKC, moderate-high for ameloblastoma) drives the need for accurate margin mapping and follow-up.

### Answer 3 — TMJ internal derangement on MRI [4+6]

**MRI is the reference standard** for TMJ internal derangement because it directly depicts the fibrocartilaginous disc, which CT cannot. The **protocol (4 marks)** uses small surface coils with dedicated **bilateral oblique-sagittal** sequences (perpendicular to the long axis of the condyle) and **oblique-coronal** images, acquired in both **closed- and open-mouth** positions; proton-density/T1 shows disc morphology and position, while T2/STIR demonstrates joint effusion and marrow oedema. Dynamic/cine acquisitions can capture the click during opening.

**Findings (6 marks):** the normal disc is a low-signal **biconcave "bow-tie"** with the thicker **posterior band at the 12 o'clock** position over the condyle in the closed mouth, translating anteriorly with the condyle on opening so that the intermediate zone stays between the condyle and articular eminence. In **anterior disc displacement with reduction**, the disc lies anterior to the condyle in the closed mouth but **returns to a normal condyle-disc relationship on opening**, typically with an audible click. In **anterior disc displacement without reduction ("closed lock")**, the disc **remains anteriorly displaced on opening**, condylar translation is restricted and the disc often becomes deformed, folded or fragmented. Additional features include **joint effusion** (bright on T2), and in advanced/degenerative disease, condylar flattening, osteophytosis, sclerosis and marrow oedema. Less commonly there is medial, lateral or posterior displacement. The report should state disc position in both phases, presence and direction of displacement, whether it reduces, effusion, and any degenerative change, as this directly guides conservative versus surgical management.

### Answer 4 — Salivary gland imaging and tumours [4+6]

**Modality roles (4 marks):** **Ultrasound** is first-line for the superficial parotid and submandibular glands — radiation-free, characterises ductal dilatation and calculi (echogenic focus with posterior shadowing) and masses, and guides FNAC/biopsy, but cannot assess the deep parotid lobe or parapharyngeal extension. **CT** (with an unenhanced phase for calculi, plus contrast) defines stones, sialadenitis, abscess, deep-lobe disease and nodal staging. **MRI** gives the best soft-tissue characterisation, defines tumour margins and is essential for detecting **perineural spread**; MR sialography non-invasively maps the ducts. Conventional sialography and scintigraphy have niche roles.

**Tumours (6 marks):** **Pleomorphic adenoma** is the commonest salivary tumour, benign, usually in the parotid superficial lobe — well-defined, lobulated, hypoechoic on US with posterior enhancement and **markedly bright on T2** MRI with a capsule; long-standing lesions carry a small risk of malignant transformation. **Warthin tumour** occurs in older male smokers, is frequently **bilateral/multifocal**, shows cystic/anechoic components, tends to be lower on T2, and characteristically **concentrates Tc-99m pertechnetate** on scintigraphy. **Adenoid cystic carcinoma** is a malignant tumour with a predilection for minor glands and the submandibular gland; it is often low on T2 (cellular), shows infiltrative margins, and is notorious for **perineural spread** — best shown on fat-suppressed post-contrast MRI as nerve thickening and enhancement with foraminal fat loss. As a rule, the larger the gland, the lower the proportion of malignant tumours. Imaging features overlap, so tissue sampling remains important.

### Answer 5 — Sialolithiasis, sialadenitis and perineural spread [5+5]

**Sialolithiasis and sialadenitis (5 marks):** calculi most often affect the **submandibular gland and Wharton's duct** (~80%), favoured by mucinous saliva and an uphill duct course; most submandibular stones are **radiopaque** and may be seen on a plain occlusal film, whereas many parotid stones are radiolucent, limiting plain-film sensitivity. **Ultrasound** is first-line, showing an echogenic focus with **posterior acoustic shadowing** and upstream ductal dilatation. **Unenhanced CT** is highly sensitive for calculi; contrast-enhanced CT and MRI demonstrate the obstructed gland. **Sialadenitis** appears as an enlarged, abnormally enhancing gland with surrounding fat stranding and ductal dilatation; chronic disease leads to a small fibrotic gland. **MR or CT sialography** maps ductal strictures and stones non-invasively. In **Sjogren syndrome**, US shows a heterogeneous "leopard-skin"/honeycomb gland with multiple small hypo-/anechoic foci.

**Perineural tumour spread (5 marks):** this is the spread of malignancy **along nerve sheaths**, classically by **adenoid cystic carcinoma**, and it markedly worsens prognosis and alters the surgical and radiotherapy field. The key relevant nerves are the **facial nerve** and branches of the **trigeminal nerve** (notably the auriculotemporal-facial communication and V3 through the **foramen ovale**). **MRI with fat-suppressed post-contrast sequences** is the modality of choice: look for **nerve thickening and abnormal enhancement**, **enlargement of and loss of fat within the neural foramen**, replacement of the normal CSF in Meckel's cave, convex enhancement, and secondary **denervation changes** in the supplied muscles (oedema then atrophy). CT may show foraminal widening. Because spread can be discontinuous ("skip" lesions), the entire course of the suspected nerve to the skull base and beyond must be imaged. Reporting perineural spread explicitly is essential for treatment planning.
