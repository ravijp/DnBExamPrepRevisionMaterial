## Practice questions

*Note: the following are practice questions written in DNB style. They are NOT cited from any real NBE past paper.*

1. Classify congenital neck masses by location and embryological origin. Describe the imaging approach to a midline neck cyst in a child, and state the single most important pre-operative imaging safeguard. [6+4]

2. A 6-year-old presents with a painless cystic swelling at the angle of the mandible. Discuss the differential diagnosis, the characteristic imaging features of a second branchial cleft cyst across US/CT/MRI, and the key malignant mimic to consider in an adult. [4+4+2]

3. Describe the ISSVA classification of vascular anomalies. Outline the imaging features that distinguish a low-flow lymphatic malformation from a high-flow arteriovenous malformation. [5+5]

4. Compare dermoid, epidermoid and ranula of the floor of mouth/midline neck. Emphasise the MRI features (including DWI) that separate them, and describe the imaging of a plunging ranula. [4+3+3]

5. Discuss ectopic thyroid tissue in the neck. Describe the role of ultrasound and radionuclide imaging, and explain why this is critical in the work-up of a suspected thyroglossal duct cyst. [5+5]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 [6+4]

Congenital neck masses are best classified by **location** and **embryological origin**. By location: **midline** lesions include the thyroglossal duct cyst (commonest), dermoid/epidermoid, ectopic (lingual) thyroid and ranula; **lateral** lesions are dominated by branchial cleft cysts (2nd commonest congenital neck mass), typically along the anterior border of sternocleidomastoid (SCM); **posterior-triangle / trans-spatial** lesions are typically lymphatic malformations; and **vascular malformations** may occur anywhere. By embryology: thyroglossal duct remnants (failed involution from foramen cecum to thyroid bed), branchial apparatus remnants (1st-4th clefts/pouches), lymphatic/vascular dysgenesis (ISSVA framework), and ectodermal inclusion rests (dermoid/epidermoid).

For a **midline neck cyst in a child**, ultrasound is first-line: it confirms the cystic nature, defines the relationship to the hyoid, and applies Doppler to exclude vascularity. A thyroglossal duct cyst is a well-defined midline/near-midline anechoic-to-hypoechoic cyst embedded in or abutting the hyoid, classically moving with tongue protrusion. CT or MRI is used when the anatomy is complex, infection is suspected, or a soft-tissue nodule (raising concern for rare thyroglossal duct carcinoma) is present; MRI gives the best soft-tissue characterisation, with a T2-bright, variably T1 cyst.

The **single most important pre-operative safeguard** is to **confirm a normal orthotopic thyroid gland in the thyroid bed** before excision (Sistrunk procedure). The cyst, or an associated base-of-tongue lingual thyroid, may represent the patient's only functioning thyroid tissue, and its removal would render the child hypothyroid. Ultrasound usually documents the orthotopic gland; radionuclide scintigraphy (Tc-99m pertechnetate or I-123) is added when ectopic functioning tissue is suspected.

### Answer 2 [4+4+2]

A painless cystic swelling at the **angle of the mandible** in a child has a differential of: **second branchial cleft cyst** (most likely at this site), infected/cystic lymph node, lymphatic malformation, dermoid, and an abscess if there are inflammatory signs. The location anterior to SCM and lateral to the carotid space strongly favours a second branchial cleft cyst.

**Imaging features of a second branchial cleft cyst:**
- **US:** a well-defined anechoic-to-hypoechoic cyst anteromedial to SCM; internal debris and a thicker, more vascular wall develop after infection; no internal arterial flow.
- **CT (contrast):** a smooth, thin-walled, low-attenuation cyst located posterolateral to the submandibular gland, lateral to the carotid space and anteromedial to SCM. A characteristic **"beak"** of tissue may point medially toward the carotid bifurcation between the internal and external carotid arteries. Wall thickening and enhancement indicate infection.
- **MRI:** T2 hyperintense, T1 signal varying with protein content, thin non-enhancing wall when uninfected; best for mapping extent.

The **key malignant mimic in an adult** is a **cystic nodal metastasis**, classically from HPV-related oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma. A new unilocular cystic neck mass in an adult should therefore be regarded as cystic metastasis until proven otherwise, prompting search for a primary and tissue sampling rather than assumption of a benign branchial cyst.

### Answer 3 [5+5]

The **ISSVA classification** divides vascular anomalies into two broad groups. **Vascular tumours** are true neoplasms with endothelial proliferation, the prototype being the **infantile haemangioma** (a proliferating phase followed by involution). **Vascular malformations** are developmental errors of vessel morphogenesis that grow with the child and do not involute; they are subdivided by **flow dynamics**:
- **Low-flow malformations:** capillary, venous, and lymphatic (macrocystic, microcystic or mixed), plus combined forms.
- **High-flow malformations:** arteriovenous malformation (AVM) and arteriovenous fistula.

This flow-based split directly drives imaging and management.

**Distinguishing low-flow lymphatic malformation from high-flow AVM:**
- A **lymphatic malformation** is a low-flow, trans-spatial, multiseptated cystic mass favouring the posterior triangle. On US it shows septated cysts, frequently with **fluid-fluid levels** from prior haemorrhage, and **no significant internal arterial flow** on Doppler. On MRI it is lobulated with very high T2 signal, fluid-fluid levels, and **only thin septal enhancement** (the locule contents do not enhance). It characteristically insinuates between structures without mass effect proportional to its size.
- An **AVM** is high-flow. Doppler shows numerous flow channels with **high-velocity, low-resistance arterial waveforms** and arterialised draining veins. On MRI there are clustered **flow voids** forming a "bag of worms" with **no discrete soft-tissue mass**; dynamic MRA/CTA confirms enlarged feeding arteries and early venous drainage. The absence of a true mass and the presence of high-flow vessels separate it decisively from the cystic, flow-negative lymphatic malformation.

### Answer 4 [4+3+3]

**Dermoid, epidermoid and ranula** can all present as midline or floor-of-mouth cystic lesions, but their contents differ.

- **Epidermoid** is lined by squamous epithelium and filled with keratin debris; it follows fluid on most sequences but shows **characteristic restricted diffusion (bright on DWI, low ADC)**, which is its single most useful discriminator.
- **Dermoid** contains dermal adnexal elements and **fat**: it shows T1-hyperintense components that suppress on fat-saturation, may be heterogeneous, and can show a "sack of marbles" appearance from fat globules. It does not typically restrict diffusion uniformly.
- **Ranula** is a mucus retention cyst/pseudocyst arising from the sublingual gland: a thin-walled, T2-bright, T1-low (or higher if proteinaceous) cyst in the floor of mouth.

**MRI separation:** epidermoid = diffusion restriction; dermoid = fat signal; ranula = simple sublingual-space cyst with thin or imperceptible wall.

**Plunging (diving) ranula:** when a simple sublingual ranula ruptures or extends, it herniates **through or around the posterior free margin of mylohyoid** into the submandibular space and beyond. The diagnostic clue is the **"tail" sign** — a tapering tract that points back into the sublingual space, confirming the origin. On CT/MRI it is a unilocular, thin-walled, fluid-density/intensity collection straddling the mylohyoid; the tail and the lack of internal enhancement distinguish it from an abscess or cystic neoplasm.

### Answer 5 [5+5]

**Ectopic thyroid tissue** results from arrest of thyroid descent along the thyroglossal tract between the foramen cecum and the normal thyroid bed. The commonest and most important location is the **lingual thyroid** at the tongue base, but ectopic tissue can lie anywhere along the tract, including within a thyroglossal duct cyst. Ectopic tissue may be the patient's **only functioning thyroid tissue**, and lingual thyroid can be hypofunctioning, leading to hypothyroidism or compensatory enlargement.

**Role of imaging:**
- **Ultrasound** assesses the thyroid bed to determine whether an orthotopic gland is present and characterises any neck cyst or tongue-base mass. It is the first-line, radiation-free study, particularly in children.
- **Radionuclide imaging** with **Tc-99m pertechnetate (or I-123)** is decisive: it demonstrates **functioning thyroid tissue**, showing focal uptake at the tongue base in lingual thyroid and confirming whether uptake is present or absent in the thyroid bed. This distinguishes functioning ectopic thyroid from other tongue-base masses. (CT/MRI add anatomical detail; a lingual thyroid is often hyperdense on CT due to iodine content.)

**Why critical in suspected thyroglossal duct cyst:** before excising a midline cyst (Sistrunk procedure) or a tongue-base mass, one must be certain that **normal orthotopic thyroid tissue exists elsewhere**. If the cyst or ectopic focus is the only functioning thyroid, removal causes iatrogenic hypothyroidism. Ultrasound documentation of an orthotopic gland, supplemented by scintigraphy when ectopic functioning tissue is suspected, is therefore an essential pre-operative safeguard.
