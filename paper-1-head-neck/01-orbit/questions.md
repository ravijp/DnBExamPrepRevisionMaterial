## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — Causes of unilateral proptosis and imaging approach [6+4]

Proptosis is best classified by orbital compartment, which directs the differential. **Intraocular** causes are uncommon causes of proptosis but include large retinoblastoma or melanoma. **Intraconal** lesions cause axial proptosis: cavernous haemangioma (commonest benign adult orbital mass), optic nerve glioma, optic nerve sheath meningioma, schwannoma and orbital varix. **Conal** (extraocular muscle) disease includes thyroid eye disease — the single commonest cause of unilateral and bilateral proptosis in adults — orbital myositis, metastasis and lymphoma. **Extraconal** lesions cause eccentric proptosis: dermoid, lacrimal gland tumours (pleomorphic adenoma, adenoid cystic carcinoma), rhabdomyosarcoma, subperiosteal abscess and lymphoma. **Vascular** causes include carotid-cavernous fistula and varix, and **inflammatory/infective** causes include cellulitis and idiopathic orbital inflammation.

The imaging approach answers structured questions. First clarify the clinical pattern: axial proptosis points intraconally, eccentric to an extraconal mass, pulsatile to a vascular lesion (CCF, or absent sphenoid wing in NF1), and Valsalva-dependent to a varix; bilateral disease favours thyroid eye disease, lymphoma or IgG4. CT is first-line in acute trauma and infection, defining bone, calcification and abscess. MRI with fat-suppressed T2 and post-contrast T1 is superior for soft-tissue characterisation, the optic nerve, the orbital apex and intracranial or perineural extension. Ultrasound is reserved for the globe and anterior orbit. The combination of compartment, laterality and lesion behaviour (mass, infiltrative, vascular, inflammatory) narrows the diagnosis efficiently.

### Answer 2 — Leukocoria in a child [5+5]

Leukocoria is a white pupillary reflex; the imaging task is to distinguish a malignant mass from benign mimics, with calcification and globe size as the two key discriminators. **Retinoblastoma** is the commonest intraocular malignancy of childhood (typically under three years), often presenting with leukocoria. CT and US show an enhancing intraocular mass that characteristically **calcifies**; MRI is used to assess optic nerve invasion and to exclude a "trilateral" pineal tumour, while avoiding over-reliance on radiation in these children. A calcified intraocular mass in a young child is retinoblastoma until proven otherwise.

**Persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous (PHPV)** presents in a **microphthalmic** eye with retrolental soft tissue and a persistent hyaloid remnant; crucially there is **no calcification**, and the small globe distinguishes it from retinoblastoma. **Coats disease** typically affects older boys with a **normal-size** globe and shows an exudative retinal detachment with lipoproteinaceous subretinal exudate that is T1 hyperintense; there is **no mass and no calcification**. Other causes include toxocariasis and retinopathy of prematurity.

A practical grid uses two axes: presence of calcification and globe size. Calcified mass equals retinoblastoma; no calcification with a small globe equals PHPV; no calcification, normal globe and exudative detachment without a mass equals Coats. US characterises the intraocular contents and detects calcification; MRI characterises soft tissue and excludes nerve and intracranial spread. This systematic use of imaging reliably separates a sight- and life-threatening malignancy from benign causes.

### Answer 3 — Intraconal mass differential [4+6]

Slowly progressive painless axial proptosis in an adult suggests a well-defined intraconal mass. The principal differentials are cavernous haemangioma, optic nerve glioma, optic nerve sheath meningioma, schwannoma and orbital varix. The three classic tumours are separated by morphology and enhancement.

**Cavernous haemangioma** (orbital venous malformation) is the commonest benign orbital mass in adults. It is a well-circumscribed intraconal mass, often lateral to the optic nerve, and its signature is **progressive "fill-in" enhancement** — a focal enhancing node on early images that spreads to fill the lesion on delayed images. It is slow-flow and does not pulsate.

**Optic nerve glioma** is primarily a paediatric tumour strongly associated with **neurofibromatosis type 1**; in an adult it is less likely, but when present it produces **fusiform enlargement of the optic nerve** in which tumour and nerve are inseparable, often with a kink or buckle. **Optic nerve sheath meningioma** is the relevant adult lesion, with a female predilection. It grows around the nerve, producing the **tram-track sign** (enhancing sheath flanking the non-enhancing nerve on axial images) and a doughnut pattern on coronal images; it may calcify. The discriminator is that the meningioma surrounds an identifiable, preserved nerve, whereas the glioma expands the nerve itself.

MRI with fat-suppressed post-contrast T1 is the key sequence, defining the nerve, the apex and any intracranial extension; CT adds detection of calcification. Cavernous haemangioma is confirmed by its fill-in enhancement, and a varix by Valsalva-dependent distension.

### Answer 4 — Thyroid eye disease and Chandler classification [5+5]

**Thyroid eye disease (Graves orbitopathy)** is the commonest cause of proptosis in adults, frequently bilateral. The imaging hallmark on CT and MRI is **fusiform enlargement of the extraocular muscle bellies with sparing of the tendinous insertions** — this tendon-sparing pattern is the discriminator from orbital myositis, which involves the tendon. Multiple muscles are typically affected in a characteristic order (inferior, medial, superior, lateral rectus; mnemonic I-M-S-L), often symmetrically, and intraconal fat may also increase. The critical complication is **apical crowding causing compressive optic neuropathy**, so the orbital apex must be specifically assessed. Coronal images best demonstrate muscle enlargement and apical crowding.

**Orbital cellulitis** is staged by the **Chandler classification**: (1) preseptal cellulitis, anterior to the orbital septum; (2) orbital (postseptal) cellulitis; (3) subperiosteal abscess; (4) orbital abscess; and (5) cavernous sinus thrombosis. Most disease is sinogenic, spreading through the lamina papyracea from the ethmoid sinuses. The imaging priority is to separate preseptal from postseptal disease and to detect a drainable subperiosteal or orbital abscess and intracranial extension.

Regarding modality roles: contrast-enhanced **CT** is first-line in acute orbital infection because it is rapid, defines bone and sinus disease, and detects an abscess requiring drainage. **MRI** is superior for intracranial and cavernous sinus complications and for soft-tissue detail, and is the better tool when optic nerve compression or cavernous sinus thrombosis is suspected. In thyroid eye disease, CT also provides the bony detail relevant to surgical decompression planning, while MRI assesses disease activity and the optic nerve.

### Answer 5 — Orbital trauma evaluation [10]

CT is the first-line modality for orbital trauma: it is rapid, multiplanar, and ideal for bone, most foreign bodies and acute haemorrhage. MRI is reserved for problem-solving soft-tissue and optic nerve injury and for non-metallic foreign bodies, but only after metal has been excluded.

**Blow-out fracture** results from a sudden rise in intraorbital pressure that fractures the weakest walls — the **orbital floor** (into the maxillary sinus) and the **medial wall** (lamina papyracea). CT shows the bony defect and herniation of orbital fat and muscle, including the soft-tissue **"teardrop" sign** projecting into the antrum. The crucial complication to report is **inferior rectus entrapment**, which restricts upgaze and causes diplopia and may need urgent surgery. Also assess for orbital emphysema with medial wall fractures and for infraorbital nerve involvement with floor fractures.

**Intraocular foreign body** evaluation requires localisation as intra- versus extraocular and characterisation of material. CT readily detects metallic and most glass foreign bodies as high-density objects; organic or wooden material may be CT-occult or mimic air and is better characterised on MRI — but a metallic foreign body must be excluded on CT before any MRI to avoid magnetically induced injury.

**Globe rupture** is a surgical emergency. CT signs include an altered globe contour with volume loss, a flat or deepened anterior chamber, scleral discontinuity, intraocular air or haemorrhage, and lens dislocation. Ultrasound must be avoided because globe compression risks extrusion of contents. The radiologist should therefore localise injury by compartment, identify entrapment and rupture promptly, and select CT first with MRI only when metal is excluded.
