1. Discuss the role of HRCT and MRI in the evaluation of acquired cholesteatoma. Describe the characteristic CT findings, the value of diffusion-weighted MRI, and the complications you must actively look for. [6+4]

2. Classify temporal bone fractures. Compare longitudinal and transverse fractures and explain why the otic-capsule-sparing versus otic-capsule-violating scheme is preferred. [4+6]

3. A young adult presents with a pulsatile retrotympanic mass and conductive hearing loss. Describe your imaging approach and the differentiating features of glomus tympanicum, glomus jugulare and a facial nerve schwannoma. [5+5]

4. Enumerate the causes of a cerebellopontine angle / internal auditory canal mass. Tabulate the distinguishing MRI features of vestibular schwannoma, meningioma and epidermoid. [4+6]

5. Describe the imaging of otosclerosis and of petrous apicitis. Include the relevant anatomy, modality of choice and key findings of each. [5+5]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — Cholesteatoma (HRCT and MRI) [6+4]

Cholesteatoma is a keratinising squamous epithelial collection; the common acquired pars flaccida type arises in Prussak space, the lateral epitympanic recess bounded laterally by the scutum and pars flaccida and medially by the ossicular neck. HRCT (thin-section, bone algorithm, axial and coronal) is the first-line modality and the surgical roadmap. The hallmark is a non-dependent soft-tissue mass in Prussak space causing bone erosion: blunting or frank erosion of the scutum, medial displacement and erosion of the ossicles (the long process of the incus and the stapes superstructure are particularly vulnerable), and widening of the aditus ad antrum. Beyond making the diagnosis, the report must actively search for complications, because these change management: erosion of the lateral semicircular canal (labyrinthine fistula, presenting with vertigo), dehiscence of the tegmen tympani (route for intracranial spread, meningitis or abscess), and erosion of the facial nerve canal (risk to CN VII).

The limitation of CT is that it shows erosion and soft tissue but cannot distinguish cholesteatoma from granulation tissue, fibrosis or effusion. This is where MRI, specifically non-echo-planar diffusion-weighted imaging, is decisive: keratin debris shows true restricted diffusion (high signal on DWI with low ADC), whereas granulation and effusion do not. Cholesteatoma does not enhance centrally; only a thin peripheral rim of granulation may enhance on post-gadolinium T1. DWI is especially valuable in the post-operative ear to detect residual or recurrent disease and to reduce the need for second-look surgery. In summary, CT shows that something is eroding bone and MRI/DWI confirms it is cholesteatoma.

### Answer 2 — Temporal bone fractures [4+6]

Temporal bone fractures are classically classified by their orientation relative to the long axis of the petrous pyramid into longitudinal, transverse and mixed/oblique types; HRCT with multiplanar reformats is the modality of choice. Longitudinal fractures, the commoner type, run parallel to the petrous long axis and usually follow a temporoparietal blow. They tend to traverse the external auditory canal and middle ear, producing ossicular disruption and a conductive hearing loss, and commonly spare the otic capsule; facial nerve injury is comparatively less frequent. Transverse fractures run perpendicular to the long axis, typically after an occipital impact, and may cross the otic capsule and internal auditory canal, producing sensorineural hearing loss and a higher rate of facial nerve injury.

In practice the orientation scheme is imperfect because most fractures are oblique or mixed and orientation correlates poorly with the clinically important outcomes. The modern, more prognostic classification is otic-capsule-sparing versus otic-capsule-violating. Otic-capsule-violating fractures cross the dense labyrinthine bone and carry a substantially higher risk of profound sensorineural deafness, facial nerve palsy, cerebrospinal fluid leak and meningitis, whereas otic-capsule-sparing fractures more often cause conductive loss and have a better prognosis. Whichever scheme is used, the report must specifically address: ossicular dislocation (incudostapedial and incudomalleal disruption), involvement of the facial nerve canal (especially the perigeniculate region), integrity of the tegmen and any CSF-leak risk, the presence of pneumolabyrinth, and any vascular injury near the carotid canal or sigmoid sinus. This targeted checklist, rather than a label alone, guides surgical and conservative management.

### Answer 3 — Pulsatile retrotympanic mass [5+5]

A pulsatile retrotympanic mass with conductive hearing loss raises a vascular middle ear lesion, chiefly paraganglioma, and imaging must both characterise the mass and exclude vascular variants (aberrant internal carotid artery, dehiscent jugular bulb) before any biopsy. The approach combines HRCT for bone detail and contrast-enhanced MRI for soft tissue and extent. Glomus tympanicum is a paraganglioma confined to the middle ear: HRCT shows a small enhancing soft-tissue mass sitting on the cochlear promontory, typically with intact underlying bone, correlating with the clinically visible red retrotympanic mass. Glomus jugulare arises at the jugular foramen; HRCT shows a permeative, moth-eaten (irregular, destructive) erosion of the jugular foramen margins, and on MRI a larger lesion shows the classic salt-and-pepper appearance, the pepper being serpiginous flow voids from its hypervascularity, with avid enhancement.

The key differentiator from a schwannoma is the bone margin: paraganglioma produces permeative-destructive erosion, whereas a jugular foramen schwannoma produces smooth, sharply corticated scalloping and enlargement without aggressive destruction. Facial nerve schwannoma is a quite different entity: it is an enhancing fusiform mass that follows and smoothly expands the facial nerve canal, commonly centred at the geniculate ganglion and sometimes extending as a dumbbell across the labyrinthine and tympanic segments; recognising that it tracks the facial canal and remodels bone smoothly distinguishes it from the destructive paraganglioma. Because paragangliomas are intensely vascular, biopsy in the clinic is hazardous, so the imaging diagnosis is critical, and catheter angiography with preoperative embolisation may be planned. Always document multicentricity, since paragangliomas can be multiple, particularly in familial syndromes.

### Answer 4 — CPA / IAC mass [4+6]

Causes of a cerebellopontine angle or internal auditory canal mass include, in approximate order of frequency, vestibular schwannoma (by far the commonest), meningioma and epidermoid cyst, followed by less common lesions such as facial nerve schwannoma, arachnoid cyst, metastasis, and aneurysm/dolichoectasia of the vertebrobasilar system. Contrast-enhanced MRI with heavily T2-weighted high-resolution sequences (CISS/FIESTA), thin-section pre- and post-gadolinium T1, and diffusion-weighted imaging is the modality of choice; CT is reserved for bone (hyperostosis, calcification) and for patients who cannot have MRI. The three classic lesions are distinguished as follows.

| Feature | Vestibular schwannoma | Meningioma | Epidermoid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Centre/origin | Within IAC, extends to CPA (ice-cream cone), on CN VIII | Off-centre, broad dural base on petrous bone | CPA cistern, insinuating around structures |
| Enhancement | Avid, homogeneous (may be cystic) | Avid, homogeneous, with dural tail | None |
| Angle to petrous bone | Acute | Obtuse | n/a |
| Bone / porus | Funnels and widens the porus acusticus | Hyperostosis; centred off the porus | No bony change |
| DWI | No restriction | No restriction | Restricts (bright on DWI) |
| Calcification | No | May calcify (CT) | No |

The vestibular schwannoma classically widens the internal auditory canal and shows the ice-cream-cone shape; bilateral lesions suggest neurofibromatosis type 2. The meningioma is identified by its broad dural base, obtuse angle to the bone and dural tail. The epidermoid is the trap: it follows CSF signal on conventional sequences and can mimic an arachnoid cyst, but it restricts diffusion (bright on DWI) and insinuates rather than displaces, which is the discriminator. This DWI behaviour makes diffusion the single most useful sequence for the non-enhancing CPA mass.

### Answer 5 — Otosclerosis and petrous apicitis [5+5]

Otosclerosis is a primary dysplasia of the otic capsule in which normal bone is replaced by foci of spongy, vascular otospongiotic bone that later sclerose. HRCT is the modality of choice. The fenestral form, the commoner type and the cause of conductive hearing loss, produces a lucent demineralised focus at the fissula ante fenestram, immediately anterior to the oval window, often with thickening of the stapes footplate. The retrofenestral (cochlear) form produces confluent pericochlear lucency, described as a halo or double-ring of demineralisation around the cochlea, and causes sensorineural or mixed hearing loss. In the active otospongiotic phase the abnormal pericochlear bone may enhance after gadolinium on MRI, which can corroborate active disease, but CT remains the primary diagnostic tool. Recognising the precise location (fissula ante fenestram versus pericochlear) is what separates the two patterns and predicts the type of hearing loss.

Petrous apicitis is infection of a pneumatised petrous apex, usually a complication of otomastoiditis. On HRCT there is opacification of the petrous apex air cells with demineralisation and breakdown of the bony trabeculae and marginal cortical erosion; MRI shows T2-hyperintense fluid, marginal and dural enhancement, and helps detect intracranial complications. The classic clinical correlate is Gradenigo syndrome, the triad of otorrhoea, deep retro-orbital or facial pain from involvement of the trigeminal nerve and Meckel cave at the petrous apex, and an abducens (CN VI) palsy producing diplopia, because CN VI runs through the adjacent Dorello canal. MRI is essential to assess the cavernous sinus, Meckel cave, dura and any associated abscess or venous sinus thrombosis, so CT and MRI are complementary: CT for the bone, MRI for soft-tissue and intracranial extent.
