## Practice questions

*Note: the following are practice questions written in DNB style. They are not cited from any real past paper.*

1. Describe the anatomy of the ostiomeatal unit and outline a pre-FESS CT checklist, including the anatomic variants a radiologist must report. [6+4]
2. Classify fungal sinusitis. Describe the CT and MRI features of allergic fungal rhinosinusitis and of acute invasive fungal sinusitis, and explain how you would distinguish them. [4+6]
3. A 14-year-old boy presents with recurrent epistaxis and nasal obstruction. Discuss the imaging approach, diagnostic imaging features, and the role of angiography/embolisation for the likely diagnosis. [4+4+2]
4. Enumerate the malignant tumours of the sinonasal tract. Describe the imaging features that help differentiate squamous cell carcinoma, esthesioneuroblastoma and lymphoma, and comment on staging. [3+5+2]
5. Discuss the imaging of mucocele and of CSF rhinorrhoea (CSF leak), including the modalities used and the key findings. [5+5]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1

The ostiomeatal unit (OMU) is the common drainage channel of the anterior sinus group. Its components are the maxillary sinus ostium, the ethmoid infundibulum, the uncinate process, the ethmoid bulla, the hiatus semilunaris and the middle meatus; the frontal sinus, anterior ethmoid cells and maxillary sinus all drain through it. The posterior ethmoid cells and sphenoid sinus drain separately via the sphenoethmoidal recess into the superior meatus, and the nasolacrimal duct into the inferior meatus. Obstruction of the OMU is the central mechanism of most inflammatory anterior sinus disease.

A pre-FESS CT is performed as thin-section multiplanar acquisition with coronal reformats on bone windows, and the report functions as a surgical roadmap. The radiologist must specifically flag anatomic variants that narrow drainage or endanger the surgeon. Concha bullosa (pneumatised middle turbinate) and Haller cell (infraorbital ethmoid cell) narrow the infundibulum/OMU. The Onodi cell (most posterior ethmoid cell pneumatising posterolaterally) is critical because it lies adjacent to the optic nerve and carotid canal, which are at risk during dissection. The Keros classification grades the depth of the olfactory fossa/lateral lamella of the cribriform plate (Types I to III); the deeper Type III carries the highest risk of iatrogenic CSF leak.

The checklist also covers: septal deviation and spurs, uncinate attachment, dehiscence of the lamina papyracea, course of the anterior ethmoidal artery, sphenoid sinus septations inserting on the carotid canal, and skull base asymmetry. Reporting these protects the orbit, optic nerve, carotid and anterior cranial fossa, and tailors the surgical approach.

### Answer 2

Fungal sinusitis is classified first by tissue invasion. Non-invasive forms are allergic fungal rhinosinusitis (AFRS) and the fungal ball (mycetoma). Invasive forms are acute fulminant (in immunocompromised or poorly controlled diabetic patients, e.g. mucormycosis), chronic invasive, and chronic granulomatous.

AFRS occurs in atopic, immunocompetent patients and is typically multi-sinus and bilateral. The hallmark CT finding is expansile sinus disease with central hyperdense allergic mucin (due to concentrated metals and inspissation) and smooth bony expansion/remodelling rather than destruction. On MRI the allergic mucin shows characteristic low to signal-void T2 with variable T1, a pitfall that can mimic a normally aerated sinus; peripheral mucosa enhances. Correlating CT hyperdensity with the MRI T2 void confirms the diagnosis.

Acute invasive fungal sinusitis is an emergency. CT shows soft-tissue disease with the key feature of bony erosion/destruction, infiltration and loss of the periantral fat planes, and extension into the orbit, pterygopalatine fossa or skull base. MRI shows loss of normal mucosal enhancement (the devitalised mucosa "black turbinate" sign) and maps orbital, cavernous sinus, vascular and intracranial spread.

The distinction rests on the host and on bone behaviour: AFRS is in an immunocompetent atopic patient with smooth bony expansion and no invasion, whereas invasive disease is in an immunocompromised host with frank bony destruction, fat-plane infiltration and extrasinus spread with loss of mucosal enhancement. Recognising the latter prompts urgent surgical debridement and antifungal therapy.

### Answer 3

The clinical picture (adolescent male, recurrent epistaxis, nasal obstruction) is classic for juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma (JNA), a benign but locally aggressive, highly vascular tumour.

Imaging approach: contrast-enhanced CT and MRI are complementary. CT (bone windows) demonstrates the bony changes and origin; MRI maps soft-tissue and intracranial extent; catheter angiography is reserved for confirmation of feeders and for preoperative embolisation rather than primary diagnosis. Biopsy is avoided because of the bleeding risk.

Diagnostic features: the mass is centred on the sphenopalatine foramen and pterygopalatine fossa (PPF). The characteristic signs are widening of the PPF and anterior bowing of the posterior wall of the maxillary sinus (the Holman-Miller or antral sign). It extends into the nasopharynx, nasal cavity and PPF, and may pass through the foramen rotundum/vidian canal and superior orbital fissure toward the cavernous sinus. The lesion enhances intensely; on MRI it shows multiple flow voids producing a "salt-and-pepper" appearance, and MRA/MRI define intracranial and cavernous extension.

Role of angiography/embolisation: catheter angiography demonstrates the vascular supply, most often from the internal maxillary artery (a branch of the external carotid), and may show contributions from the internal carotid in advanced disease. Preoperative embolisation (typically 24-48 hours before surgery) markedly reduces intraoperative haemorrhage and is the main role of the interventional radiologist in this disease.

### Answer 4

Malignant sinonasal tumours include squamous cell carcinoma (SCC, the most common), adenocarcinoma, adenoid cystic carcinoma, esthesioneuroblastoma (olfactory neuroblastoma), non-Hodgkin lymphoma (including NK/T-cell), melanoma, and metastases.

Differentiating features: SCC most often arises in the maxillary antrum and shows an aggressive soft-tissue mass with frank cortical bony destruction; CT best depicts the bone involvement and MRI the soft-tissue and perineural extent. Esthesioneuroblastoma arises in the olfactory recess at the cribriform plate and characteristically forms a dumbbell-shaped mass straddling the skull base; a suggestive sign is peripheral intracranial cysts at the tumour-brain interface, and MRI defines dural and brain invasion. Sinonasal lymphoma typically presents as bulky, homogeneous midline soft tissue with relatively preserved/remodelled bone compared with the destruction caused by SCC, and shows restricted diffusion; NK/T-cell lymphoma may cause midline destruction overlapping with granulomatous disease.

Staging: sinonasal SCC is staged with site-specific AJCC TNM, with separate criteria for the maxillary sinus and for the nasal cavity/ethmoid sinuses; exact T-category boundaries should be quoted only from a current AJCC table (verify exact values). Esthesioneuroblastoma is commonly described using the Kadish system (A confined to the nasal cavity, B extension to the paranasal sinuses, C beyond the sinuses including orbit/skull base/intracranial), often paired with the Hyams histological grade. MRI is essential for assessing orbital, dural, brain and perineural spread, which drives staging and surgical planning.

### Answer 5

Mucocele is a chronically obstructed, mucus-filled sinus that expands over time. It most often involves the frontal and ethmoid sinuses. On CT it appears as a completely opacified, airless, expanded sinus with smooth thinning and remodelling of the bony walls (expansion, not destruction); attenuation varies with the protein concentration of the contents. On MRI the signal depends on protein/water content, often becoming T1-bright as it inspissates, with a thin peripherally enhancing mucosal rim and a non-enhancing centre. Enhancing solid tissue or bony destruction should prompt reconsideration of tumour or a mucopyocele (secondary infection).

CSF rhinorrhoea (CSF leak) requires localisation of both the bony defect and the dural breach. High-resolution thin-section CT (bone algorithm, coronal) is used to identify the skull base defect, commonly at the cribriform plate, the lateral lamella (relevant to deep Keros configurations), the ethmoid roof, or the sphenoid. MRI with heavily T2-weighted/cisternographic sequences (MR cisternography) shows the CSF column tracking through the defect and any associated meningocele or encephalocele, and demonstrates herniated brain tissue without intrathecal contrast. CT cisternography (intrathecal contrast) can be used to confirm an active leak and pinpoint the site, particularly when MRI is equivocal or the leak is intermittent. The combination of bone-detail CT for the defect and fluid-sensitive MRI for the leak and any herniation provides the surgeon with the precise site for endoscopic repair.
