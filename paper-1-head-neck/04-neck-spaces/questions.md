## Practice questions

*Note: the following are practice questions written in DNB style. They are NOT cited past-paper questions.*

1. Describe the deep cervical fascial layers and the suprahyoid neck spaces they define. Explain how the direction of displacement of the parapharyngeal fat helps localise a deep neck mass. [6+4]

2. A 5-year-old presents with neck stiffness, drooling and fever. Discuss the imaging approach to a suspected retropharyngeal abscess, the relevant fascial anatomy, and the complication of descending mediastinitis. [4+3+3]

3. Outline the cervical nodal level classification (levels I-VII) with the key anatomical boundaries of each level. [10]

4. Enumerate the imaging criteria used to distinguish a malignant cervical node from a reactive node on CT, MRI and ultrasound, and explain the significance of extranodal extension. [7+3]

5. Compare the roles of CT, MRI and PET/CT in the evaluation of deep neck space pathology, with examples of when each is the preferred modality. [10]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 [6+4]

The deep cervical fascia has three layers. The superficial (investing) layer encircles the neck and splits to enclose sternocleidomastoid and trapezius, contributing to the masticator and parotid spaces. The middle (visceral or buccopharyngeal) layer surrounds the pharynx, larynx, trachea, oesophagus and thyroid and forms the anterior wall of the retropharyngeal space. The deep (prevertebral) layer surrounds the vertebral column and paraspinal muscles and splits into prevertebral and alar divisions; the alar division separates the retropharyngeal from the danger space.

These layers define the suprahyoid spaces arranged around a central fat-filled parapharyngeal space (PPS): the pharyngeal mucosal space lies medial to it, the masticator space anterolateral, the parotid space lateral, the carotid space posterolateral, and the retropharyngeal space posteromedial.

Because the PPS is fat (bright on T1, low on CT) and sits at the crossroads of these spaces, a mass arising in any neighbour pushes the fat in a characteristic direction, which identifies the space of origin. A mucosal-space lesion displaces the fat laterally; a masticator-space lesion (for example an odontogenic abscess or a V3 nerve sheath tumour) pushes it posteromedially; a parotid deep-lobe tumour pushes it medially; a carotid-space lesion such as a paraganglioma or schwannoma pushes it anteriorly; and a retropharyngeal lesion pushes it anterolaterally. Reading the direction of PPS fat displacement first, ideally on a T1 axial image, therefore allows rapid and reliable localisation before further characterisation.

### Answer 2 [4+3+3]

Imaging approach: a lateral soft-tissue neck radiograph may show widening of the prevertebral/retropharyngeal soft tissues, loss of lordosis or an air-fluid level, but contrast-enhanced CT of the neck is the primary investigation. CT distinguishes a true abscess — a rim-enhancing low-attenuation collection with mass effect on the airway, sometimes containing gas — from non-suppurative retropharyngeal oedema or effusion, which lacks a thick enhancing wall. CT also defines craniocaudal extent and assesses airway compromise. MRI with diffusion can confirm a suppurative collection (central restricted diffusion) when CT is equivocal.

Fascial anatomy: the retropharyngeal space lies between the middle (buccopharyngeal) layer anteriorly and the alar division of the deep layer posteriorly, extending from the skull base to roughly the upper mediastinum. In children it commonly contains lymph nodes that suppurate after pharyngitis, explaining the typical age.

Complication: infection may extend through or rupture the alar fascia into the danger space, which runs uninterrupted from the skull base to the diaphragm. This provides a direct conduit to the posterior mediastinum, producing descending necrotising mediastinitis — a life-threatening complication. CT of the neck and chest is used to track caudal spread, demonstrating mediastinal fluid, gas, widening and pleural or pericardial effusion, and guides urgent surgical drainage.

### Answer 3 [10]

The cervical nodes are classified into levels I-VII using imaging-based boundaries:

- Level I — submental (IA) lies between the anterior bellies of digastric above the hyoid; submandibular (IB) lies posterior to IA and anterior to the posterior margin of the submandibular gland.
- Level II — upper internal jugular nodes, from the skull base to the lower body of the hyoid; subdivided into IIA and IIB by the spinal accessory nerve.
- Level III — mid-jugular nodes, from the lower hyoid to the lower margin of the cricoid cartilage.
- Level IV — lower jugular nodes, from the lower cricoid to the clavicle.
- Level V — posterior triangle nodes, posterior to sternocleidomastoid; VA lies above and VB below the lower cricoid level.
- Level VI — anterior/visceral compartment nodes (pretracheal, prelaryngeal/Delphian, paratracheal), between the carotid sheaths from hyoid to suprasternal notch.
- Level VII — superior mediastinal nodes, below the suprasternal notch (the precise caudal limit varies by classification — verify exact value).

Retropharyngeal and parotid nodes lie outside the I-VII scheme and are described separately. Knowing these boundaries allows accurate reporting of nodal disease and correlates levels with likely primary sites, guiding the search for an unknown primary.

### Answer 4 [7+3]

Imaging criteria for malignancy in a cervical node assess size, shape, internal architecture, margin and vascularity. Size is judged by short-axis diameter against level-specific thresholds (level II nodes are often considered abnormal above about 11 mm and other levels above about 10 mm, with retropharyngeal nodes lower — verify exact value); size alone is insensitive and non-specific. Shape is more useful: a round node (long-to-short axis ratio approaching 1) is suspicious, whereas a reactive node is oval. Loss of the normal fatty echogenic hilum on ultrasound is a further sign.

The most reliable single feature is central necrosis — a low-attenuation centre on CT or T2-bright/non-enhancing centre on MRI — which strongly indicates metastasis. An irregular or indistinct margin is also suspicious. On Doppler ultrasound, a shift from central hilar vascularity to peripheral or chaotic flow supports malignancy. Clustering of three or more borderline nodes adds suspicion.

Extranodal extension is the breach of the nodal capsule with infiltration of perinodal fat, seen as loss of the smooth margin and stranding or invasion of adjacent structures. It is a major adverse prognostic factor, upstages disease in several sites, predicts recurrence and poorer survival, and often alters management toward more aggressive treatment, so it must be reported explicitly whenever present.

### Answer 5 [10]

CT, MRI and PET/CT are complementary in the deep neck.

Contrast-enhanced CT is the first-line modality for infection and acute presentations because it is fast, tolerant of unwell or uncooperative patients, and superb for gas, abscess wall enhancement, bone erosion and airway assessment. It is the modality of choice for suspected retropharyngeal abscess, Ludwig angina and descending mediastinitis (with chest CT), and gives good nodal information including necrosis and extranodal extension.

MRI is superior for soft-tissue characterisation and is preferred for staging neoplasms, for perineural tumour spread (for example along V3 through foramen ovale into the masticator space, or along the facial nerve in the parotid), for marrow infiltration of the mandible or skull base, and for intracranial extension. The bright T1 parapharyngeal fat makes MRI excellent for localising deep masses, and diffusion-weighted imaging helps confirm abscess and characterise tumours.

PET/CT contributes functional information: it is valuable for staging, for detecting an unknown primary in a patient presenting with malignant cervical nodes, for assessing treatment response and for surveillance. It does not replace anatomical imaging — small necrotic nodes and post-treatment inflammation are pitfalls, and limited spatial resolution hampers small-node assessment.

In practice: CT for infection and the airway; MRI for perineural spread, marrow and skull base and for tumour staging; PET/CT for unknown primary, distant staging and response assessment. Combined CT/MRI plus PET/CT often gives the most complete evaluation.
