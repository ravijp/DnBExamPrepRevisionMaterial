## Complete answers

### Q1. MRI protocol for the knee; primary and secondary signs of an ACL tear. [3+(4+3)]

**Knee MRI protocol [3].**
The patient is examined supine with the knee in slight (15–20°) external rotation to align the ACL with the sagittal plane, using a dedicated knee coil on a high-field (preferably 1.5T or 3T) system. The core sequence set comprises **fluid-sensitive fat-saturated proton-density / T2 sequences in all three orthogonal planes** to render fluid, oedema and tears conspicuous, together with **T1** for marrow and anatomical detail. The **sagittal and coronal** planes are optimal for the menisci and cruciate ligaments; the **axial** plane assesses the patellofemoral joint, retinacula, trochlea and the cruciate origins. Slices are kept thin (of the order of 3–4 mm; verify against local protocol) to avoid volume-averaging small meniscal tears. STIR may be added for robust marrow-oedema detection, and 3D gradient-echo for cartilage. MR arthrography is reserved for the post-operative meniscus or equivocal subtle tears.

**Primary (direct) signs of ACL tear [4].**
These relate to the ligament itself:
- Complete **fibre discontinuity** or **non-visualisation** of the ligament.
- Abnormal **increased intrasubstance T2 signal** (oedema/haemorrhage).
- Abnormal **flat or horizontal slope** of the ligament — the normal ACL parallels Blumensaat's line (the roof of the intercondylar notch).
- A **wavy, mass-like or balled-up stump**.

**Secondary (indirect) signs of ACL tear [3].**
These imply the tear from associated derangement and are well rewarded:
- **Anterior tibial translation** relative to the femur.
- **Deep lateral femoral notch sign**.
- The characteristic **"kissing" bone bruise** at the lateral femoral condyle (sulcus terminalis) and the posterolateral tibial plateau, reflecting the **pivot-shift** mechanism.
- **PCL buckling** (PCL line/angle sign).
- **Uncovering of the posterior horn of the lateral meniscus**.
- **Segond fracture** — lateral capsular avulsion, strongly associated with ACL rupture.

### Q2. Classify meniscal tears; MRI signs of a bucket-handle tear; role of MR arthrography. [4+4+2]

**Classification of meniscal tears [4].**
Tears are classified morphologically:
- **Horizontal (cleavage)** — degenerative, parallel to the tibial plateau, associated with parameniscal cysts.
- **Vertical** — subdivided into **longitudinal** (may progress to a bucket-handle tear) and **radial** (perpendicular to the free edge; gives the "truncated triangle" and "ghost meniscus" appearances).
- **Complex** — combination of planes, typically degenerate.
- **Displaced** — bucket-handle (displaced longitudinal fragment) and flap tears.
- **Root tears** — avulsion of the meniscal root from its tibial attachment, functionally equivalent to total meniscectomy.

The MRI criterion for a tear is **intrameniscal signal unequivocally contacting an articular surface**, or morphological distortion; intrasubstance signal not reaching a surface is degenerative (Grade 1–2), not a tear.

**MRI signs of a bucket-handle tear [4].**
A bucket-handle tear is a displaced longitudinal tear in which the inner fragment ("handle") flips into the intercondylar notch. Named signs:
- **Double-PCL sign** — the displaced fragment lies in the notch anteroinferior to the true PCL on sagittal images.
- **Absent bow-tie sign** — the normal meniscal-body "bow-tie" appears on fewer-than-expected sagittal slices because the body has migrated.
- **Flipped-meniscus / fragment-in-notch sign** — an abnormally enlarged or duplicated anterior horn, or a fragment seen within the notch.
- A small **truncated peripheral remnant** at the donor site.

**Role of MR arthrography [2].**
Direct MR arthrography (intra-articular dilute gadolinium) distends the joint and improves detection of subtle inner-margin and undersurface tears. Its highest-yield indication is the **post-operative meniscus**: after meniscectomy/repair, intrasubstance signal persists and standard criteria fail, so demonstration of **contrast imbibing into the substance of a tear** is the reliable sign of a re-tear. Indirect (intravenous) arthrography is an alternative when joint puncture is undesirable.

### Q3. Normal age-related distribution and conversion of bone marrow; distinguishing infiltration from normal marrow on MRI. [5+5]

**Normal distribution and conversion [5].**
At birth the skeleton is almost entirely **red (haematopoietic) marrow**. With age, an orderly **red-to-yellow (fatty) conversion** occurs:
- **Epiphyses and apophyses** convert first, becoming fatty soon after ossification.
- Conversion then sweeps **from the appendicular periphery toward the axial skeleton**.
- Within a long bone it proceeds from the **diaphysis outward toward the metaphyses**.
- Consequently the **proximal metaphyses of the femur and humerus retain red marrow longest** into adulthood, a normal finding not to be mistaken for disease.

On **T1**, fatty marrow is bright; normal **red marrow is brighter than skeletal muscle and the intervertebral disc** because even red marrow contains fat. **Reconversion** (yellow → red) occurs under haematopoietic stress — chronic anaemia, marrow-stimulating drugs, heavy smoking, obesity, endurance athletes/high altitude — proceeding in the reverse order.

**Distinguishing infiltration from normal marrow [5].**
- **T1 signal rule:** marrow that is **isointense to or darker than muscle/intervertebral disc on T1** indicates pathological infiltration/replacement (metastasis, myeloma, lymphoma, leukaemia). Normal marrow stays brighter than muscle.
- **Chemical-shift (in/opposed-phase) imaging:** normal marrow and red-marrow rests contain fat and **lose signal on opposed-phase**; replacing tumour contains little fat and shows **no significant drop**.
- **Distribution:** normal marrow is **symmetric and age-appropriate**; infiltration is **focal, patchy or diffuse and asymmetric**.
- **Fluid-sensitive sequences:** infiltrative disease is bright on STIR/fat-sat T2, though marrow oedema from benign causes overlaps — interpret with T1 and chemical shift.
- **Ancillary:** post-contrast enhancement, associated cortical destruction or soft-tissue mass favour tumour.

### Q4. Role of MRI in avascular necrosis including staging. [10]

**Why MRI [2].** MRI is the **most sensitive and specific** modality for avascular necrosis (AVN/osteonecrosis) and turns positive **before radiographs**, scintigraphy or CT. It detects the earliest marrow change, defines the extent of the necrotic segment (a prognostic factor), assesses the articular surface for collapse, and screens the contralateral joint and other sites.

**MRI findings [4].**
- The hallmark is the **double-line sign** on T2-weighted images: an inner **bright line** of hypervascular granulation/repair tissue and an outer **dark line** of reactive sclerosis demarcating the necrotic segment.
- The lesion is **serpentine/geographic** and subchondral, often **retaining central fatty (preserved) signal** early.
- A **crescent sign** (subchondral fluid/fracture cleft) heralds **subchondral collapse** and articular incongruity.
- **Marrow oedema** (low T1, high STIR) is a marker of progression and pain.
- Joint effusion is common.

**Staging [2].** Two named systems:
- **Ficat and Arlet** (stages 0–IV): conceptual progression from normal imaging → marrow signal change before radiographic change → sclerosis/cyst with crescent sign/subchondral fracture → articular collapse → secondary osteoarthritis.
- **ARCO** (Association Research Circulation Osseous): a more quantitative system incorporating lesion size, location and degree of collapse (verify exact grade boundaries).
State the conceptual sequence rather than memorising every numeric sub-grade.

**Causes to enumerate [2].** Corticosteroids, alcohol, sickle-cell disease, trauma (femoral neck fracture, hip dislocation), systemic lupus erythematosus, Gaucher disease, caisson (dysbaric) disease, radiation and pancreatitis. Note that the classic scintigraphic pattern is a photopenic centre with a surrounding ring of uptake ("doughnut"), now largely superseded by MRI.

### Q5. Short notes. [5+5]

**(a) Rotator cuff tear — MRI [5].**
MRI (and MR arthrography) is the imaging standard for the cuff, complementing ultrasound. The supraspinatus is most commonly involved. The spectrum is graded:
- **Tendinosis** — intermediate signal and thickening without a discrete fluid-signal defect.
- **Partial-thickness tear** — focal fluid-bright defect of the **articular- or bursal-surface** fibres, not spanning the full tendon.
- **Full-thickness tear** — fluid-signal gap traversing the whole tendon on fluid-sensitive sequences.

Describe and quantify: the **size of the gap and tendon retraction** (e.g. to the level of the glenoid/medial to it), the **tendon-edge quality**, fluid in the subacromial–subdeltoid bursa, and associated cuff-arthropathy changes. Chronicity is judged by **muscle atrophy and fatty infiltration**, graded with the **Goutallier** system on sagittal-oblique T1 images (higher fat grade predicts poorer surgical repair). MR arthrography improves detection of articular-surface partial tears (contrast tracks into the defect). Note associated findings: subacromial spur/os acromiale, biceps pathology and labral lesions.

**(b) Bone bruise patterns [5].**
A bone bruise (bone contusion / occult osseous injury) is **trabecular microfracture with marrow haemorrhage and oedema** without a discrete cortical fracture line. It is **low on T1** and **high on STIR / fat-sat PD/T2**, located subcortically. Its value is that the **distribution encodes the injury mechanism**, pointing to the associated soft-tissue tear:
- **Pivot-shift (ACL injury):** "**kissing**" contusions at the **lateral femoral condyle** (sulcus terminalis) and the **posterolateral tibial plateau** — the classic pattern.
- **Dashboard injury (PCL):** anterior proximal tibia.
- **Patellar dislocation:** medial patellar facet and anterolateral femoral condyle, with medial patellofemoral ligament injury.
- **Hyperextension / direct valgus:** "kissing" anterior femur and tibia, or medial-compartment bruising with MCL injury.

Bone bruises usually **resolve over weeks to months**; persistence, a subchondral fracture line or overlying chondral loss raises concern for **subchondral insufficiency fracture or early osteochondral injury**. Always interrogate the adjacent ligaments and cartilage when a contusion pattern is identified.
