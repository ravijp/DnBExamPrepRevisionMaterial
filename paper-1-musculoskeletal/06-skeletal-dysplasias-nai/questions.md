## Complete answers

### Q1. Radiological features of achondroplasia. [10]

**Framing.** Achondroplasia is the commonest non-lethal skeletal dysplasia, an autosomal-dominant disorder of endochondral ossification (FGFR3) producing **disproportionate rhizomelic dwarfism** with a normal trunk length. Diagnosis is essentially radiographic; the findings are best assessed on a skeletal survey region by region.

**Skull.** Large calvarium with **frontal bossing**, a relatively small skull base and a **narrow foramen magnum** (a major source of morbidity owing to cervicomedullary compression). The midface is hypoplastic.

**Spine.** The hallmark is **progressive narrowing of the interpedicular distance from L1 to L5** (the normal spine *widens* caudally). Pedicles are **short**, producing a congenitally narrow, stenotic canal; there is posterior vertebral body scalloping and a thoracolumbar kyphosis/gibbus in infancy. Lateral films show short pedicles and reduced AP canal diameter.

**Pelvis.** **"Champagne-glass" pelvic inlet**, squared (rounded) iliac wings, and **horizontal acetabular roofs** with small sacrosciatic notches.

**Long bones & hands.** **Rhizomelic** (proximal-segment) shortening — short, stout femora and humeri with metaphyseal flaring and a **"chevron"/inverted-V" distal femoral physis**. The hand shows the **"trident" configuration** (short tubular bones with a gap between the third and fourth digits).

**Cross-sectional imaging.** CT and especially MRI are reserved for complications: **foramen-magnum / craniocervical stenosis** with cord compression and **lumbar canal stenosis** with neurogenic claudication.

**Buzzwords to state:** interpedicular narrowing caudally, champagne-glass pelvis, trident hand, frontal bossing, rhizomelia.

### Q2. Radiological findings in NAI; high-specificity fractures; skeletal survey protocol. [5+3+2]

**(a) Radiological findings in NAI [5].** Non-accidental injury produces a constellation of skeletal and extraskeletal findings. Skeletal: **multiple fractures, often bilateral and at different stages of healing**, in unusual sites and in a **non-ambulant infant**, with an explanation that does not fit the injury or developmental stage. Fracture types include **classic metaphyseal lesions (CMLs)**, rib fractures (especially posterior), and long-bone shaft fractures. Extraskeletal correlates include subdural haematomas and abdominal visceral injury. Always assess the **healing stage**: soft-tissue swelling resolves early; periosteal new bone appears within roughly the first 1–2 weeks; soft then hard callus form over subsequent weeks; remodelling takes months — give *ranges, not single days* (verify exact values). Fractures at clearly different ages imply repeated injury. Crucially, **exclude osteogenesis imperfecta, rickets, Menkes disease and physiological periostitis** before alleging abuse.

**(b) Fractures of high specificity [3].** The classic high-specificity group: **classic metaphyseal lesions — the "corner" and "bucket-handle" metaphyseal fractures**; **posterior rib fractures** (near the costovertebral junction); and **scapular, sternal and spinous-process** fractures. (Moderate specificity: multiple fractures of different ages, complex/diastatic skull fractures; low specificity: clavicular shaft and isolated long-bone shaft fractures.)

**(c) Skeletal survey protocol [2].** A **dedicated, multi-view, individually coned survey — not a single whole-body "babygram"**, which has unacceptable geometry and misses subtle injury. It comprises AP and lateral skull; AP and lateral spine; AP chest with **oblique rib views**; AP abdomen/pelvis; and **AP views of each long bone separately**, plus AP hands and feet. A **repeat survey at ~2 weeks** increases yield as periosteal reaction and healing CMLs become visible. Correlate with CT head; scintigraphy is a rib-fracture adjunct.

### Q3. Causes of a diffusely sclerotic/dense skeleton in a child; imaging of any two. [4+6]

**(a) Enumeration [4].** Causes of a diffusely dense skeleton in a child:
1. **Osteopetrosis** ("marble bone disease").
2. **Pyknodysostosis**.
3. **Sclerosing diaphyseal dysplasias** (e.g. Camurati-Engelmann / progressive diaphyseal dysplasia; van Buchem disease).
4. **Renal osteodystrophy** (can cause sclerosis, including rugger-jersey spine).
5. **Fluorosis**.
6. Rarer: **hypervitaminosis D**, heavy-metal (lead) lines (focal), myelosclerosis/metastatic marrow infiltration (uncommon in a child).

**(b) Imaging of any two [6].**

**Osteopetrosis.** A failure of osteoclastic resorption produces diffusely dense, **brittle** bone. Radiographic hallmarks: **generalised increased bone density** with loss of corticomedullary differentiation; the **"bone-in-bone" appearance** (a miniature bone within the bone); **"sandwich"/"rugger-jersey" vertebra** (dense superior and inferior endplate bands with a lucent centre); and **Erlenmeyer-flask** metaphyseal undermodelling. Complications include pathological transverse fractures, marrow failure (extramedullary haematopoiesis), and **cranial-nerve palsies from foraminal narrowing** at the skull base. CT/MRI assess foraminal stenosis and marrow.

**Pyknodysostosis.** An osteoclast (cathepsin K) defect giving dense but brittle bone. Distinguishing features from osteopetrosis: **acro-osteolysis of the distal phalanges (terminal tuft resorption)**, **wormian bones with delayed sutural closure and an obtuse (open) mandibular angle**, persistent fontanelles, and hypoplastic facial bones — combined with generalised osteosclerosis. Fractures are common.

*(Either two of the above earn full marks; osteopetrosis is the safest first choice.)*

### Q4. Short notes. [5+5]

**(a) Osteogenesis imperfecta [5].** A heritable disorder of **type-I collagen** producing **bone fragility**. Clinically: blue sclerae, dentinogenesis imperfecta, hearing loss; severity ranges from lethal (type II) to mild (type I). Radiological features: **multiple fractures of differing ages** with exuberant callus; **gracile, thin, osteopenic long bones** with bowing deformity; **wormian bones** in the skull with delayed cranial ossification; and **vertebral compression / biconcave ("codfish") vertebrae** with kyphoscoliosis. Lethal type II shows **crumpled/beaded ribs** and a poorly ossified, fracture-laden skeleton on the babygram. Severe forms show "popcorn" metaphyseal/epiphyseal calcifications. It is the principal differential of NAI — but bones are *osteopenic/gracile*, wormian bones are present, and CMLs/posterior rib fractures are uncommon.

**(b) Wormian bones — causes [5].** **Wormian bones** are accessory intrasutural ossicles within the cranial sutures (most often lambdoid); to be significant they should be **numerous, large, and arranged in a mosaic** (a few small ones can be a normal variant). Causes are remembered by the mnemonic **"PORKCHOPS"**:
- **P** — Pyknodysostosis / Pachydermoperiostosis
- **O** — Osteogenesis imperfecta
- **R** — Rickets (in healing phase)
- **K** — Kinky-hair (Menkes) disease
- **C** — Cleidocranial dysplasia
- **H** — Hypothyroidism / Hypophosphatasia
- **O** — Otopalatodigital syndrome
- **P** — Primary (idiopathic / normal variant)
- **S** — Syndrome of Down (trisomy 21)

The two you must always name in an exam are **osteogenesis imperfecta** and **cleidocranial dysplasia**.

### Q5. Imaging features of mucopolysaccharidoses (dysostosis multiplex). [10]

**Framing.** The mucopolysaccharidoses (e.g. Hurler, Hunter, Morquio) are lysosomal storage disorders in which glycosaminoglycan accumulation produces a stereotyped, generalised constellation of skeletal changes termed **dysostosis multiplex**. The imaging is radiographic and best described region by region.

**Skull.** A thick **calvarium** and a characteristic **"J-shaped" (omega) sella turcica** with a long, shallow floor.

**Spine.** The signature is the **anteroinferiorly beaked / hook-shaped vertebral body** (in Morquio the beak is more central/middle, in Hurler more anteroinferior — *verify*), associated with a **thoracolumbar gibbus/kyphosis** and **platyspondyly** in Morquio. There may be **atlantoaxial instability** (odontoid hypoplasia, especially Morquio) — best assessed with MRI for cord compression.

**Thorax.** **"Oar-shaped" / "canoe-paddle" ribs** — narrow posteriorly at the vertebral end and broad/widened anteriorly.

**Pelvis.** **Flared iliac wings** with inferior tapering, shallow dysplastic acetabulae, and **coxa valga** with femoral-head dysplasia/avascular change.

**Hands.** **Proximally pointed ("V-shaped") metacarpals** (bullet-shaped short tubular bones) with widened diaphyses and irregular carpal ossification; the appearances give the "trident-like" stubby hand.

**Long bones.** Widened, under-tubulated diaphyses with metaphyseal irregularity.

**Cross-sectional role.** **MRI** is essential for the neurological burden — **craniocervical-junction/atlantoaxial cord compression, communicating hydrocephalus, perivascular cribriform spaces, and cord myelopathy.**

**Buzzwords to state:** J-shaped sella, oar/paddle ribs, anteroinferiorly beaked vertebra with gibbus, V-shaped metacarpals, flared iliac wings.
