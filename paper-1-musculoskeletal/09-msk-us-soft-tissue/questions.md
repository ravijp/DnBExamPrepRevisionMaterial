## Complete answers

### Q1. Role of ultrasound in the evaluation of the musculoskeletal system [10]

**Introduction.** Ultrasound (US) is the workhorse for superficial musculoskeletal structures. Its strengths are high spatial resolution, real-time dynamic assessment, contralateral comparison, absence of ionising radiation, low cost, and the ability to guide intervention. Its principal limitations are operator dependence, poor penetration of deep or large lesions, and inability to image through bone or gas.

**Applications (enumerate by structure).**

1. **Tendons and ligaments.** The normal tendon shows a **fibrillar** echotexture in long axis. Tendinosis appears as **hypoechoic fusiform thickening with neovascularity on power Doppler**; tears show a hypo/anechoic gap with retraction and dynamic gapping. **Anisotropy** — artefactual hypoechogenicity when the probe is off-perpendicular — is the classic pitfall mimicking a tear; tilt the probe to confirm.
2. **Muscle.** Grades strain and tear (oedema/feathery hyperechogenicity through to complete discontinuity with a retracted stump), and detects haematoma and muscle hernia (best on dynamic/standing imaging).
3. **Joint and bursa.** Distinguishes compressible anechoic **effusion** from non-compressible hypoechoic **synovial hypertrophy**; **power Doppler signal within synovium denotes active inflammation**, a key disease-activity marker in inflammatory arthritis. Also assesses bursitis.
4. **Soft-tissue mass.** Separates **cystic** (anechoic, through-transmission, no internal Doppler — ganglion, Baker cyst) from **solid** (internal echoes, vascularity) lesions; characterises superficial masses and triages for MRI.
5. **Nerve.** Demonstrates the fascicular "honeycomb" pattern, entrapment, and nerve-sheath tumours.
6. **Foreign body.** Detects echogenic foreign bodies (including radiolucent wood missed on radiographs), with shadowing or comet-tail artefact and surrounding granulation.
7. **Interventional guidance.** Real-time guidance for aspiration, injection and biopsy.
8. **Paediatric.** DDH assessment (Graf method), septic hip effusion, and imaging of non-ossified cartilage.

**Conclusion.** US excels at superficial triage, dynamic problem-solving and guidance, but MRI remains the staging tool for deep or indeterminate lesions.

### Q2. MRI characterisation of a soft-tissue mass; features of malignancy; principles of biopsy [4+4+2]

**(a) MRI features that characterise a soft-tissue mass [4].**
A logical protocol pairs **T1** (anatomy, fat), a **fluid-sensitive fat-suppressed sequence** (T2 FS/STIR for conspicuity and oedema), **gradient-echo** (haemosiderin/calcium blooming) and **post-gadolinium** images (enhancement, necrosis). Tissue-specific signatures allow a confident diagnosis in the minority of lesions:
- **Fat** — high T1 and T2, suppresses on fat-sat → lipoma.
- **Fluid** — low T1, very high T2, no solid enhancement → cyst/ganglion.
- **Haemosiderin** — low T1/T2 with GRE **blooming** → PVNS/GCT tendon sheath.
- **Serpentine flow voids + fat** → haemangioma/vascular malformation.
- **Very high T2, low T1, faint enhancement** → myxoid tumour.

**(b) Imaging features suggesting malignancy [4].**
No single feature is diagnostic; use the constellation:
- Size **> 5 cm**.
- Location **deep to the deep fascia**.
- **Heterogeneous** signal with **necrosis** and ill-defined margins.
- **Rapid growth**, peritumoral oedema, neurovascular or bone involvement.
Crucially, **MRI cannot reliably exclude sarcoma** — benign lesions may be large/deep and some sarcomas are small, so an indeterminate mass is treated as potentially malignant.

**(c) Principles of biopsy [2].**
Biopsy after — not before — full MRI staging. The tract must be **placed through the future surgical resection corridor** so it can be excised en bloc, planned in discussion with the operating (ideally sarcoma-unit) surgeon. Target the enhancing/solid component (avoid necrosis), use image guidance, and avoid contaminating uninvolved compartments or neurovascular structures. A poorly-sited tract can compromise limb salvage.

### Q3. Imaging of peripheral nerve-sheath tumours; schwannoma vs neurofibroma [6+4]

**(a) Imaging features [6].**
**Classification.** Benign — schwannoma (neurilemmoma) and neurofibroma (localised/diffuse/**plexiform**); malignant — **MPNST**, often arising in NF1. Associations: NF1 (neurofibromas, plexiform, MPNST risk) and NF2/schwannomatosis (multiple schwannomas).

**Ultrasound.** A **hypoechoic fusiform mass continuous with a nerve**, often with posterior acoustic enhancement (cyst mimic) and the parent nerve entering/exiting at the poles.

**MRI** is the modality of choice. Shared benign feature: a **fusiform mass oriented along the long axis of a nerve** with the nerve entering and exiting at its poles. Classic signs:
- **Split-fat sign** — thin rim of fat at the poles (intermuscular nerve location).
- **Target sign** — central low and peripheral high T2 signal.
- **Fascicular sign** — multiple small ring-like fascicles within the mass.
- **Entering/exiting nerve** traced into and out of the lesion.

**Suspect MPNST** with rapid growth, large size (> ~5 cm), ill-defined margins, marked heterogeneity/necrosis, peritumoral oedema, loss of the target sign and new pain — particularly in NF1. **FDG-PET** detects malignant transformation as a focally hypermetabolic nodule within a known plexiform neurofibroma.

**(b) Schwannoma vs neurofibroma [4].**

| Feature | Schwannoma | Neurofibroma |
|---|---|---|
| Relation to nerve | **Eccentric** — nerve splayed over surface | **Central** — nerve runs through it |
| Capsule | Encapsulated | Non-encapsulated |
| Resectability | Can be shelled out, nerve preserved | Resection often sacrifices nerve |
| Target sign | Less common | More common |
| Cystic/haemorrhagic change | More common (ancient schwannoma) | Less common |
| Plexiform form | No | **Yes — pathognomonic of NF1** |
| Syndrome | NF2 / schwannomatosis | NF1 |

### Q4. Short notes [5+5]

**(a) PVNS / GCT of tendon sheath [5].**
These are related benign proliferative synovial lesions characterised by **haemosiderin deposition**. **Giant cell tumour of tendon sheath** is the **localised** form (a solid juxta-articular nodule, classically of the fingers); **pigmented villonodular synovitis (PVNS)** is the **diffuse intra-articular** form, commonest in the knee.

- **Radiograph:** soft-tissue mass; in PVNS, pressure erosions on both sides of a joint with **preserved joint space and no calcification**.
- **Ultrasound:** solid, hypoechoic, vascular synovial nodule/thickening, ± effusion.
- **MRI (diagnostic):** **low signal on T1 and T2** with characteristic **"blooming" on gradient-echo** sequences due to the paramagnetic effect of haemosiderin; variable enhancement.

Treatment is synovectomy; the diffuse form has a notable recurrence rate. The blooming low-signal synovial process is the buzzword.

**(b) Lipomatous tumours — imaging [5].**
**Simple lipoma** is the commonest soft-tissue tumour. On all modalities it **follows subcutaneous fat**: lucent on radiographs, fat-attenuation on CT, **high T1 and T2 signal that suppresses on fat-saturated sequences** on MRI, with at most a few thin septa and no solid enhancing nodularity.

**Worrying features** raising **atypical lipomatous tumour / well-differentiated liposarcoma**: large size, **thick (> 2 mm) or nodular septa**, prominent **non-fatty enhancing soft-tissue components**, and a lower proportion of fat. Higher-grade liposarcomas may contain little visible fat and appear as non-specific heterogeneous masses. Variants include angiolipoma and the fat-containing **hibernoma**. Any fatty mass with thick septa, nodules or enhancing soft tissue requires MRI characterisation and biopsy rather than reassurance.

### Q5. Role of imaging in a suspected soft-tissue sarcoma including staging and follow-up [10]

**Introduction.** The radiologist's role is to detect, characterise, **stage**, guide a correctly-placed biopsy, and monitor treatment — never to confidently exclude malignancy, since **imaging cannot reliably distinguish benign from malignant** soft-tissue masses.

**Detection and characterisation.**
- **Radiograph:** first-line; assesses calcification/ossification (amorphous calcification favours synovial sarcoma) and bone involvement, though a normal film never excludes a tumour.
- **Ultrasound:** confirms a solid vascular mass, triages superficial lesions and guides biopsy.
- **MRI** is the primary local modality: defines **size, depth relative to the deep fascia, compartment of origin, relationship to the neurovascular bundle, bone/joint involvement and an enhancing target for biopsy**. Features favouring malignancy are size > 5 cm, deep location, heterogeneity/necrosis, ill-defined margins and rapid growth. Be alert to **synovial sarcoma**, a malignant mimic of a benign cyst (juxta-articular, may calcify, fluid–fluid levels, triple sign).

**Staging.**
- **Local staging on MRI** maps to the **Enneking** musculoskeletal system and **AJCC TNM**, incorporating **histologic grade** (quote exact stage/grade cut-offs only from the chart to hand — verify exact value).
- **Distant staging:** **CT chest** for pulmonary metastases (the dominant metastatic site).
- **FDG-PET/PET-CT** correlates with grade and aids whole-body staging.

**Biopsy.** Image-guided biopsy after staging, with the tract **placed through the planned resection corridor**, agreed with the sarcoma-unit surgeon.

**Follow-up.** Post-treatment MRI (ideally with a baseline) monitors the surgical bed for **local recurrence**, distinguishing recurrence from post-operative/radiotherapy change with the help of enhancement and **FDG-PET**; **surveillance CT chest** screens for metastatic disease. In NF1, surveillance ± PET targets MPNST transformation.
