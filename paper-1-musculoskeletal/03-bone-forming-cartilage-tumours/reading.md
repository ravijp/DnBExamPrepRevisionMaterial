# Bone-forming & Cartilage Tumours

> Osteosarcoma and the cartilage tumours are recurrent named-entity questions.

## Bone-forming tumours

**Osteoid osteoma** — young, painful (night pain, relieved by NSAIDs), **small radiolucent nidus (<1.5 cm) with surrounding reactive sclerosis**; CT best to show nidus; treated by RFA.

**Osteoblastoma** — like a giant osteoid osteoma (>2 cm), posterior elements of spine.

**Osteosarcoma**
- Conventional (intramedullary, high-grade): metaphysis of long bones (distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus), young (10-25 yr). **Osteoid (cloud-like) matrix, aggressive periosteal reaction (Codman/sunburst), cortical destruction, soft-tissue mass.**
- **Subtypes (enumerate):** **parosteal** (low-grade, surface, posterior distal femur, "stuck-on"), **periosteal** (intermediate, surface, diaphyseal), **telangiectatic** (lytic, expansile, **fluid-fluid levels** — mimics ABC), high-grade surface, secondary (Paget, post-radiation).
- **MRI for staging:** marrow extent, **skip lesions**, joint/physis involvement, neurovascular bundle, soft-tissue mass. CT chest for pulmonary metastases.

## Cartilage tumours

**Enchondroma** — benign, central, medullary, **chondroid matrix (rings-and-arcs)**, hand/foot short tubular bones; multiple = **Ollier disease / Maffucci** (+soft-tissue haemangiomas) with malignant-transformation risk.

**Osteochondroma (exostosis)** — commonest benign bone tumour; **continuity of cortex AND medulla with parent bone is the diagnostic feature**; sessile/pedunculated, points away from joint. Malignant transformation to chondrosarcoma if growth after skeletal maturity or **cartilage cap >1.5-2 cm** (MRI). Multiple = hereditary multiple exostoses.

**Chondroblastoma** — epiphysis/apophysis of skeletally immature; lytic, sclerotic rim, chondroid matrix, ± secondary ABC.

**Chondromyxoid fibroma** — eccentric metaphyseal, lytic, scalloped sclerotic margin.

**Chondrosarcoma** — older adults, axial/proximal (pelvis, proximal femur/humerus); **deep endosteal scalloping (>2/3 cortical thickness), cortical thickening, rings-and-arcs matrix**; MRI for soft-tissue/marrow extent. Distinguishing enchondroma from low-grade chondrosarcoma is a classic problem (pain, size >5 cm, deep scalloping, cortical breach favour malignancy).

## Pearls / buzzwords
- "Fluid-fluid levels" → ABC or telangiectatic osteosarcoma (and others).
- "Rings and arcs / popcorn" → chondroid matrix.
- Osteochondroma cap thickness on MRI is the key to malignant transformation.

## What to draw
Osteosarcoma with Codman triangle + sunburst + soft-tissue mass; osteochondroma showing cortico-medullary continuity.
