## Complete answers

### Q1. Enumerate bone-forming tumours. Discuss the imaging features and the role of MRI in staging of osteosarcoma. [2+5+3]

**Enumeration of bone-forming (osteogenic) tumours [2]**
These produce osteoid/bone matrix (cloud-like, fluffy, ivory mineralisation).
- *Benign:* osteoma, osteoid osteoma, osteoblastoma.
- *Intermediate:* aggressive osteoblastoma.
- *Malignant:* osteosarcoma — conventional (intramedullary, high-grade; osteoblastic/chondroblastic/fibroblastic), telangiectatic, small-cell, surface variants (parosteal, periosteal, high-grade surface), and secondary (Paget, post-radiation).

**Imaging features of osteosarcoma [5]**
Conventional high-grade osteosarcoma arises in the **metaphysis of long bones** (distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus), peak 10–25 years.
- **Radiograph (anchor):** metaphyseal lesion with **aggressive (Lodwick) features** — osteoid (cloud-like/fluffy) matrix, mixed lytic-sclerotic destruction, **permeative/moth-eaten margins with a wide zone of transition**, aggressive periosteal reaction (**Codman triangle**, **sunburst/sunray spiculation**) and a **soft-tissue mass**.
- **CT:** best for subtle matrix mineralisation and cortical breach; **CT chest is mandatory** for pulmonary metastases (commonest site).
- **US:** limited; confirms a soft-tissue mass and guides biopsy along a surgeon-planned tract.
- **Nuclear:** bone scan shows intense uptake and screens the skeleton; FDG-PET for staging/chemo-response where available.

**Role of MRI in staging [3]**
MRI is the **local staging workhorse** and must image the **whole bone including the joint above and below**:
- **Intramedullary extent** of tumour (T1 marrow replacement).
- **Skip metastases** — separate intramedullary foci.
- **Physeal and joint involvement**, and relationship of the soft-tissue mass to the **neurovascular bundle** (resectability).
- **Assessment of neoadjuvant chemotherapy response** (necrosis, reducing mass/oedema).

### Q2. Discuss the imaging features that help differentiate an enchondroma from a low-grade chondrosarcoma. [10]

This is a classic discrimination problem. Both are lobulated chondroid lesions showing **rings-and-arcs / stippled matrix** and **markedly T2-hyperintense lobules with peripheral/septal enhancement** on MRI. The distinction rests on a **constellation of features**, not a single sign, and on the clinical context.

**Clinical / location**
Enchondroma favours the **short tubular bones of the hand** (and proximal humerus/femur), is usually **incidental and painless**. An aggressive-looking cartilage lesion in the **axial skeleton, pelvis or proximal long bones**, or one causing **unexplained persistent/mechanical pain**, should be regarded as chondrosarcoma until proven otherwise.

**Discriminating imaging features**

| Feature | Enchondroma (benign) | Low-grade chondrosarcoma |
|---|---|---|
| Pain (unexplained, persistent) | Usually absent | Often present |
| Size | Smaller | Larger (often >5 cm) *(verify exact value)* |
| Endosteal scalloping | Shallow (<2/3 cortical thickness) | **Deep (>2/3 cortical thickness)**, extensive |
| Cortex | Intact | Thickening / expansion / breach |
| Periosteal reaction | Absent | May be present |
| Soft-tissue mass | Absent | May be present |
| Peritumoral marrow oedema (MRI) | Absent | May be present |
| Common site | Hand short tubular bones | Axial / proximal long bones |

**Practical rule:** depth and extent of **endosteal scalloping (>2/3 cortex)**, cortical thickening/destruction, a soft-tissue mass, surrounding oedema, larger size and an aggressive site/pain together favour chondrosarcoma. CT best shows cortical involvement and matrix; MRI defines marrow and soft-tissue extent. When several features coexist, favour malignancy.

### Q3. Describe the imaging features of osteochondroma and the signs of malignant transformation. [6+4]

**Imaging features of osteochondroma [6]**
Osteochondroma (osteocartilaginous exostosis) is the **commonest benign bone tumour** — a metaphyseal bony outgrowth capped by cartilage, **pointing away from the adjacent joint**, sessile or pedunculated.
- **Radiograph (diagnostic):** the defining feature is **continuity of BOTH the cortex AND the medullary cavity of the lesion with the parent bone** (corticomedullary continuity). This clinches the diagnosis.
- **CT:** confirms corticomedullary continuity in complex/axial sites and demonstrates matrix mineralisation.
- **MRI:** confirms continuity and is the modality to **measure the cartilage cap** and assess any soft-tissue component.
- Multiple lesions = **hereditary multiple exostoses** (with associated modelling deformity and increased malignant risk).

**Signs of malignant transformation (to chondrosarcoma) [4]**
Suspect malignant transformation (peripheral chondrosarcoma) when there is:
- **A thick cartilage cap** — the key sign on MRI/CT (a cap commonly cited as >1.5–2 cm in adults; *verify exact value*).
- **Continued growth after skeletal maturity** or a sudden increase in size.
- **New onset of pain** not attributable to mechanical/bursal causes or fracture.
- A **soft-tissue mass**, irregular/indistinct cap surface, scattered new lucencies/destruction within a previously mature lesion.

### Q4. Short notes: (a) Osteoid osteoma [5] (b) Telangiectatic osteosarcoma [5]

**(a) Osteoid osteoma [5]**
A small benign osteoblastic lesion of the young (second–third decade), classically causing **night pain dramatically relieved by NSAIDs/aspirin** (prostaglandin-mediated).
- **XR:** a **small radiolucent nidus (usually <1.5 cm)** surrounded by dense **fusiform reactive cortical sclerosis**, typically diaphyseal/cortical (femur, tibia). An intra-articular nidus may show only effusion/synovitis with little sclerosis and is easily missed.
- **CT (investigation of choice):** depicts the nidus precisely (often a central calcified dot) and localises it for **radiofrequency ablation (RFA)**, the standard treatment.
- **MRI:** can mislead — exuberant marrow/soft-tissue oedema may overshadow the nidus; the nidus enhances avidly. Correlate with CT.
- **Bone scan:** intense focal uptake with the classic **"double-density" sign** (hotter central nidus within reactive uptake).

**(b) Telangiectatic osteosarcoma [5]**
An aggressive high-grade osteosarcoma variant that is predominantly haemorrhagic/cystic.
- **XR:** a **purely lytic, expansile, destructive metaphyseal lesion** with little or no matrix mineralisation and **aggressive margins** (wide zone of transition, cortical breach, periosteal reaction).
- **CT / MRI:** **fluid-fluid levels** from haemorrhagic cystic spaces — an **aneurysmal bone cyst (ABC) mimic**.
- **Distinguishing from ABC:** the malignancy shows **thick/nodular enhancing septa and solid soft-tissue components**, aggressive margins and a soft-tissue mass; fluid-fluid levels alone are **not** benign.
- Same young age group as conventional osteosarcoma; staged and treated similarly (MRI whole bone, mandatory CT chest).

### Q5. Discuss the imaging features of Ewing sarcoma and its differential diagnosis. [6+4]

**Imaging features of Ewing sarcoma [6]**
Ewing sarcoma is the prototypic **small round blue cell** tumour of bone (Ewing/PNET family), of children/adolescents (peak ~5–15 years), classically arising in the **diaphysis of long bones and the flat bones** (pelvis, scapula, ribs). It often presents with pain, **fever and raised inflammatory markers** — an **osteomyelitis mimic**.
- **XR:** an ill-defined **permeative/moth-eaten lytic lesion** with **lamellated "onion-skin" periosteal reaction** (sometimes Codman triangle/sunburst); the hallmark is a **disproportionately large soft-tissue mass** relative to the degree of bone destruction.
- **CT:** confirms bone destruction and the soft-tissue component; **CT chest** for pulmonary metastases.
- **MRI:** the staging modality — best demonstrates the **large soft-tissue mass**, intramedullary/marrow extent and compartmental relations (neurovascular bundle, joint).
- **Nuclear/PET:** bone scan/FDG-PET screens for skeletal and marrow metastases (lung, bone, marrow are common sites).

**Differential diagnosis [4]**
- **Osteomyelitis** — fever and raised markers overlap; look for sequestrum/abscess and clinical course.
- **Langerhans cell histiocytosis** — can be permeative with periosteal reaction in the same age group.
- **Lymphoma of bone** — permeative destruction with a soft-tissue mass, usually older patients.
- **Metastatic neuroblastoma** — in the very young child (permeative metastatic disease).
- (Also **conventional osteosarcoma** — but expect osteoid matrix and a metaphyseal site rather than diaphyseal permeative lysis.)
