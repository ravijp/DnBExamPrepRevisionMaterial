## Complete answers

### Q1. Thoracolumbar spine fracture classification; imaging findings and instability of a burst fracture. [4+(3+3)]

**(a) Classification [4]**

Thoracolumbar injuries are framed using two complementary systems.

**Denis three-column concept** divides the vertebra into:
- **Anterior column** — anterior longitudinal ligament + anterior two-thirds of the body and annulus.
- **Middle column** — posterior third of the body, posterior annulus and posterior longitudinal ligament. This is the *critical* column for stability.
- **Posterior column** — the bony neural arch plus the posterior ligamentous complex (PLC: supraspinous, interspinous, ligamentum flavum, facet capsules).

Disruption of **≥2 columns = mechanically unstable**.

**Morphological types:**
| Type | Columns | Mechanism | Hallmark |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compression (wedge) | Anterior only | Flexion/axial | Anterior height loss, intact middle column |
| Burst | Anterior + middle | Axial load | Retropulsion, widened interpedicular distance |
| Chance (flexion-distraction) | All three (horizontal) | Lap-belt flexion | Horizontal split; bowel/mesenteric injury |
| Fracture-dislocation | All three | Shear/rotation | Translation, facet disruption — grossly unstable |

Modern management-oriented schemes (**TLICS / AO Spine**) score morphology + PLC integrity + neurological status, with the PLC being the pivotal element directing operative vs conservative care.

**(b) Imaging findings of a burst fracture [3]**

A burst fracture is axial-load failure of the anterior **and** middle columns. On **radiographs**: loss of anterior and posterior vertebral body height, **widened interpedicular distance** on the AP view, and a fragment displaced posteriorly on the lateral. **CT** is the work-horse — sagittal/axial reformats demonstrate the comminuted body, the **retropulsed posterosuperior fragment** projecting into the canal, and allow quantification of canal compromise. **MRI** (STIR) shows marrow oedema, any cord contusion/compression and, critically, the state of the **PLC**.

**(c) Criteria for instability [3]**

Instability is suggested by: middle-column disruption with **retropulsion and canal compromise**; **posterior ligamentous complex injury** (high STIR signal, interspinous widening); kyphotic angulation and significant vertebral height loss (greater loss = greater instability); **neurological deficit**; and any associated posterior-element fracture or translation. Two- or three-column involvement, PLC disruption or neurological compromise tip the injury toward operative stabilisation.

### Q2. Unstable pelvic injury after RTA — Young-Burgess classification and role of imaging including IR in pelvic haemorrhage. [5+3+2]

**(a) Young-Burgess classification [5]**

A mechanistic scheme based on the direction of injuring force:

- **Anteroposterior compression (APC I–III)** — the "open-book." Progressive symphyseal diastasis with sequential failure of the sacrotuberous/sacrospinous and posterior sacroiliac ligaments. **APC-III** = complete posterior disruption, a fully unstable hemipelvis.
- **Lateral compression (LC I–III)** — sacral impaction and horizontally oriented pubic rami fractures. **LC-III** is the "windswept" rollover (ipsilateral LC with contralateral APC pattern).
- **Vertical shear (VS)** — Malgaigne-type vertical displacement of the hemipelvis (fall from height).
- **Combined mechanical (CM).**

For stability, this maps onto the **Tile** scheme: A = stable, B = rotationally unstable, C = rotationally and vertically unstable. **APC-III and VS** patterns carry the highest haemorrhage risk because the volume-expanding open-book loses its tamponade effect.

**(b) Role of imaging [3]**

**Radiographs** (AP + inlet/outlet) screen the ring and show symphyseal diastasis, SI widening and vertical displacement. **CT with multiplanar and 3D reconstructions** is the reference standard — it defines the ring/column pattern, posterior (sacral/SI) injury and acetabular involvement, and is fast enough for the unstable patient. **CT angiography** localises arterial injury, demonstrating a **contrast "blush"/active extravasation** that directs further management. **FAST/E-FAST** ultrasound is an adjunct to exclude intraperitoneal haemorrhage but does not assess the ring.

**(c) Role of interventional radiology [2]**

In ongoing pelvic arterial haemorrhage, **catheter angiography with transcatheter embolisation** is both diagnostic and therapeutic — identifying the bleeding vessel (commonly internal iliac branches) and controlling it with coils/gelfoam, either selectively or as non-selective internal iliac embolisation in the unstable patient. It complements mechanical stabilisation (binder/external fixation) and venous/bony bleeding control by pelvic packing.

### Q3. Salter-Harris classification with diagrams; prognostic significance. [5+5]

**(a) Classification with diagrams [5]**

The Salter-Harris system describes injuries involving the growth plate (physis). Mnemonic **SALTR**:

| Type | SALTR | Plane of injury |
|---|---|---|
| I | **S**lipped | Through the physis only |
| II | **A**bove | Physis + metaphysis (Thurston-Holland fragment) — **commonest** |
| III | **L**ower | Physis + epiphysis (intra-articular) |
| IV | **T**hrough | Metaphysis + physis + epiphysis (intra-articular) |
| V | **R**ammed | Crush/compression of the physis |

*What to draw:* five line diagrams of a long-bone end (epiphysis–physis–metaphysis), showing the fracture plane: type I purely along the physis; II exiting through the metaphysis (corner fragment); III dropping through the epiphysis into the joint; IV crossing all three vertically; V as axial compression of the physis with no visible line.

**(b) Prognostic significance [5]**

- **Type I** — generally good; periosteum usually intact, low growth-arrest risk.
- **Type II** — commonest; prognosis good, growth arrest uncommon.
- **Type III** — intra-articular, requires **anatomical reduction** to restore the joint and reduce growth-disturbance risk.
- **Type IV** — intra-articular and crosses the physis; needs anatomical reduction; significant risk of a **bony bridge (growth-arrest bar)**, angular deformity and limb-length discrepancy.
- **Type V** — **worst prognosis**; physeal crush causes growth arrest and is often only recognised retrospectively when deformity develops.

In general, the higher the type the greater the risk to the joint surface and to subsequent growth; MRI best depicts cartilaginous physeal involvement and early bony-bridge formation.

### Q4. Imaging modalities for suspected scaphoid fracture; superiority of MRI; complications of a missed fracture. [3+3+4]

**(a) Imaging modalities [3]**

- **Radiographs** — first line: dedicated **scaphoid series** (PA, lateral, oblique and a PA in **ulnar deviation** to elongate the scaphoid). A normal initial film does not exclude fracture.
- **Repeat radiographs** at 10–14 days (resorption at the fracture line may then be visible) — a traditional but insensitive approach.
- **CT** — depicts the fracture line, displacement, comminution and is useful for assessing union and humpback deformity.
- **MRI** — the most sensitive test, able to confirm or exclude fracture acutely.
- **Bone scintigraphy** — sensitive but non-specific; now largely superseded by MRI.

**(b) Why MRI is superior [3]**

MRI is the most sensitive and specific modality for the **radiographically occult** scaphoid fracture and can be performed within hours of injury. Acute fracture shows a **low-signal line on T1** with surrounding **high signal on T2 fat-saturated/STIR** sequences reflecting marrow oedema. It thereby allows early definitive diagnosis — avoiding both prolonged unnecessary immobilisation of an uninjured wrist and the dangers of a missed fracture. MRI additionally assesses **proximal-pole vascularity/early avascular necrosis** and associated ligamentous injury, which radiographs and CT cannot.

**(c) Complications of a missed fracture [4]**

The scaphoid has a **retrograde (distal-to-proximal) blood supply**, so missed or untreated fractures, especially of the waist or proximal pole, lead to:
- **Non-union** — the commonest complication.
- **Avascular necrosis of the proximal pole** (poor proximal perfusion).
- **Scaphoid non-union advanced collapse (SNAC wrist)** — a predictable pattern of progressive **degenerative osteoarthritis** and carpal collapse/malalignment, with chronic pain and functional loss.

These outcomes underscore why a clinically suspected scaphoid fracture with normal radiographs warrants further imaging (MRI) rather than discharge.

### Q5. Short notes. [5+5]

**(a) Lipohaemarthrosis [5]**

Lipohaemarthrosis is the presence of **fat and blood within a joint**, and is a reliable indicator of an **intra-articular fracture** — marrow fat and haemorrhage escape from the fractured cancellous bone into the joint. Because fat is less dense than blood/serum, layering occurs and is best demonstrated on a **horizontal-beam (cross-table) radiograph or CT**, showing a **fat–fluid level** (and on CT/MRI sometimes a two- or three-layer fat–serum–cellular level). It is classically sought in the **knee** (lateral horizontal-beam view of the suprapatellar pouch) but occurs at the shoulder, elbow, hip and ankle. On **MRI**, fat-sensitive and fluid-sensitive sequences confirm the layered fat and dependent blood; chemical-shift artefact may be seen at the fat–fluid interface. **Significance:** its presence mandates a careful search for the responsible fracture even when the bony injury is not directly visible — *find the fracture*.

**(b) Insufficiency fractures of the sacrum [5]**

Sacral insufficiency fractures occur when **normal/physiological stress acts on abnormal, weakened bone** — typically the **osteoporotic elderly**, and patients post-pelvic **radiotherapy** or on long-term steroids. They present with low back/buttock pain, often without a clear history of trauma. **Radiographs** are frequently normal or show only subtle sclerosis of the sacral ala, so are insensitive. **Bone scintigraphy (Tc-99m MDP)** classically shows the **"Honda"/"H" sign** — bilateral vertical sacral-ala uptake joined by a horizontal component. **CT** shows the sclerotic fracture line(s) running vertically through the ala, sometimes with a transverse component, and excludes an underlying lytic/metastatic lesion. **MRI** is the most sensitive — band-like marrow **oedema** (low T1, high STIR) in the sacral ala, frequently in an **H/butterfly distribution**, with the low-signal fracture line within. The key differential is **metastatic disease/pathological fracture**, favoured by a soft-tissue mass or bone destruction; insufficiency fractures lack a destructive mass and follow the characteristic ala distribution.
