# Skeletal Changes in Haematological Disease (Thalassaemia, Sickle Cell Disease, Leukaemia)

The haemoglobinopathies and leukaemias share a final common pathway on imaging: **expansion of red marrow** (in chronic anaemias) or **replacement of marrow by abnormal cells** (in leukaemia), with superimposed **ischaemic/infarctive complications** and **iron-overload/treatment effects**. The candidate should be able to separate marrow hyperplasia changes from infarction changes, and to recognise which findings are disease-specific (e.g. "hair-on-end" skull, H-shaped vertebrae, leukaemic metaphyseal bands).

---

## 1. Classification / enumeration framework

Organise the skeletal manifestations by mechanism — this framework answers most essay questions.

**A. Marrow hyperplasia (compensatory, chronic haemolysis — thalassaemia >> sickle cell):**
- Widening of medullary space, thinning of cortex, coarsening/loss of trabeculae.
- Diploic widening of skull with "hair-on-end" striations; failure of paranasal sinus pneumatisation (esp. maxillary) -> "rodent facies" in thalassaemia.
- Extramedullary haematopoiesis (paravertebral, presacral, hepatosplenic, rarely intrathoracic).

**B. Ischaemia / infarction (predominantly sickle cell):**
- Bone infarcts (medullary), avascular necrosis (femoral and humeral heads).
- Dactylitis / hand-foot syndrome in infants.
- H-shaped ("fish-mouth"/"Lincoln-log") vertebrae from endplate central depression.
- Growth disturbance — premature physeal fusion, epiphyseal deformity.

**C. Marrow replacement / infiltration (leukaemia, and marrow reconversion):**
- Diffuse osteopenia, leukaemic lucent metaphyseal bands, periosteal reaction, lytic/permeative destruction, osteosclerosis (less common).

**D. Complications & treatment effects (all groups):**
- Infection — osteomyelitis (Salmonella classically in sickle cell), septic arthritis.
- Iron overload (transfusion) — marrow/organ signal loss on MRI; secondary haemochromatic arthropathy and osteoporosis.
- Chelation/desferrioxamine and chemotherapy effects on the growing skeleton.
- Secondary gout, secondary hyperuricaemia.

Keep "hyperplasia vs infarction vs infiltration" as the spine of any answer.

## 2. Modality-wise findings

### 2.1 Radiography (still the work-horse; first-line and most examinable)

**Thalassaemia.** Marrow hyperplasia dominates because the anaemia is severe and lifelong. The skull shows **widening of the diploic space with thinning of the outer table** and vertical trabecular striations producing the **"hair-on-end" appearance**; this is classically more florid and frontal in thalassaemia, with characteristic **sparing of the occiput below the internal occipital protuberance** (no marrow there). The **facial bones** show failure of pneumatisation of the paranasal sinuses (especially maxillary) and lateral displacement of the orbits, giving **"rodent facies."** In the appendicular skeleton and hands there is **medullary expansion, cortical thinning and a coarse "honeycomb"/reticulated trabecular pattern**; long bones may lose their normal modelling and show a rectangular/"Erlenmeyer-flask"-like widened metaphysis. The ribs may show posterior expansion and a "rib-within-a-rib" appearance; **costal/paravertebral extramedullary haematopoiesis** can produce smooth paraspinal soft-tissue masses.

![Lateral skull radiograph showing widened diploe with vertical hair-on-end striations sparing the occiput](images/thalassaemia-hair-on-end-skull.jpg)

**Sickle cell disease.** Marrow hyperplasia is present but usually **milder** than thalassaemia (anaemia is less severe); **infarction and growth changes dominate the radiographic picture.** Look for:
- **H-shaped (Lincoln-log) vertebrae** — central, square-shouldered endplate depressions from growth-plate ischaemia; a near-specific sign.
- **Hand-foot syndrome (dactylitis)** in infants/toddlers — soft-tissue swelling then periosteal reaction and a moth-eaten/destructive appearance of the small tubular bones, which usually heals.
- **Avascular necrosis** of femoral (and humeral) heads — subchondral lucency/"crescent sign," sclerosis, flattening, secondary osteoarthritis.
- **Medullary bone infarcts** — serpentine sclerosis/lucency with a sclerotic rim in the metadiaphysis.
- **Osteomyelitis** — aggressive periosteal reaction and lysis; *Salmonella* is the classically over-emphasised organism, though *Staphylococcus aureus* remains common overall (verify emphasis).

![AP spine radiograph showing H-shaped vertebrae with central endplate depressions in sickle cell disease](images/sickle-cell-H-shaped-vertebrae.jpg)

**Leukaemia (esp. childhood ALL).** Radiographs are insensitive but examinable:
- **Diffuse osteopenia** (often the earliest and commonest finding).
- **Leukaemic lines / lucent metaphyseal bands** — transverse radiolucent bands at the metaphyses of long bones; in a child beyond infancy these are suggestive of marrow infiltration (in neonates non-specific).
- **Permeative/moth-eaten osteolysis, periosteal reaction**, and occasionally **osteosclerosis** or focal lytic lesions.
- Vertebral collapse / generalised demineralisation may simulate metabolic bone disease.

### 2.2 Ultrasound (limited primary role)

Ultrasound does not assess marrow but is useful for **complications and extramedullary disease**: detecting **joint effusions** (early AVN, septic arthritis), guiding aspiration, assessing **subperiosteal collections** in osteomyelitis, demonstrating **hepatosplenomegaly and splenic sequestration/autosplenectomy** in sickle cell, and characterising **extramedullary haematopoietic masses**. In dactylitis, US can show soft-tissue swelling and early periosteal elevation before radiographs. State explicitly that US has **no role in marrow assessment** — that is MRI's domain.

### 2.3 CT

CT is problem-solving rather than first-line. It excellent depicts **cortical detail and the trabecular pattern** (diploic widening, honeycomb trabeculation), shows **paravertebral/posterior-mediastinal extramedullary haematopoiesis** as smooth, well-marginated, often fat-containing paraspinal soft-tissue masses, characterises **bone infarcts and the bony sequestrum/involucrum** of osteomyelitis, and stages **AVN-related collapse**. CT is also used to quantify **transfusional iron deposition** indirectly (hepatic attenuation) and to assess **gallstones** in sickle cell/haemolytic disease. Radiation dose limits its use in young, frequently-imaged patients.

### 2.4 MRI (the key modality for marrow and complications)

MRI is the most sensitive tool for marrow and is central to modern answers.

- **Marrow hyperplasia / reconversion:** persistence or reconversion of **red (cellular) marrow** which is **hypointense to fat on T1** and intermediate-to-mildly hyperintense on T2/STIR; the normal adult fatty (yellow) marrow signal is lost. Distribution helps — reconversion proceeds in reverse order to normal maturation (axial first, then proximal-to-distal in the limbs).
- **Bone infarction (sickle cell):** geographic lesion with a **serpentine low-signal rim**; the classic **"double-line sign"** on T2 (inner bright granulation tissue, outer dark sclerosis) in AVN. STIR/T2 marrow oedema indicates **acute infarct**, and MRI is the modality of choice to **distinguish acute infarct from osteomyelitis** (osteomyelitis favours soft-tissue collection, sinus tract, marked surrounding oedema and enhancement — though overlap is real; verify on a case basis).
- **Iron overload:** transfusional haemosiderosis causes **marked T2*/T2 signal loss ("dark" marrow, liver, spleen, sometimes pancreas/myocardium)**; **T2\* / R2\* mapping (and liver R2 / FerriScan-type techniques)** are used to quantify hepatic and **cardiac iron** to guide chelation (verify exact thresholds locally).
- **Leukaemia:** diffuse **low-T1 marrow** replacing fat (marrow signal lower than adjacent muscle/disc is a useful red flag), heterogeneous T2/STIR hyperintensity and diffuse enhancement; MRI also detects **chloroma/granulocytic sarcoma** and epidural disease causing cord compression.

![Coronal T1 MRI of pelvis showing diffuse low-signal marrow replacing normal fatty marrow in leukaemic infiltration](images/leukaemia-marrow-infiltration-T1.jpg)

### 2.5 Nuclear medicine

- **Bone scintigraphy (Tc-99m MDP):** in acute sickle infarction there is often **early reduced/absent uptake (photopenia)** followed by later increased uptake during healing; osteomyelitis shows increased uptake. The infarct-vs-osteomyelitis distinction may be aided by a **Tc-99m sulphur-colloid marrow scan** (infarct = photopenic on both bone and marrow agents; osteomyelitis = preserved/increased) or labelled-WBC studies (verify protocol).
- **FDG-PET** has a role in leukaemia/lymphoma for extramedullary disease (chloroma) and marrow assessment, but is not first-line for the haemoglobinopathies.

## 3. Differentials and comparison tables

### Thalassaemia vs sickle cell disease (skeletal emphasis)

| Feature | Thalassaemia major | Sickle cell disease |
|---|---|---|
| Dominant mechanism | Marrow **hyperplasia** (severe, lifelong anaemia) | **Infarction**/ischaemia; hyperplasia milder |
| Skull "hair-on-end" | Florid, frontal; **occiput spared** | Less common, less florid |
| Facial bones | "Rodent facies," poor sinus pneumatisation | Usually normal |
| Vertebrae | Osteopenia, coarse trabeculae | **H-shaped/Lincoln-log** endplates |
| Hands/feet | Trabecular coarsening, medullary expansion | **Dactylitis** (hand-foot syndrome) |
| AVN | Uncommon | **Common** (femoral/humeral head) |
| Extramedullary haematopoiesis | Common, often bulky | Less common |
| Osteomyelitis organism | — | *Salmonella* (classic), *S. aureus* |
| Iron overload | Marked (transfusion-dependent) | Variable (transfusion-related) |

### "Hair-on-end" skull — differential

| Cause | Helpful discriminator |
|---|---|
| Thalassaemia | Most florid; frontal; occiput spared; facial changes |
| Sickle cell / other chronic haemolytic anaemias | Milder, with systemic clues |
| Cyanotic congenital heart disease, severe iron-deficiency | Clinical context |
| Haemangioma of skull (focal "sunburst") | Focal, not diffuse |

### Acute bone infarct vs osteomyelitis in sickle cell (a classic exam dilemma)

| Feature | Infarct | Osteomyelitis |
|---|---|---|
| Onset/multiplicity | Often multifocal, rapid | Usually focal |
| Soft tissue | Little/none | Collection, sinus tract, abscess |
| MRI | Marrow oedema, thin rim enhancement | Marked oedema, **rim-enhancing collection**, cortical breach |
| Marrow scan | Photopenic | Preserved/increased uptake |

## 4. Pearls and buzzwords

- **"Hair-on-end" skull with occipital sparing + rodent facies = thalassaemia.**
- **H-shaped (Lincoln-log/fish-mouth) vertebrae = sickle cell** (central endplate infarction).
- **Hand-foot syndrome (dactylitis)** = often the first skeletal manifestation of sickle cell in infancy.
- **Salmonella osteomyelitis** is the classic association of sickle cell disease.
- **Leukaemic lucent metaphyseal bands** in a child beyond infancy suggest marrow infiltration; **diffuse osteopenia** is the commonest leukaemic finding.
- **MRI marrow signal lower than muscle/disc on T1** is a useful flag for pathological marrow replacement.
- **Double-line sign** on T2 = AVN.
- **T2\*/R2\* mapping** quantifies hepatic and **cardiac** iron and drives chelation decisions.
- Extramedullary haematopoiesis = **smooth, fat-containing, often paravertebral/posterior-mediastinal** masses; they tend to be **non-aggressive** and may contain marrow signal.
- Sickle cell **autosplenectomy** (small calcified spleen) vs thalassaemia **splenomegaly**.

## 5. What to draw

- **Lateral skull:** widened diploe with vertical "hair-on-end" striations, thinned outer table, and **arrow showing occipital sparing**; small/under-pneumatised maxillary sinus for "rodent facies."
- **Lateral spine:** vertebral body with **central H-shaped endplate depression** (sickle cell) beside a normal biconcave endplate for contrast.
- **Hand (infant):** dactylitis — soft-tissue swelling around metacarpals/phalanges with periosteal reaction.
- **Long-bone diagram:** medullary expansion + cortical thinning + coarse trabeculae (thalassaemia) versus a **serpentine medullary infarct with sclerotic rim** (sickle cell).
- **Coronal T1 pelvis:** diffuse dark marrow (leukaemia/reconversion) versus normal bright fatty marrow.
- **Mechanism triangle:** hyperplasia — infarction — infiltration, with each disease mapped to its dominant arm.
