## Complete answers

### Q1. Differentiate osteoarthritis from rheumatoid arthritis of the hand. [5+5]

A systematic approach uses the four reading axes — **distribution, bone density, joint space and erosion/new-bone pattern** — applied to a PA radiograph of both hands.

**(a) Osteoarthritis of the hand [5]**

OA is a **productive ("hypertrophic")** arthropathy with **normal bone density** and **no true synovial erosions**.

1. **Distribution:** asymmetric, targeting the **DIP joints (Heberden nodes)**, **PIP joints (Bouchard nodes)** and the **first carpometacarpal / trapezio-scaphoid** joint at the thumb base. The MCP joints are relatively spared.
2. **Bone density:** preserved (no periarticular osteopenia).
3. **Joint space:** **non-uniform / asymmetric narrowing**, greatest where mechanical load is highest.
4. **Productive change:** **marginal osteophytes**, **subchondral sclerosis (eburnation)** and **subchondral cysts (geodes)**.
5. **Erosive (inflammatory) OA** variant: **central erosions** producing the **"gull-wing"** deformity of the IP joints, but still with normal density and surrounding productive bone.

**(b) Rheumatoid arthritis of the hand [5]**

RA is the prototype **symmetric, erosive, "atrophic"** synovial arthritis.

1. **Distribution:** **bilateral and symmetric**, targeting the **MCP, PIP and wrist** (radiocarpal, ulnar styloid); the **DIP joints are characteristically spared** — the mirror image of OA.
2. **Bone density:** **periarticular (juxta-articular) osteopenia** — early and characteristic.
3. **Soft tissue:** fusiform periarticular swelling (synovitis).
4. **Joint space:** **uniform (concentric) narrowing** of the whole joint.
5. **Erosions:** **marginal erosions** at the **"bare areas"** (intracapsular bone uncovered by cartilage); no productive new bone.
6. **Late deformities:** **ulnar deviation** at the MCPs, swan-neck and boutonnière deformities, carpal crowding/ankylosis, arthritis mutilans.

**One-line discriminator:** OA = asymmetric, DIP-predominant, normal density, osteophytes, non-uniform narrowing; RA = symmetric, MCP/PIP/wrist, osteopenia, marginal erosions, uniform narrowing, DIP spared.

### Q2. Imaging features of seronegative spondyloarthropathies, including sacroiliac joint involvement. [10]

**Definition and unifying features.** The seronegative spondyloarthropathies (SpA) are a family — **ankylosing spondylitis (AS), psoriatic arthritis, reactive arthritis (Reiter) and enteropathic (IBD-associated) arthritis** — unified by **HLA-B27 association, RF-negativity, enthesitis, sacroiliitis/spondylitis, productive new bone, normal bone density (no periarticular osteopenia)** and **asymmetric / DIP-pattern peripheral disease.**

**Sacroiliac joint involvement (the high-yield discriminator).** Sacroiliitis is the earliest and most consistent finding. Radiographic evolution: **erosions and "pseudo-widening" → subchondral sclerosis → joint-space loss → bony ankylosis.** Pattern of symmetry localises the disease:

- **Bilateral, symmetric** → AS and enteropathic arthritis.
- **Bilateral, asymmetric** → psoriatic and reactive arthritis.
- **Unilateral** → infection or very early SpA.

**MRI of the SI joints** is the cornerstone of early diagnosis when radiographs are normal: **active inflammation = subchondral bone marrow oedema (high STIR/T2)**; **structural lesions = erosion, sclerosis, fat metaplasia and ankylosis.** This underpins the concept of **non-radiographic axial SpA.** CT best demonstrates established cortical erosion and ankylosis.

**Disease-specific peripheral / axial features.**

- **Ankylosing spondylitis:** axial-predominant. Spine shows **shiny corners (Romanus lesions)**, **vertebral body squaring**, **thin vertical marginal syndesmophytes** bridging to a **"bamboo spine"**, ligamentous ossification and **Andersson (discovertebral) lesions**. The ankylosed spine is brittle → unstable fractures.
- **Psoriatic arthritis:** asymmetric, **DIP / whole-"ray"** distribution, **"sausage digit" (dactylitis)**, **fluffy/whiskery periostitis**, the **"pencil-in-cup"** deformity and, when severe, **arthritis mutilans (opera-glass hand)**. Coarse, non-marginal/paravertebral syndesmophytes.
- **Reactive arthritis:** asymmetric **lower-limb** disease with prominent **calcaneal enthesopathy** (fluffy plantar/retrocalcaneal spurs).

**Pearl:** SpA is a **productive arthropathy with normal bone density** — contrast with the osteopenic, atrophic erosions of RA.

### Q3. Radiological features of gout. How does it differ from rheumatoid arthritis? [6+4]

**(a) Radiological features of gout [6]**

Chronic tophaceous gout produces a characteristic radiographic picture that **lags acute attacks by years**.

1. **Distribution:** **asymmetric**; classic target is the **first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra)**, then tarsus, hands and elbows (olecranon bursa).
2. **Erosions:** **punched-out "para-articular" erosions** with **sclerotic margins** and the characteristic **overhanging edge ("rat-bite" / Martel's hook)** of cortex.
3. **Joint space:** **preserved until late** — destruction of the cartilage is a late event.
4. **Bone density:** **normal** (no diffuse periarticular osteopenia).
5. **Soft tissue:** **eccentric soft-tissue masses = tophi**, which may **calcify** (especially in renal impairment).
6. **Advanced imaging:** **Dual-energy CT (DECT)** colour-codes and quantifies **monosodium urate** non-invasively; **ultrasound** shows the **"double-contour sign"** (urate coating hyaline cartilage) and hyperechoic tophi.

**(b) How gout differs from rheumatoid arthritis [4]**

| Feature | Gout | Rheumatoid arthritis |
|---|---|---|
| Symmetry | **Asymmetric** | **Symmetric, bilateral** |
| Bone density | **Normal** | **Periarticular osteopenia** |
| Erosion type | **Punched-out with overhanging edge**, para-articular | **Marginal**, bare-area |
| Joint space | **Preserved until late** | **Uniform narrowing** (early) |
| Soft tissue | **Eccentric tophus** (may calcify) | **Fusiform** synovitis |
| Target joint | **1st MTP** (podagra) | **MCP/PIP/wrist**, DIP spared |

In short: gout is an **asymmetric, normal-density** disease with **overhanging-edge erosions, preserved joint space and eccentric tophi**, whereas RA is a **symmetric, osteopenic** disease with **marginal erosions and uniform joint-space loss**.

### Q4. What is chondrocalcinosis? Enumerate its causes and describe the arthropathy of CPPD. [2+3+5]

**(a) Definition of chondrocalcinosis [2]**

Chondrocalcinosis is **radiographically visible calcification of cartilage** — linear or punctate calcium deposition within **hyaline (articular) cartilage** and **fibrocartilage**. Classic high-yield sites are the **knee menisci**, the **triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC) of the wrist** and the **symphysis pubis**. It is most commonly due to calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate (CPPD) crystal deposition, but is a *sign*, not a disease.

**(b) Causes [3]**

The classic enumeration — "the H's plus age":

1. **Idiopathic / ageing** (commonest).
2. **Hyperparathyroidism.**
3. **Haemochromatosis.**
4. **Hypophosphatasia** and **hypomagnesaemia.**
5. Associations: **gout** and **Wilson disease.**

(Remember to look for an underlying metabolic cause when chondrocalcinosis is florid or occurs in a young patient.)

**(c) Arthropathy of CPPD (pyrophosphate arthropathy) [5]**

CPPD crystal deposition can cause acute attacks (**"pseudogout"**) and a chronic structural arthropathy with these features:

1. **An OA-like picture in unusual joints / compartments** — the hallmark clue. Sites include the **radiocarpal compartment of the wrist**, the **patellofemoral compartment**, the **elbow** and the **shoulder** — joints that primary OA tends to spare.
2. **Radiocarpal involvement** progressing to a **SLAC-like (scapholunate advanced collapse) pattern** with scapholunate dissociation.
3. **Prominent subchondral cysts (geodes)**, often large and numerous.
4. **Structural change out of proportion** to the degree of typical OA, with **fragmentation** and sometimes a near-destructive appearance.
5. **Chondrocalcinosis** in adjacent cartilage/fibrocartilage as the accompanying clue.
6. **Ultrasound** shows hyperechoic intrasubstance foci within cartilage/fibrocartilage.

**Pearl:** "OA in the wrist (radiocarpal) or patellofemoral joint + chondrocalcinosis" → think CPPD pyrophosphate arthropathy.

### Q5. Short notes [5+5]

**(a) Atlanto-axial subluxation in RA [5]**

1. **Mechanism:** rheumatoid synovitis/pannus erodes the **transverse atlantal ligament** and the **odontoid process (dens)**, producing instability at the C1–C2 (atlanto-axial) joint. This is the commonest and most important cervical complication of RA.
2. **Type:** usually **anterior** atlanto-axial subluxation (C1 moves forward on C2); vertical subluxation (cranial settling / basilar invagination) and subaxial subluxation also occur.
3. **Radiographic sign:** **widening of the anterior atlanto-dental interval (ADI)** — abnormal if **> 3 mm in adults (verify exact value)** — best demonstrated on a **lateral cervical spine view in flexion** (dynamic instability).
4. **MRI** is essential to assess **pannus around the dens**, **cord compression** and the **posterior (cervico-medullary) space available for the cord**; it detects myelopathy risk that radiographs miss.
5. **Clinical significance:** risk of **cervical cord compression and myelopathy**; mandatory consideration before intubation/anaesthesia in RA patients.

**(b) Role of MRI / US in early inflammatory arthritis [5]**

Plain radiographs **lag** disease, showing erosions only after irreversible damage; MRI and US detect inflammation **before** radiographic change.

1. **Ultrasound:** demonstrates **joint effusion and synovial hypertrophy (hypoechoic pannus)**, with **active synovitis shown as hyperaemia on power/colour Doppler.** It detects **cortical erosions earlier than radiographs** and visualises **enthesitis, tenosynovitis and bursitis.** It is also a key tool for **crystal disease** (gout double-contour sign, hyperechoic tophi; CPPD cartilage foci) and **guides aspiration.** Limitations: operator-dependent, poor for deep/axial joints.
2. **MRI:** the most sensitive modality for early/pre-radiographic disease. It shows **synovitis/pannus** (enhances post-gadolinium), **bone marrow oedema (osteitis)** — an early predictor of subsequent erosion — and **erosions** before they are radiographically visible. In axial SpA, **SI-joint MRI** detects active sacroiliitis (subchondral marrow oedema) when radiographs are normal.
3. **Clinical impact:** enables **early diagnosis and treatment** within the therapeutic window, monitors disease activity and distinguishes active inflammation from established damage.
