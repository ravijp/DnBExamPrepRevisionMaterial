## Complete answers

### Q1. Describe the radiographic findings in rickets and osteomalacia. `[5+5]`

Rickets and osteomalacia are the **same disease of defective mineralisation of osteoid**, separated only by skeletal maturity: rickets affects the child with an open growth plate, osteomalacia the adult with a fused physis.

**Rickets `[5]`.** The abnormality is centred on the **growth plate and metaphysis**, so the most rapidly growing physes — distal radius/ulna (wrist), knee and costochondral junctions — are affected earliest and most severely. On the radiograph (the cornerstone; describe the wrist):

- The classic metaphyseal triad of **cupping, fraying and splaying**, with loss of the sharp white zone of provisional calcification.
- A **widened, lucent physis**, because the cartilage cannot mineralise.
- **Generalised osteopenia with coarse trabeculae**, and bowing of weight-bearing long bones (genu varum/valgum) once the child is ambulant.
- Regional signs: the **rachitic rosary** (expanded anterior costochondral junctions), **frontal bossing** with delayed fontanelle closure, and the **Harrison sulcus**.
- Greenstick/insufficiency fractures; on treatment, a dense line of provisional calcification reappears (healing rickets).

**Osteomalacia `[5]`.** With the physis fused, the changes are of **generalised softening** rather than physeal disruption:

- **Generalised osteopenia with a coarse, "smudgy"/indistinct trabecular pattern** and cortical thinning.
- The single most important sign is the **Looser zone (pseudofracture, Milkman line)** — a transverse lucent band perpendicular to the cortex with sclerotic margins, representing unmineralised osteoid; characteristically **bilateral and symmetrical** at the medial femoral neck, pubic/ischial rami, medial scapular border, proximal ulna and ribs.
- Softening deformities: **protrusio acetabuli**, triradiate/bell-shaped pelvis and biconcave ("codfish") vertebrae.

Cross-sectional imaging is not required for diagnosis in either condition; the plain radiograph with biochemistry is sufficient.

### Q2. Enumerate the radiological manifestations of hyperparathyroidism. Which sign is most specific? `[8+2]`

The unifying mechanism is excess parathyroid hormone driving **osteoclastic bone resorption**; subclassify the resorption patterns first, then list the secondary manifestations.

**Patterns of bone resorption `[8]`:**

1. **Subperiosteal resorption** — classically along the **radial (lateral) aspect of the middle phalanges of the index and middle fingers**, and at the phalangeal tufts (best seen on a magnified hand film).
2. **Acro-osteolysis** — resorption of the terminal phalangeal tufts.
3. **Subchondral resorption** — at the sacroiliac and acromioclavicular joints and symphysis pubis, and beneath the vertebral endplates (contributing to the rugger-jersey appearance).
4. **Intracortical / endosteal / trabecular resorption** — cortical tunnelling and the granular **"salt-and-pepper" skull**.

**Other manifestations:**

- **Brown tumours (osteitis fibrosa cystica)** — lytic, sometimes expansile lesions, more common in primary disease; may mimic metastasis or a primary tumour, so correlate with calcium.
- **Chondrocalcinosis** and **soft-tissue, vascular and periarticular calcification**.
- **Loss of the lamina dura** around the teeth.
- Generalised osteopenia and insufficiency fractures.
- Localising imaging (neck US, Tc-99m sestamibi, 4D-CT) is directed at the parathyroid lesion, not the skeleton.

**Most specific sign `[2]`.** **Subperiosteal bone resorption along the radial aspect of the middle phalanges of the index and middle fingers** is the most specific radiographic sign of hyperparathyroidism, and should be stated explicitly when asked.

### Q3. Describe the imaging spectrum of renal osteodystrophy. `[10]`

Renal osteodystrophy is the **composite skeletal manifestation of chronic kidney disease**, best answered as a combination of four processes rather than a single appearance.

**1. Secondary hyperparathyroidism.** Driven by phosphate retention, low calcitriol and hypocalcaemia. Produces **subperiosteal and subchondral resorption**, **acro-osteolysis**, the **salt-and-pepper skull**, and **brown tumours** (less common than in primary disease).

**2. Osteomalacia (renal).** **Generalised osteopenia with coarse trabeculae and Looser zones** in adults, and **rachitic changes (renal rickets)** at the physes in children.

**3. Osteosclerosis.** The signature is the **"rugger-jersey spine"** — alternating sclerotic bands at the superior and inferior vertebral endplates with a relatively lucent mid-body, resembling a striped rugby shirt. This is more characteristic of the renal/secondary setting than of primary hyperparathyroidism.

**4. Soft-tissue, vascular and periarticular calcification.** From a raised calcium-phosphate product — vascular wall calcification, periarticular **tumoral calcinosis** and chondrocalcinosis.

**Dialysis-related additions.** Long-standing dialysis adds **amyloid (beta-2-microglobulin) deposition** with subchondral cysts and erosions (shoulder, hip, carpus) and a **destructive spondyloarthropathy**. Aluminium-related and **adynamic bone disease** shift the balance between low-turnover osteomalacia and high-turnover hyperparathyroid change.

**Modality roles.** Radiographs of the hands, lateral spine and pelvis are the principal survey. CT confirms vascular/visceral calcification and brown tumours; MRI characterises amyloid and its complications; bone scintigraphy may show a **superscan**.

### Q4. Discuss the principle of DEXA and the WHO criteria for diagnosis of osteoporosis. What are the pitfalls? `[3+4+3]`

**Principle of DEXA `[3]`.** Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry passes **two X-ray beams of different energies** through the region of interest. Because soft tissue and bone attenuate the two energies differently, subtraction isolates the bone mineral content, expressed as an **areal bone mineral density (BMD) in g/cm²**. It is a projectional (2D, areal) technique, low in dose and highly reproducible, making it the gold standard for diagnosis and serial monitoring. Standard sites are the **lumbar spine (L1–L4)** and the **proximal femur (femoral neck and total hip)**, with the distal radius used when these are unevaluable. The **T-score** compares BMD to a young healthy adult; the **Z-score** to an age- and sex-matched population.

**WHO diagnostic criteria `[4]`** (apply the T-score to postmenopausal women and men aged 50 and over):

| Category | T-score |
|---|---|
| Normal | ≥ −1.0 |
| Osteopenia (low bone mass) | between −1.0 and −2.5 |
| Osteoporosis | ≤ −2.5 |
| Severe (established) osteoporosis | ≤ −2.5 with a fragility fracture |

In **premenopausal women, men under 50, and children**, the T-score criteria do not apply; the **Z-score** is used, with ≤ −2.0 reported as "below the expected range for age" (verify exact value against current ISCD guidance).

**Pitfalls `[3]`:**

- **Falsely raised spinal BMD** from degenerative osteophytes and facet sclerosis, aortic calcification, compression fractures, surgical instrumentation or overlying calcification — the **hip is often more reliable** in older patients.
- It is an **areal, not volumetric, measurement** — uncorrected for bone size, so it underestimates BMD in small-framed and overestimates in large-framed patients.
- Positioning/patient errors, and the need to use the **same scanner and software** for monitoring.
- **QCT** gives a true volumetric BMD (mg/cm³), isolates trabecular bone and is **unaffected by osteophytes/aortic calcification**, at the cost of higher dose and no WHO T-score definition.

### Q5. Short notes. `[5+5]`

**(a) Paget disease imaging `[5]`.** Paget disease is a disorder of **disordered bone remodelling** progressing through three phases:

- **Lytic phase** — in the skull, the well-defined lucency of **osteoporosis circumscripta**; in long bones, a **"blade-of-grass" / flame-shaped** advancing lytic front.
- **Mixed phase** — combined lysis and sclerosis.
- **Sclerotic phase** — late, dense bone.

The signature features are **cortical thickening, coarsened/thickened trabeculae and bone expansion (enlargement)**: bone that is both **bigger and denser** is the key discriminator from sclerotic metastasis. Classic regional signs are the **"picture-frame" vertebra**, the **"cotton-wool" skull**, basilar invagination, and bowing of weight-bearing bones. Complications: insufficiency fractures, high-output cardiac failure, and **sarcomatous transformation** (osteosarcoma), suggested by a new soft-tissue mass or aggressive lysis. The **bone scan** shows intense uptake and is the best tool for mapping disease extent.

**(b) Looser zones `[5]`.** A Looser zone (synonyms: **pseudofracture, Milkman line, Looser–Milkman zone**) is the hallmark radiographic sign of **osteomalacia** (and of the osteomalacic component of renal osteodystrophy). It is a **transverse lucent band oriented perpendicular to the cortex, with sclerotic margins**, representing a stress-related zone of **unmineralised osteoid** rather than a true complete fracture. Key features:

- Characteristically **bilateral and symmetrical**.
- Typical sites: the **medial femoral neck, the pubic and ischial rami, the medial border of the scapula, the proximal ulna, and the ribs**.
- They tend to occur on the compression/concave side of stressed bones.
- They must not be mistaken for a complete fracture or an aggressive lesion; bilateral symmetry, the perpendicular orientation with sclerotic margins, and biochemistry (low/low-normal calcium, low phosphate, raised alkaline phosphatase — verify per cause) confirm the diagnosis. On scintigraphy, multiple symmetrical foci may be seen, and in severe disease a **superscan** pattern.
