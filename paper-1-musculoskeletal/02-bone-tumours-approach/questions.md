# Bone Tumours (Approach) — Questions

## Previously asked (NBE)

- An 11-year-old boy with limping since 1 month. Describe your radiological approach to a suspected bone tumour. Discuss the imaging features that help differentiate a benign from a malignant bone lesion. *(DNB Jun 2022, Paper I)*

## Complete answers

### A1. Approach to a solitary bone lesion + benign vs malignant [6+4]

**Systematic approach (6 marks).** The radiograph remains the single most decisive investigation; I interrogate every lesion through a fixed seven-step framework so that the descriptive features themselves narrow the differential.

1. **Patient age** — the most powerful single discriminator. Under 30 years the differential is dominated by lesions such as eosinophilic granuloma, simple/aneurysmal bone cyst, osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma; over 40 years metastases, myeloma and chondrosarcoma predominate.
2. **Site within the skeleton and within the bone** — which bone, and the longitudinal position (epiphysis/metaphysis/diaphysis) and transverse position (central, eccentric, cortical, juxtacortical).
3. **Zone of transition** — the single best radiographic sign of biological aggressiveness; a narrow, sharply defined zone suggests slow growth, a wide imperceptible zone suggests aggression.
4. **Margin / Lodwick grade** — geographic (IA–IC), moth-eaten (II) or permeative (III) patterns reflect increasing growth rate.
5. **Periosteal reaction** — continuous/solid (slow) versus interrupted patterns (lamellated, spiculated, Codman triangle; aggressive).
6. **Matrix mineralisation** — chondroid (rings-and-arcs, popcorn) versus osteoid (cloud-like, ivory) versus ground-glass (fibrous).
7. **Cortex and soft tissue** — endosteal scalloping, cortical breach and a soft-tissue mass.

I then correlate clinically (pain, duration, systemic features) and select cross-sectional imaging for local staging and tissue characterisation. A vital safety habit is to recognise the **"leave-me-alone" (do-not-touch) lesions** that should not be biopsied — non-ossifying fibroma/fibroxanthoma, bone island (enostosis), fibrous dysplasia, healed/healing fracture, bone infarct and Paget disease — because confidently identifying these on imaging avoids needless intervention.

**Benign vs malignant (4 marks).**

| Feature | Benign | Malignant |
|---|---|---|
| Zone of transition | Narrow, sharp | Wide, ill-defined |
| Margin/Lodwick | Geographic (IA–IC), often sclerotic rim | Moth-eaten / permeative (II–III) |
| Periosteal reaction | Solid, continuous | Interrupted: lamellated, spiculated, Codman |
| Cortex | Intact / expanded-but-preserved | Destroyed/breached |
| Soft-tissue mass | Absent / small | Present, often large |
| Growth rate | Slow | Rapid |

Caveat: aggressive-looking benign lesions (eosinophilic granuloma, osteomyelitis, ABC) and bland-looking malignancies (low-grade chondrosarcoma) are recognised mimics — imaging guides but does not replace biopsy.

### A2. Imaging of skeletal metastases [10]

Metastases are the commonest malignant bone tumour in adults; the skeleton is the third commonest metastatic site after lung and liver. The axial skeleton (spine, pelvis, ribs, proximal long bones) bears red marrow and is preferentially involved, reflecting haematogenous spread.

**Primary sources and appearance.** Lytic metastases arise from kidney, thyroid, lung and gastrointestinal primaries (renal and thyroid classically expansile/"blow-out"). Sclerotic metastases are typical of prostate carcinoma, treated/scirrhous breast carcinoma, carcinoid, and **small-cell lung carcinoma**; mixed lytic-sclerotic lesions are seen in breast and lung. (Breast is the classic source of all three patterns.)

**Modality roles.**
- *Radiographs* are insensitive — roughly 30–50% of trabecular bone must be lost before a lytic lesion is visible (verify exact value) — but remain useful for characterising a known lesion and assessing fracture risk.
- *CT* depicts cortical destruction, the matrix and impending pathological fracture, and guides biopsy.
- *MRI* is the most sensitive technique for marrow disease; lesions are low signal on T1 (replacing fatty marrow), high on T2/STIR, and enhance. Whole-body MRI with diffusion is increasingly used for marrow screening.
- *Bone scintigraphy* (Tc-99m MDP) remains the workhorse for whole-skeleton survey, detecting the osteoblastic response; pitfalls include the **flare phenomenon** (transient increased uptake with treatment response), the **superscan** (diffuse uptake with faint kidneys), and **photopenic/false-negative** purely lytic lesions (myeloma, aggressive lytic mets).
- *FDG-PET/CT* detects lytic and marrow-based disease that scintigraphy misses and assesses extraosseous disease.

**Fracture-risk assessment.** In a long-bone metastasis the radiologist should comment on impending pathological fracture. The **Mirels scoring system** combines site, pain, lytic-vs-blastic nature and the extent of cortical involvement; broadly, a lytic lesion involving more than about half the cortical diameter or causing functional pain warrants orthopaedic referral for prophylactic fixation.

**Pearls.** A solitary "metastasis" in an adult without known primary requires biopsy. Vertebral metastases characteristically destroy the **pedicle** ("winking owl"/absent-pedicle sign on the frontal radiograph) and tend to spare the disc — a useful contrast with **myeloma, which tends to spare the pedicles until late** and with infective spondylodiscitis, which destroys the disc.

### A3. Periosteal reactions [5+5]

Periosteal reaction is new bone laid down by the periosteum in response to an underlying process; its **pattern reflects the rate of the insult, not its specific cause**. It is broadly divided into continuous (non-aggressive) and interrupted (aggressive) types.

**Continuous / non-aggressive (5 marks).** Slow processes allow the periosteum to lay down organised, mature bone.
- *Solid/dense* — a thick, uniform layer of cortical thickening; seen in chronic osteomyelitis, osteoid osteoma, healing fracture and eosinophilic granuloma in its healing phase.
- *Single thin lamella / thin shell* — a single thin layer; an early reaction that may be seen in benign processes but can also be the first sign of an aggressive lesion, so it is interpreted in context.
- *Buttressing and expanded shell with intact outer margin* — slow expansile lesions (e.g. ABC, fibrous dysplasia) where the cortex remodels rather than breaks.

**Interrupted / aggressive (5 marks).** Rapid processes outpace organised bone formation.
- *Lamellated ("onion-skin")* — multiple concentric layers; classically Ewing sarcoma, also osteomyelitis.
- *Spiculated — "sunburst"* — divergent radiating spicules perpendicular to cortex; osteosarcoma (also some metastases).
- *Spiculated — "hair-on-end"* — fine parallel spicules; seen in marrow expansion (e.g. thalassaemia, where the diploic change of the skull mimics a periosteal pattern) and some aggressive tumours.
- *Codman triangle* — a wedge of new bone at the margin where periosteum is elevated and the central reaction is destroyed; osteosarcoma, Ewing, aggressive osteomyelitis.

**Pearl.** No periosteal pattern is tumour-specific, and a Codman triangle alone is not proof of malignancy; aggressive osteomyelitis can reproduce every "malignant" pattern. Interpret the periosteal reaction together with the zone of transition and matrix.

### A4. Epiphyseal lytic lesion + GCT [3+7]

**Causes of an epiphyseal lytic lesion (3 marks).** A short, high-yield list (the "epiphyseal equator"):
- **Giant cell tumour** — in the skeletally mature patient (closed physis), abutting the articular surface.
- **Chondroblastoma** — in the skeletally immature patient (open physis), often with chondroid matrix.
- **Infection** (Brodie abscess crossing into the epiphysis) and, in adults, **clear-cell chondrosarcoma**, **subchondral geode/intraosseous ganglion**, and **Langerhans cell histiocytosis**.

**Giant cell tumour of bone (7 marks).** GCT is a locally aggressive, usually benign tumour of young adults (peak ~20–40 years, slight female predominance) arising **after physeal closure**. Sites: around the knee (distal femur, proximal tibia) and the distal radius; in the spine it favours the sacrum.

*Radiograph* — the classic description is an **eccentric, lytic, expansile lesion in the metaphysis extending into the epiphysis to the subarticular (subchondral) bone**, with a **narrow zone of transition and a well-defined but non-sclerotic margin (Lodwick IB)**, no matrix mineralisation, and a thinned/expanded cortex; the trabeculated "soap-bubble" appearance is typical. Importantly, GCT classically has **no sclerotic rim and no periosteal reaction** — a discriminating negative feature. More aggressive examples lose definition (progressing toward Lodwick IC), breach the cortex and develop a soft-tissue component.

*CT* — confirms the absence of mineralised matrix, defines cortical integrity and any pathological fracture, and better demonstrates subarticular extension.

*MRI* — predominantly low-to-intermediate T1 and heterogeneous signal; **low-signal areas from haemosiderin** are characteristic, and **fluid–fluid levels** indicate secondary aneurysmal-bone-cyst change (seen in roughly 10–14% — verify exact value). MRI defines soft-tissue and joint extension for surgical planning.

*Radiological grading and behaviour* — the **Campanacci system** grades GCT as I (latent: well-defined, intact cortex), II (active: thinned/expanded cortex), and III (aggressive: cortical destruction and soft-tissue mass). GCT recurs locally after curettage; a small proportion give rise to histologically benign **pulmonary "metastases"**, and rare malignant transformation occurs. Treatment options include intralesional curettage with an adjuvant (PMMA cement, phenol, high-speed burr), en-bloc resection for aggressive lesions, and **denosumab** (a RANK-ligand inhibitor) for unresectable/recurrent disease. A multifocal or histologically giant-cell-rich appearance should raise the differential of **brown tumours of hyperparathyroidism**, which GCT mimics.

### A5. Short notes [5+5]

**(a) Lodwick grading [5].** The Lodwick–Madewell system grades the **pattern of bone destruction** as a surrogate for the lesion's growth rate and hence aggressiveness; it is the formal expression of the "zone of transition" concept. Grading uses defined sub-criteria — presence of a sclerotic rim, definition of the margin, degree of cortical penetration and whether an expanded shell is maintained.

- **Grade IA** — geographic lysis with a sharply defined **sclerotic rim** (slowest; e.g. non-ossifying fibroma, simple bone cyst).
- **Grade IB** — geographic lysis with a well-defined margin but **no sclerotic rim** (e.g. giant cell tumour).
- **Grade IC** — geographic lysis with an **ill-defined/ragged margin** and cortical penetration.
- **Grade II** — **moth-eaten** destruction (multiple medium-sized lucencies, wider zone of transition).
- **Grade III** — **permeative** destruction (innumerable tiny lucencies, imperceptible margin; most aggressive — round-cell tumours such as Ewing/lymphoma, and aggressive infection).

Ascending grade correlates with increasing biological aggressiveness, but is not a histological diagnosis.

**(b) Imaging of multiple myeloma [5].** Myeloma is a clonal plasma-cell malignancy of older adults; bone disease is purely **osteolytic** because it suppresses osteoblastic activity.

- *Radiographs/skeletal survey* — multiple, well-circumscribed **"punched-out" lytic lesions without sclerotic rims** (the "raindrop" skull), classically in the skull, spine, pelvis and proximal long bones; diffuse osteopenia and vertebral compression fractures also occur.
- *Bone scintigraphy is characteristically NEGATIVE/photopenic* because there is little osteoblastic reaction — a key pitfall; cross-sectional imaging or the survey must be used instead.
- *Whole-body low-dose CT (WBLDCT)* is sensitive for lytic lesions and, with whole-body MRI and FDG-PET/CT, has **superseded the conventional radiographic skeletal survey** in current IMWG recommendations for detecting bone disease.
- *MRI* is the most sensitive for marrow infiltration and is required for spinal cord/cauda compression; patterns include focal, diffuse, and "salt-and-pepper" variegated marrow.
- *FDG-PET/CT* assesses disease activity and extramedullary disease.

In the IMWG framework, a focal lytic lesion or focal marrow lesions on imaging count among the **myeloma-defining events** (alongside the **CRAB** features — hyperCalcaemia, Renal impairment, Anaemia, Bone lesions). A solitary lesion (plasmacytoma) may precede disseminated disease.
