1. A 38-year-old woman has fasting hypoglycaemia with raised insulin and C-peptide (Whipple triad). Describe your imaging strategy to localise the suspected insulinoma, including the role and limitations of each modality. [6+4]

2. Outline the imaging features of pancreatic neuroendocrine tumours across CT and MRI, and explain why arterial-phase imaging is essential. Add the role of gallium-68 DOTATATE PET/CT. [6+4]

3. Describe the technique and imaging findings of dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI for a functioning pituitary microadenoma. What is the next step when MRI is negative in suspected Cushing disease? [7+3]

4. Discuss the imaging approach to a young woman with rapid-onset virilisation and markedly raised testosterone. How do you distinguish a tumoural cause from polycystic ovary syndrome? [5+5]

5. Compare MEN1 and MEN2 in terms of component tumours and explain the concept of imaging surveillance in MEN syndromes. [5+5]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — Insulinoma localisation [6+4]

Insulinoma is biochemically confirmed before imaging (the Whipple triad with inappropriately raised insulin/C-peptide), so the radiological task is purely localisation of a typically small (<2 cm), evenly distributed, intrapancreatic, usually benign tumour.

I would begin with a **multiphasic pancreatic-protocol CT**: unenhanced, late-arterial (pancreatic parenchymal) and portal-venous phases with thin sections. Insulinomas are hypervascular and enhance avidly on the late-arterial phase, appearing as a bright nodule that may become iso-dense on portal-venous phase; this is why a portal-venous-only study will miss them. **Dynamic gadolinium MRI** is an excellent alternative or adjunct: lesions are T1-hypointense, T2-hyperintense, show avid early arterial enhancement and often restrict diffusion, and MRI avoids radiation in young patients needing follow-up.

If cross-sectional imaging is negative, **endoscopic ultrasound (EUS)** is the most sensitive next step, particularly for small lesions in the head and uncinate, and it allows fine-needle aspiration. **Gallium-68 DOTATATE PET/CT** can help but carries an important caveat — well-differentiated insulinomas may have low somatostatin-receptor density and can be falsely negative, so a negative DOTATATE does not exclude the diagnosis. At surgery, **intra-operative ultrasound** is the gold-standard adjunct, especially for deep or multiple lesions.

Limitations to note: transabdominal US is insensitive because of bowel gas and lesion size; CT may miss tiny or iso-enhancing lesions if the arterial phase is suboptimal; EUS is operator-dependent and the tail is less accessible. I would also remember the MEN1 association: multiplicity, a young patient, or family history should prompt a search for synchronous pancreatic, parathyroid and pituitary lesions.

### Answer 2 — PNET imaging features and DOTATATE [6+4]

Pancreatic neuroendocrine tumours are typically hypervascular. On **multiphasic CT** they enhance avidly on the late-arterial (pancreatic parenchymal) phase as well-defined bright nodules and tend to become iso- or hypodense relative to pancreas on the portal-venous phase. Larger, often non-functioning tumours may show heterogeneous enhancement, central necrosis, cystic change and coarse calcification, and may demonstrate nodal and hypervascular hepatic metastases.

On **MRI** they are characteristically T1-hypointense and T2-hyperintense, with avid early arterial enhancement on dynamic gadolinium imaging and frequent restricted diffusion. MRI's superior soft-tissue contrast and DWI make it particularly good for small primaries and for detecting small hepatic deposits, without ionising radiation — valuable in young or syndromic patients under repeated surveillance.

**Arterial-phase imaging is essential** because the diagnostic feature of these tumours is brisk arterial enhancement; on the portal-venous phase a small lesion frequently equilibrates with the pancreas and disappears. Protocols that omit a dedicated late-arterial phase therefore systematically miss small functioning tumours.

**Gallium-68 DOTATATE PET/CT** exploits somatostatin-receptor over-expression and is the functional imaging standard, with much higher sensitivity than the older indium-111 octreotide scintigraphy. It detects occult primaries (notably small or multiple duodenal gastrinomas), characterises metastatic burden on a whole-body basis, and confirms receptor status predicting response to peptide-receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT). A key caveat is that well-differentiated insulinomas can be receptor-poor and DOTATATE-negative, while poorly differentiated high-grade tumours lose receptor expression and are better assessed with FDG-PET.

### Answer 3 — Dynamic pituitary MRI for microadenoma [7+3]

A microadenoma is a pituitary adenoma under 10 mm and is hunted on the basis of proven hormone excess (raised prolactin, the Cushing biochemical picture, or raised GH/IGF-1). The decisive technique is **dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI**: thin (around 2-3 mm) coronal and sagittal T1 sequences acquired rapidly during and immediately after a gadolinium bolus.

The principle rests on differential enhancement kinetics. The normal anterior pituitary enhances briskly and homogeneously, whereas a microadenoma enhances more slowly. Therefore, on the early dynamic frames, the lesion appears as a focal **relatively hypoenhancing focus** against the avidly enhancing normal gland; on delayed images this difference may reverse or wash out, which is why correct timing is critical and a delayed-only study can miss the lesion.

Supportive secondary signs include focal upward convexity of the gland surface, deviation of the infundibulum away from the lesion, and focal depression or erosion of the sellar floor — none specific in isolation.

When MRI is negative or equivocal in suspected **Cushing disease** but the biochemistry is convincing, the next step is **bilateral inferior petrosal sinus sampling (BIPSS)**. This catheter-based functional test confirms a central (pituitary) source of ACTH and helps lateralise the adenoma, guiding the surgeon when imaging cannot. It is worth noting that corticotroph adenomas are often the smallest and most occult of the functioning microadenomas, and that in MEN1 the pituitary lesion is most often a prolactinoma.

### Answer 4 — Virilisation: tumour vs PCOS [5+5]

Rapid-onset virilisation with markedly raised testosterone is a red flag for an androgen-secreting tumour and demands a directed search. I would image the **ovaries** with high-resolution transvaginal/transabdominal ultrasound and pelvic MRI, looking for a unilateral solid or solid-cystic, frequently hypervascular adnexal mass; the classic culprit is a **Sertoli-Leydig cell tumour** in a young woman, with steroid (lipid) cell tumours another androgenic source. If the ovaries are normal, I would image the **adrenals** with dedicated CT/MRI for an androgen-secreting cortical adenoma or carcinoma — a large, heterogeneous, irregular mass with venous invasion or metastases favouring adrenocortical carcinoma.

The critical distinction from **polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)** is both clinical and radiological. PCOS produces gradual, peripubertal, mild hyperandrogenism with only modestly raised testosterone and metabolic features. Sonographically the ovaries are bilaterally enlarged with numerous small peripheral follicles in a "string of pearls" pattern and increased echogenic central stroma. By contrast, a tumoural cause gives rapid, progressive, severe virilisation with markedly elevated testosterone and a unilateral solid/hypervascular ovarian or adrenal mass.

The simple discriminator to state in an exam: **bilateral polyfollicular ovaries with gradual mild androgen excess = PCOS; unilateral solid hypervascular ovarian/adrenal mass with rapid severe virilisation = tumour.** Postmenopausal stromal hyperthecosis (solid enlarged ovaries) is a further non-neoplastic androgenic cause to remember. Correlating the imaging with the degree and tempo of biochemistry is essential to direct the search to the right organ.

### Answer 5 — MEN1 vs MEN2 and surveillance [5+5]

The MEN syndromes are autosomal-dominant conditions producing multiple, metachronous endocrine tumours. **MEN1 (Wermer)** is remembered as the "3 Ps": **Parathyroid** hyperplasia (very common, primary hyperparathyroidism), **Pancreatic** neuroendocrine tumours (gastrinoma, insulinoma and non-functioning PNETs), and **Pituitary** adenoma (commonly prolactinoma); it is linked conceptually to the menin tumour-suppressor gene.

**MEN2** centres on the thyroid/adrenal axis and the RET proto-oncogene. **MEN2A (Sipple)** comprises **medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC)**, **phaeochromocytoma** (frequently bilateral) and **parathyroid** hyperplasia. **MEN2B** comprises MTC and phaeochromocytoma plus mucosal neuromas, a marfanoid habitus and intestinal ganglioneuromatosis, but typically lacks parathyroid disease.

The **surveillance imaging concept** follows from the lifelong, multiplicity of tumours: imaging is a programmatic, repeated screening exercise rather than a single diagnostic study. In **MEN1**, this means periodic pancreatic cross-sectional imaging (multiphasic CT or MRI, often supplemented by gallium-68 DOTATATE PET/CT to detect PNETs early), periodic pituitary MRI, and biochemical screening for hyperparathyroidism; radiation-sparing MRI is preferred for repeated follow-up in young patients. In **MEN2**, biochemical surveillance (calcitonin, plasma/urinary metanephrines) drives imaging — neck ultrasound for the thyroid and CT/MRI with MIBG or DOTATATE for phaeochromocytoma — with prophylactic thyroidectomy guided by genetics and calcitonin.

The overarching radiological principle is that **multiplicity, bilaterality and young age are syndromic red flags**: on finding one functioning endocrine tumour, the radiologist should actively look for the others and enquire about family history, then ensure the patient enters an appropriate surveillance programme.
