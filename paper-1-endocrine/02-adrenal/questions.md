## Complete answers

### Answer 1 (Adrenal CT washout protocol)

The dedicated adrenal CT protocol comprises three phases: an unenhanced scan, a contrast-enhanced portal venous phase at about 60-70 seconds, and a delayed phase at about 15 minutes. On the unenhanced scan, a region of interest is placed over roughly two-thirds of the lesion, avoiding necrosis and the edge. A homogeneous lesion measuring **<=10 HU is a lipid-rich adenoma** and requires no further work-up; the low density reflects its intracytoplasmic (microscopic) lipid. Lesions measuring above 10 HU are indeterminate by density alone — this group includes lipid-poor adenomas as well as phaeochromocytoma, adrenocortical carcinoma and metastases — and proceed to washout calculation.

The principle is that adenomas, whether lipid-rich or lipid-poor, enhance and then de-enhance (wash out) rapidly, whereas non-adenomas retain contrast and wash out slowly. Two formulae are used. **Absolute percentage washout (APW)** = (enhanced HU - delayed HU) / (enhanced HU - unenhanced HU) x 100, used when all three phases are available; an adenoma is suggested by a high APW, commonly cited around 60% (verify exact value). **Relative percentage washout (RPW)** = (enhanced HU - delayed HU) / (enhanced HU) x 100, used when no unenhanced scan is available; an adenoma is suggested by a high RPW, commonly cited around 40% (verify exact value).

The rule of thumb: an adenoma washes out a lot; a non-adenoma washes out little. A key pitfall is that phaeochromocytoma and occasional hypervascular metastases or ACC can show adenoma-range washout, so washout is not infallible and must be interpreted alongside biochemistry, lesion size and morphology, and any history of primary malignancy.

### Answer 2 (Adrenal incidentaloma work-up)

An adrenal incidentaloma is a lesion discovered on imaging performed for an unrelated indication. Work-up proceeds along two parallel arms — biochemical and imaging — because a lesion that looks benign may still be hormonally active.

The biochemical arm asks whether the lesion is functional. All incidentalomas are screened for cortisol excess using an overnight dexamethasone suppression test, and for phaeochromocytoma using plasma or 24-hour urinary metanephrines. If the patient is hypertensive or hypokalaemic, the aldosterone-to-renin ratio is added to screen for Conn syndrome. A hyperfunctioning lesion is referred for surgery even if imaging is benign.

The imaging arm asks whether the lesion is benign. A homogeneous lesion measuring <=10 HU on unenhanced CT is a lipid-rich adenoma and needs no further imaging. A lesion containing macroscopic fat is a myelolipoma and is benign. A lesion above 10 HU is indeterminate and proceeds to adrenal washout CT or chemical-shift MRI; adenoma-range washout (high APW/RPW) or opposed-phase signal drop confirms an adenoma. If the lesion remains indeterminate, is large (>4 cm), shows suspicious morphology (heterogeneity, necrosis, irregular margins, invasion), or grows on follow-up, the concern for malignancy rises and one considers FDG-PET/CT, image-guided biopsy (only after phaeochromocytoma has been biochemically excluded), or resection. Combining the two arms allows confident triage of the great majority of incidentalomas into benign-non-functional (leave alone), functional (refer for surgery), or indeterminate/suspicious (further imaging or resection).

### Answer 3 (Chemical-shift MRI)

Chemical-shift imaging (CSI) is the key MRI technique for characterising the indeterminate adrenal mass, particularly the lipid-poor lesion, and is performed without ionising radiation. It exploits the fact that hydrogen protons in water and in fat precess at slightly different resonant frequencies. Two gradient-echo sequences are acquired in a single breath-hold at different echo times: an **in-phase** image, in which fat and water signals within a voxel add together, and an **opposed-phase** image, in which they are out of phase and cancel.

A voxel that contains both fat and water — as in a lesion with **intracytoplasmic (microscopic) lipid** — therefore loses signal on the opposed-phase image relative to the in-phase image. This **signal drop on opposed-phase is diagnostic of a lipid-containing adenoma**. It is assessed both visually (the lesion darkens on opposed-phase) and quantitatively (adrenal-to-spleen signal ratio or signal-intensity index).

Lesions that show signal drop are adenomas containing microscopic lipid. Lesions that do **not** show significant drop include most metastases, adrenocortical carcinoma, phaeochromocytoma, and importantly the lipid-poor adenoma — which is the principal trap, because it may be indeterminate on both CT density and CSI and is then best characterised by washout. It is important to distinguish this microscopic lipid from the **macroscopic (bulk) fat** of a myelolipoma: bulk fat follows fat on all sequences, suppresses on fat-saturated images, and shows India-ink (chemical-shift) artefact only at the interface between fat and non-fatty tissue rather than diffuse intravoxel signal loss. CSI and unenhanced CT density essentially detect the same microscopic lipid, so they are complementary rather than independent confirmations.

### Answer 4 (Phaeochromocytoma)

Phaeochromocytoma is a catecholamine-secreting tumour of adrenal medullary chromaffin cells, with the extra-adrenal counterpart termed paraganglioma. On CT it is usually a soft-tissue-density mass measuring above 10 HU, often heterogeneous with cystic or necrotic change in larger lesions, and shows **avid enhancement**; importantly it tends to **retain contrast and wash out slowly**, although occasional lesions show adenoma-range washout, which is a recognised pitfall. On MRI the classic finding is **marked T2 hyperintensity — the "light-bulb" sign — with avid post-contrast enhancement**, and it shows no significant signal drop on opposed-phase imaging because it lacks intracytoplasmic lipid.

In nuclear medicine, **MIBG (metaiodobenzylguanidine)** is taken up by chromaffin tissue and confirms and localises the tumour, being especially valuable for multiple, extra-adrenal or metastatic disease, and 131-I MIBG can be used therapeutically. FDG-PET/CT and somatostatin-receptor imaging are also used in selected cases.

The classic **rule of 10s** states that approximately 10% are bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% malignant, 10% occur in children, 10% are familial (now known to be a higher proportion with modern genetic testing — verify exact value), and 10% are not associated with hypertension.

The crucial safety caveat: **do not biopsy a suspected phaeochromocytoma**, and ensure the lesion is biochemically excluded (plasma or urinary metanephrines) before any intervention, because mechanical disturbance can precipitate a life-threatening hypertensive crisis. Adequate alpha-blockade is required before surgery.

### Answer 5 (Myelolipoma vs ACC vs metastasis)

**Myelolipoma** is a benign tumour composed of mature adipose tissue and haematopoietic elements. Its defining feature is **macroscopic (bulk) fat**: it appears echogenic on ultrasound, contains regions of fat attenuation (very low/negative HU) on CT, and follows fat signal on MRI with suppression on fat-saturated sequences and India-ink artefact at its margin. The presence of bulk fat is essentially diagnostic and the lesion is benign; large lesions can occasionally haemorrhage. It is non-functional.

**Adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC)** is a rare aggressive malignancy that classically presents as a **large mass (often >4-6 cm), heterogeneous, with necrosis, haemorrhage and calcification**, irregular margins, and local invasion or venous (renal vein/IVC) tumour thrombus. It is generally above 10 HU, washes out slowly, shows no opposed-phase signal drop, and is frequently FDG-avid. Many are functional (Cushing, virilisation), and the combination of a large functional heterogeneous mass should prompt urgent referral.

**Adrenal metastases** are common in patients with known primary malignancy (lung, breast, melanoma, renal, GI). They are typically soft-tissue density above 10 HU, show **low washout** (helping distinguish them from adenomas), no significant opposed-phase signal drop, variable T2 signal, and are **frequently bilateral**. The strongest clue is a relevant primary tumour history. FDG-PET/CT supports the diagnosis and aids staging; biopsy may be used for confirmation once phaeochromocytoma is excluded.
