## Practice questions

*The following are practice questions, written in DNB style. They are uncited and not from any recorded past paper.*

1. Describe the ACR TI-RADS framework for characterising a thyroid nodule. Outline the five feature categories and how the points lead to a recommendation for FNA. [6+4]

2. A 40-year-old woman has a solitary thyroid nodule. Discuss the ultrasound features that distinguish a benign from a malignant nodule, and the role of scintigraphy. [6+4]

3. Outline the imaging approach to diffuse thyroid disease (Graves disease, Hashimoto thyroiditis and thyroiditis) on ultrasound and scintigraphy. [5+5]

4. Classify thyroid malignancies and describe their patterns of spread and relevant imaging findings, including nodal assessment. [4+6]

5. A patient with primary hyperparathyroidism is referred for localisation. Describe the role and findings of ultrasound, dual-phase Tc-99m sestamibi and 4D-CT. [4+3+3]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — ACR TI-RADS [6+4]

ACR TI-RADS is a points-based ultrasound reporting system that standardises thyroid nodule risk stratification and the decision to perform FNA. Each nodule is scored across five feature categories.

(1) Composition: cystic/spongiform score 0, mixed cystic-solid 1, solid 2. (2) Echogenicity: anechoic 0, hyper/isoechoic 1, hypoechoic 2, very hypoechoic 3. (3) Shape: wider-than-tall 0, taller-than-wide 3 (assessed on the transverse plane). (4) Margin: smooth or ill-defined 0, lobulated/irregular 2, extrathyroidal extension 3. (5) Echogenic foci (additive): none or large comet-tail 0, macrocalcification 1, peripheral/rim calcification 2, punctate echogenic foci 3.

The points are summed to give a TR level: 0 = TR1 (benign), 2 = TR2 (not suspicious), 3 = TR3 (mildly suspicious), 4-6 = TR4 (moderately suspicious), >=7 = TR5 (highly suspicious).

The TR level combined with the maximum nodule diameter drives management. FNA is recommended at >= 2.5 cm for TR3, >= 1.5 cm for TR4 and >= 1.0 cm for TR5; US follow-up is advised at >= 1.5, >= 1.0 and >= 0.5 cm respectively. TR1 and TR2 need neither FNA nor routine follow-up. (These are the published ACR 2017 thresholds; confirm against the current white paper.)

The practical value is reproducibility: spongiform and cystic nodules reliably score low, while solid, very hypoechoic, taller-than-wide, irregularly marginated nodules with punctate foci accumulate points into TR4/TR5, concentrating biopsy on the highest-risk lesions and reducing unnecessary FNA of benign nodules.

### Answer 2 — Benign vs malignant nodule; role of scintigraphy [6+4]

Ultrasound is the primary tool. Features favouring **malignancy** are: solid composition; marked hypoechogenicity (darker than strap muscle); taller-than-wide shape (anteroposterior dimension greater than transverse on the transverse image, reflecting growth across tissue planes); irregular, lobulated or spiculated margins; extrathyroidal extension (loss of the capsule line, invasion of strap muscle or trachea); and punctate echogenic foci representing psammomatous microcalcification of papillary carcinoma. Suspicious intranodular chaotic vascularity and associated abnormal cervical nodes add weight.

Features favouring a **benign** nodule are: purely cystic or spongiform (honeycomb microcystic) composition; iso- or hyperechogenicity; wider-than-tall shape; smooth well-defined margin with a thin complete halo; large comet-tail artefact (inspissated colloid); and perinodular rather than intranodular flow. These features underpin the TI-RADS scoring that then guides FNA.

**Scintigraphy** addresses function, not morphology, and is indicated chiefly when TSH is suppressed. Tc-99m pertechnetate or I-123 identifies an autonomously functioning ("hot") nodule that suppresses the rest of the gland — a toxic adenoma; because hot nodules are very rarely malignant, a confirmed hot nodule generally does not require FNA. A "cold" (non-functioning) nodule shows reduced/absent uptake; although most cold nodules are still benign, malignancy is concentrated in this group, so cold nodules are characterised and sampled according to their ultrasound TI-RADS features. Thus US characterises and selects for biopsy, while scintigraphy is reserved to exclude an autonomously functioning nodule in the biochemically hyperthyroid patient.

### Answer 3 — Diffuse thyroid disease on US and scintigraphy [5+5]

**Graves disease** (autoimmune hyperthyroidism): ultrasound shows a diffusely enlarged, hypoechoic gland with markedly increased, disorganised colour Doppler flow ("thyroid inferno"). Scintigraphy shows diffuse, homogeneously increased uptake with elevated radioiodine uptake, matching the biochemical thyrotoxicosis.

**Hashimoto (chronic lymphocytic) thyroiditis**: ultrasound shows a heterogeneous, hypoechoic gland with coarsened echotexture and multiple small hypoechoic pseudonodules separated by echogenic fibrous septa (a "giraffe-skin" pattern). The gland may initially enlarge and later atrophy; there is a recognised long-term association with thyroid lymphoma, so a rapidly enlarging Hashimoto gland warrants attention. Scintigraphic uptake is variable and the test is not central to diagnosis, which rests on antibodies and ultrasound.

**Thyroiditis (subacute de Quervain / silent / post-partum)**: in the acute thyrotoxic phase, ultrasound shows ill-defined hypoechoic areas, often with a painful gland (subacute) and reduced vascularity. The crucial scintigraphic finding is **low radiotracer uptake despite thyrotoxicosis**, because stored hormone is leaking from damaged follicles rather than being newly synthesised. This low-uptake thyrotoxicosis distinguishes thyroiditis (and exogenous or iodine-induced thyrotoxicosis) from Graves disease, which shows high uptake — a frequently tested discriminator.

In a multinodular gland, **toxic multinodular goitre** gives patchy multifocal areas of hot and cold uptake, in contrast to the uniform uptake of Graves and the single focus of a toxic adenoma.

### Answer 4 — Thyroid cancer classification, spread and imaging [4+6]

**Classification.** Differentiated thyroid carcinomas arise from follicular cells: **papillary** (most common, excellent prognosis) and **follicular** (second commonest, older patients). **Medullary** carcinoma arises from parafollicular C cells, secretes calcitonin and is associated with MEN2. **Anaplastic** carcinoma is an aggressive, undifferentiated tumour of the elderly with poor prognosis. **Thyroid lymphoma** arises on a background of Hashimoto thyroiditis.

**Spread and imaging.** Papillary carcinoma spreads via **lymphatics** to cervical nodes (central level VI and lateral levels II-IV). The primary often shows microcalcifications and a taller-than-wide shape; nodal metastases characteristically show rounded morphology with loss of the fatty hilum, cystic change, punctate calcification, hyperechoic foci and peripheral/chaotic vascularity. Follicular carcinoma spreads **haematogenously** to lung and bone; ultrasound cannot reliably separate a follicular adenoma from carcinoma because the distinction (capsular and vascular invasion) is histological. Medullary carcinoma spreads by both lymphatic and haematogenous routes, may show coarse calcification, and mandates screening for phaeochromocytoma and hyperparathyroidism if MEN2 is suspected. Anaplastic carcinoma presents as a large, invasive, necrotic mass with rapid local extension into the airway and great vessels, nodal and distant metastases.

**Imaging roles.** Ultrasound assesses the primary and the nodal basins and guides FNA of the node or aspirate for thyroglobulin/calcitonin washout. Contrast CT of the neck and chest evaluates extrathyroidal extension, airway/oesophageal invasion, retrosternal/mediastinal nodal disease and pulmonary metastases; iodinated contrast should be avoided if radioiodine therapy is planned. Post-thyroidectomy, I-131 whole-body scintigraphy detects residual or metastatic iodine-avid tissue.

### Answer 5 — Parathyroid adenoma localisation [4+3+3]

Localisation is undertaken only after biochemical confirmation of primary hyperparathyroidism (hypercalcaemia with inappropriately raised PTH); imaging guides minimally invasive parathyroidectomy and does not make the diagnosis. About 85% of cases are a single adenoma, the remainder being multigland hyperplasia or, rarely, carcinoma.

**Ultrasound** is first line for accessible neck disease. An adenoma appears as a well-defined, ovoid, homogeneously hypoechoic structure lying posterior to the thyroid (typically near the upper or lower poles), with a feeding polar vessel or peripheral vascular arc on Doppler. US is cheap and radiation-free and can guide FNA with PTH washout, but is operator-dependent and misses ectopic glands that are mediastinal, retro-oesophageal, intrathyroidal or undescended.

**Dual-phase Tc-99m sestamibi** is a functional study. MIBI is taken up by both thyroid and hyperfunctioning parathyroid tissue, but washes out of thyroid faster; on early images (about 10-15 minutes) both are seen, while on delayed images (about 1.5-3 hours) thyroid activity fades and a focus of retained activity marks the adenoma. SPECT/CT adds anatomical localisation and is particularly valuable for ectopic and mediastinal glands. Sensitivity falls for small adenomas, multigland hyperplasia and when coexistent thyroid nodules confound interpretation.

**4D-CT** is a multiphasic study (non-contrast plus arterial and delayed phases). A parathyroid adenoma typically shows low non-contrast attenuation, avid early arterial enhancement and relative washout on the delayed phase, distinguishing it from lymph nodes and thyroid tissue. It provides a precise anatomical roadmap and is the problem-solver for re-operative cases or when US and MIBI are negative or discordant, at the cost of radiation and iodinated contrast.
