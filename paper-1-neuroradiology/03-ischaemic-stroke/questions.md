## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — NCCT early signs and ASPECTS [6+4]

Non-contrast CT is the first investigation in acute stroke because it is fast and reliably excludes haemorrhage, the main contraindication to thrombolysis. Early ischaemic changes reflect cytotoxic oedema, which raises tissue water and lowers attenuation by only a few Hounsfield units, so the signs are subtle and require systematic comparison with the contralateral side.

The **hyperdense MCA sign** is intraluminal thrombus appearing as a dense M1 segment; dense thrombus in a distal Sylvian branch is the **MCA dot sign**. Caution is needed because a high haematocrit or mural calcification can mimic this, so always compare both sides. **Loss of the insular ribbon** is obscuration of the normally visible insular cortical grey-matter band. **Obscuration of the lentiform nucleus** is loss of the sharp margin and attenuation of the putamen and globus pallidus, indicating lenticulostriate (deep MCA) ischaemia. More generally there is **loss of grey-white differentiation** and **sulcal effacement** from swelling.

**ASPECTS (Alberta Stroke Program Early CT Score)** is a 10-point semi-quantitative score for the MCA territory. The hemisphere is divided into defined regions on two axial levels — the caudate, lentiform nucleus, insula and internal capsule on the lower level, and cortical regions M1-M6 on the cortical levels. A normal scan scores 10, and one point is subtracted for each region showing early ischaemic change. A lower score indicates a larger established core; higher scores generally predict better thrombectomy outcomes. Exact selection cut-offs have changed across trials, so the principle rather than a fixed threshold should be quoted (verify exact value).

### Answer 2 — Core versus penumbra and CT perfusion [4+6]

The **infarct core** is irreversibly dead tissue; the **penumbra** is hypoperfused but still viable brain kept alive by collateral flow, and it is the target of reperfusion therapy. Beyond the penumbra lies **benign oligaemia**, mildly underperfused tissue that survives without treatment. The central aim of acute imaging is to estimate how much core has already formed and how much penumbra remains to be saved.

**CT perfusion** tracks an intravenous contrast bolus to generate parametric maps. **CBF** is blood flow per tissue mass per unit time, **CBV** is blood volume per tissue mass, and **MTT** and **Tmax** are timing parameters that prolong when perfusion is delayed. The interpretive logic is: any ischaemic tissue shows **prolonged MTT/Tmax**. Within that abnormality, the **core** shows severely **reduced CBF and reduced CBV** — autoregulatory compensation has failed and the tissue is dead. The **penumbra** shows **prolonged transit and reduced CBF but preserved or relatively maintained CBV**, because collateral vessels maintain volume. The difference between the perfusion deficit and the core is the **mismatch**, which represents salvageable penumbra.

This matters for selection: a patient with a **small core and large penumbra (large mismatch)** is likely to benefit from reperfusion, particularly in extended or uncertain time windows, whereas a large completed core predicts poor benefit and higher haemorrhage risk. The specific relative-CBF, Tmax and mismatch-ratio thresholds are vendor- and trial-dependent and should be cited only as concepts (verify exact value). MR diffusion-perfusion imaging applies the same logic with DWI as the core surrogate.

### Answer 3 — DWI, ADC and DWI-FLAIR mismatch [6+4]

**Diffusion-weighted imaging** is the earliest and most sensitive sequence for acute ischaemia, becoming positive within minutes of arterial occlusion — far earlier than NCCT or conventional MRI. The mechanism is cytotoxic oedema: failure of the sodium-potassium pump shifts water into cells, restricting its random microscopic motion. Restricted tissue appears **bright on high-b-value DWI**. The essential confirmation is the **ADC map**, on which true restriction is **dark (low ADC)**. This pairing distinguishes genuine restriction from **T2 shine-through**, where a T2-hyperintense lesion looks bright on DWI but is **not** dark on ADC.

The temporal evolution is examinable. ADC is **lowest (darkest) in the hyperacute and acute phase**, then **pseudonormalises** toward the end of the first week (verify exact timing) as vasogenic change supervenes, and in the chronic phase ADC becomes **high (bright)** with encephalomalacia. DWI signal therefore must always be read together with ADC and with timing.

The **DWI-FLAIR mismatch** is used to estimate lesion age in **wake-up or unknown-onset stroke**, where the time of onset is not known. An infarct that is **bright on DWI but not yet bright on FLAIR** is likely young — broadly within the early treatment window (often quoted around the first several hours; verify exact value) — because FLAIR signal develops later than DWI. This pattern supports offering reperfusion therapy despite uncertain onset. When the lesion is **bright on both DWI and FLAIR**, it is probably older and outside the early window. Other MRI sequences complement this: SWI/GRE detect haemorrhagic transformation and the susceptibility thrombus, and MRA shows the occlusion.

### Answer 4 — Complete acute stroke imaging pathway [10]

Imaging in acute stroke is time-critical and answers two questions: is there haemorrhage, and is there salvageable brain supplied by a treatable occluded vessel? It proceeds in a defined sequence.

**Step 1 — Non-contrast CT.** Performed first to exclude intracranial haemorrhage, the principal contraindication to thrombolysis, and to exclude mimics. Early ischaemic signs (hyperdense MCA, insular ribbon loss, lentiform obscuration, loss of grey-white differentiation) are sought and the **ASPECTS** score estimates early core burden; a very large established core argues against intervention.

**Step 2 — CT angiography (arch to vertex).** Identifies **large-vessel occlusion** (intracranial ICA, MCA M1/M2, basilar), which is the prerequisite for mechanical thrombectomy, and defines proximal anatomy for endovascular access. **Multiphase CTA** grades **collateral circulation**, a strong predictor of tissue fate.

**Step 3 — CT perfusion or MRI (DWI/perfusion).** Used especially in extended or uncertain windows to quantify **core versus penumbra**. A small core with large mismatch identifies patients likely to benefit from reperfusion; a large core predicts poor outcome and haemorrhage risk. MRI with DWI-FLAIR mismatch additionally helps date wake-up strokes.

**Step 4 — Disposition.** **IV thrombolysis** is offered when haemorrhage is excluded and the patient is eligible within the accepted time window. **Mechanical thrombectomy** is offered for demonstrated LVO with a favourable core/penumbra profile, including selected patients in extended windows. Specific time windows, ASPECTS and perfusion thresholds are guideline- and trial-dependent and should be stated as principles (verify exact values). Throughout, the radiologist also watches for contraindications and for early haemorrhagic transformation. The pathway can be summarised as: exclude bleed, find the clot, measure salvageable tissue, then treat.

### Answer 5 — Arterial versus venous infarction [6+4]

**Arterial and venous cerebral infarction** differ fundamentally in distribution and behaviour, and confusing them leads to missed diagnoses.

**Distribution.** An **arterial infarct conforms to a recognised arterial territory** (for example the MCA supplies most of the lateral hemisphere plus deep grey via the lenticulostriates; the ACA the medial parasagittal cortex; the PCA the occipital lobe and medial temporal/thalamic regions). A **venous infarct does not respect arterial territories**; instead its distribution matches the obstructed venous drainage, often producing lesions in locations that make no arterial sense (e.g., parasagittal or temporal lesions from superior sagittal or transverse sinus thrombosis).

**Haemorrhage and oedema.** Arterial infarcts are typically less haemorrhagic early. **Venous infarcts frequently bleed, often out of proportion** to the clinical picture, and show prominent **vasogenic oedema** that may precede frank infarction because the primary problem is venous congestion.

**Key signs.** Arterial occlusion is shown on CTA/MRA as a vessel cut-off or absent flow. Venous occlusion shows a **dense sinus on NCCT**, the **empty delta sign** (a filling defect in the contrast-opacified superior sagittal sinus on CECT/CTV), and absent flow on CTV/MRV. Clinical context differs too — venous thrombosis favours hypercoagulable states, dehydration and the peripartum period.

**Haemorrhagic transformation of arterial infarcts** ranges from petechial haemorrhagic infarction to frank parenchymal haematoma. It is best detected on **SWI/GRE** sequences, which show blooming. Risk is higher with large infarcts, cardioembolic occlusion, and after reperfusion (lysis or thrombectomy), and its recognition is important because it influences antithrombotic management.
