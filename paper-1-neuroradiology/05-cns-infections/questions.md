1. Describe the imaging features that differentiate a pyogenic brain abscess from a necrotic/cystic intracranial tumour, including the role of advanced MR sequences. [6+4]

2. Outline the four parenchymal stages of neurocysticercosis with their CT/MRI appearances. Describe the racemose and intraventricular forms and their complications. [6+4]

3. Discuss the neuroimaging of CNS tuberculosis, covering basal meningitis with its complications and the tuberculoma. [5+5]

4. A 28-year-old presents with fever, confusion and seizures. MRI shows asymmetric medial temporal and insular T2/FLAIR hyperintensity. Discuss the likely diagnosis, imaging features, and differentials. [4+6]

5. Enumerate the differential diagnosis of a ring-enhancing intracranial lesion and describe how multiparametric MRI (DWI, perfusion, spectroscopy, SWI) helps narrow it. [4+6]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — Abscess vs necrotic tumour [6+4]
A mature pyogenic abscess and a necrotic neoplasm can look identical on conventional contrast imaging, so the distinction rests on wall morphology plus advanced MR. The abscess wall is typically a **smooth, thin, regular ring** that is characteristically **thinner along its medial (ventricular) margin**, whereas a high-grade glioma or metastasis usually has a **thick, irregular, nodular** enhancing rim. On MRI the abscess centre is T1-hypointense/T2-hyperintense with a **T2-hypointense capsule**, surrounded by vasogenic oedema.

The decisive sequence is **DWI**: the abscess cavity contains viscous, cellular pus that **restricts strongly** — bright on DWI with low ADC. The necrotic centre of most tumours does **not** restrict (high ADC). **Perfusion** complements this: the enhancing **rim of an abscess shows low rCBV**, while the margin of a high-grade glioma shows **high rCBV** from neovascularity. **MR spectroscopy** is fairly specific — an abscess yields **amino acids (~0.9 ppm), acetate, succinate, lactate and alanine** from bacterial metabolism, with absent normal metabolites, whereas a tumour shows **elevated choline and reduced NAA**. **SWI** may show a **dual-rim sign** in abscess (two concentric rims), not seen in GBM.

For the 4-mark advanced component: I would emphasise that DWI is the primary discriminator, perfusion and spectroscopy are confirmatory, and that exceptions (mucinous metastases, treated lesions) mean findings must be integrated with clinical context (immune status, source of sepsis). A coexisting sinusitis/mastoiditis or endocarditis supports abscess.

### Answer 2 — Neurocysticercosis [6+4]
Parenchymal NCC evolves through four stages. **Vesicular:** a viable larva forms a thin-walled cyst with CSF-like signal and an eccentric **scolex** giving the **"hole-with-dot"** sign; there is little oedema and minimal enhancement because the live larva evades the immune response. **Colloidal vesicular:** the larva dies, cyst fluid becomes turbid (signal slightly above CSF), the wall **enhances as a ring**, and there is **marked perilesional oedema** — the most symptomatic stage and the commonest mimic of abscess or tumour. **Granular nodular:** the cyst retracts to a small enhancing nodule with decreasing oedema. **Calcified nodular:** a small calcified nodule, best shown on CT (or SWI blooming), with no oedema or enhancement — the end-stage, which may still provoke seizures.

For the second part: **racemose NCC** is the extraparenchymal cisternal form — clustered **"bunch of grapes"** cysts in the basal/sylvian cisterns, usually **without a scolex**, causing chronic arachnoiditis, vasculitis and **obstructive hydrocephalus**; it behaves aggressively and may need long treatment. **Intraventricular** cysts (commonly the fourth ventricle) are thin-walled, may be hard to see (isointense to CSF — look for the scolex or wall enhancement on FLAIR/post-contrast), and can cause **acute obstructive hydrocephalus** requiring urgent intervention. I would note CT's superiority for the calcified stage and MRI's superiority for the scolex and for cisternal/intraventricular disease.

### Answer 3 — CNS tuberculosis [5+5]
**TB meningitis** characteristically produces a thick, enhancing **basal exudate** filling the suprasellar and ambient cisterns; non-contrast CT may show effaced/hyperdense basal cisterns and FLAIR shows cisternal hyperintensity. The three classic complications to demonstrate are: **communicating hydrocephalus** (exudate blocking CSF resorption — dilated temporal horns, periventricular interstitial oedema); **infarcts**, typically in the basal ganglia/internal capsule from a vasculitis affecting perforators that traverse the inflamed exudate; and **cranial nerve enhancement** from involvement at the skull base. These together form a recognisable triad on contrast imaging.

**Tuberculoma** is a granuloma whose imaging hallmark is a **T2-hypointense core**, reflecting caseation. A non-caseating granuloma enhances **solidly**; a caseating granuloma with a solid centre shows **ring enhancement around a T2-hypointense centre**; central liquefactive caseation gives a T2-hyperintense centre. The T2-hypointense core is a key feature separating tuberculoma from pyogenic abscess (which has a bright, restricting centre). **MR spectroscopy** classically shows a dominant **lipid peak** (~0.9-1.3 ppm). **Miliary TB** gives numerous tiny enhancing nodules, usually with pulmonary miliary disease. A **tuberculous abscess** is larger, may restrict diffusion and can mimic pyogenic abscess but usually arises in an immunocompromised host with other TB evidence. I would close by noting that coexisting basal meningitis greatly increases confidence in a tubercular aetiology for any parenchymal lesion.

### Answer 4 — Herpes simplex encephalitis [4+6]
The clinical picture (fever, encephalopathy, seizures) with **asymmetric medial temporal and insular** T2/FLAIR hyperintensity is highly suggestive of **herpes simplex encephalitis (HSE)**, a neurological emergency requiring empirical aciclovir.

Imaging features: HSE has a strong **limbic predilection** — the medial temporal lobes, insular cortex, inferior frontal lobes and cingulate gyri show **swelling and T2/FLAIR hyperintensity**, usually **bilateral but asymmetric**, with characteristic **sparing of the basal ganglia** (a key sign distinguishing it from a middle cerebral artery infarct, which involves the basal ganglia and respects a vascular territory). **DWI** is the most sensitive early sequence, showing cortical restriction before T2 changes are obvious. Later features include **gyriform enhancement** and **petechial haemorrhage** (blooming on SWI/GRE). The distribution does not conform to an arterial territory, which further argues against infarction.

Differentials to discuss: **MCA infarction** (wedge-shaped, territorial, basal ganglia involved); **limbic encephalitis** (paraneoplastic/autoimmune — usually less swelling, no haemorrhage, often bilateral and symmetric); **status epilepticus-related cortical changes**; **low-grade glioma** (no fever, more mass effect, slower course); and other viral encephalitides — notably **Japanese encephalitis**, which preferentially involves the **thalami** (often haemorrhagic) and basal ganglia rather than the limbic cortex. Given the high mortality of untreated HSE, imaging suggestive of limbic involvement should prompt immediate treatment and CSF PCR rather than waiting for confirmation.

### Answer 5 — Ring-enhancing lesion and multiparametric MRI [4+6]
The differential of a ring-enhancing lesion is conveniently enumerated as: **abscess** (pyogenic, tubercular, fungal), **neoplasm** (high-grade glioma, metastasis), **tuberculoma/NCC (colloidal)**, **demyelination (tumefactive — open ring)**, **subacute infarct or resolving haematoma**, and **radiation necrosis**. Clinical context (immune status, fever, primary malignancy, prior radiotherapy) narrows this substantially before any advanced imaging.

Multiparametric MRI then refines the diagnosis. **DWI** is the single most useful sequence: a **bright, restricting centre with low ADC** indicates pus and points to a **pyogenic abscess**, whereas a necrotic tumour centre usually does not restrict. **Perfusion (DSC rCBV)** assesses the enhancing rim — **low rCBV favours abscess**, **high rCBV favours high-grade glioma or metastasis**. **MR spectroscopy** is often confirmatory: **amino acids, acetate and succinate** indicate a pyogenic abscess; a dominant **lipid peak** suggests a **tuberculoma**; **high choline with low NAA** indicates **tumour**. **SWI** contributes the **dual-rim sign** (favours abscess), blooming calcification (calcified NCC, old tuberculoma) and haemorrhage (angioinvasive fungal disease). The **morphology** of the ring also matters — smooth and thin (thinnest medially) for abscess versus thick and nodular for tumour, and an **open/incomplete ring** classically for tumefactive demyelination. I would conclude that no single sequence is definitive: the robust approach is to combine ring morphology, DWI, perfusion and spectroscopy with the clinical picture, acknowledging recognised exceptions (mucinous metastases, treated lesions) that can mimic restriction.
