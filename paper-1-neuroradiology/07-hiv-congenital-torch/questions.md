## Practice questions

*Note: the following are practice questions written for revision. They are uncited and are NOT drawn from any recorded past NBE/DNB paper.*

1. A 32-year-old HIV-positive man (CD4 60) presents with focal seizures. MRI shows multiple ring-enhancing lesions in the basal ganglia and at the grey-white junction. Discuss the imaging differentiation of cerebral toxoplasmosis from primary CNS lymphoma, including the role of advanced MR and nuclear imaging. [6+4]

2. Describe the imaging features of progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) and contrast them with HIV encephalopathy. [6+4]

3. Write short notes on the CNS manifestations of cryptococcal and CMV infection in the HIV-positive patient. [5+5]

4. Discuss the patterns of intracranial calcification and associated malformations in congenital CMV and congenital toxoplasmosis, and how imaging helps distinguish them. [6+4]

5. Outline an imaging approach to a neonate with suspected congenital (TORCH) infection, including choice of modality and the key findings to seek. [4+6]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — Toxoplasmosis vs PCNSL [6+4]

In an HIV-positive patient with a low CD4 count and ring-enhancing lesions, the two principal considerations are cerebral toxoplasmosis and primary CNS lymphoma (PCNSL, EBV-driven). Distribution offers the first clue: toxoplasmosis favours the basal ganglia and the corticomedullary junction and is usually multiple, whereas PCNSL is characteristically periventricular/subependymal, may abut the ependyma, and can cross the corpus callosum. Subependymal spread strongly favours lymphoma.

On conventional MRI, toxoplasmosis typically shows ring or nodular enhancement; the eccentric target sign — an asymmetric enhancing mural nodule along one wall of the ring — is the classic pointer to toxoplasmosis. In AIDS, lymphoma is frequently necrotic and may also ring-enhance, so morphology alone is insufficient.

Advanced MR resolves much of the overlap. On DWI/ADC, the densely cellular lymphoma tends to restrict diffusion (low ADC), whereas necrotic toxoplasmosis shows higher ADC. On DSC perfusion, lymphoma demonstrates elevated relative CBV, while toxoplasmosis (an avascular necrotic lesion) shows low rCBV. MR spectroscopy of toxoplasmosis is dominated by lipid and lactate with depression of normal metabolites, whereas lymphoma shows elevated choline with a lipid-lactate peak.

Nuclear imaging gives a clean rule: lymphoma is metabolically active and concentrates tracer (increased thallium-201 SPECT uptake, FDG-PET hypermetabolism), whereas toxoplasmosis is "cold" (low uptake, hypometabolic). Practically, a therapeutic trial of anti-toxoplasma therapy is often used: clinical and imaging improvement supports toxoplasmosis, while failure to respond raises lymphoma and prompts biopsy. CSF EBV PCR and serology supplement imaging.

### Answer 2 — PML vs HIV encephalopathy [6+4]

Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy results from JC virus reactivation in the profoundly immunosuppressed (typically very low CD4). The hallmark is asymmetric, multifocal, confluent white-matter disease that is T2/FLAIR hyperintense and T1 hypointense. The lesions characteristically involve the subcortical U-fibres, frequently begin in the parieto-occipital white matter, and spare the overlying cortex. A defining negative feature is the absence of enhancement and the absence of mass effect disproportionate to lesion size. A thin peripheral rim of restricted diffusion may mark the advancing edge of active demyelination. When enhancement appears, it suggests PML-associated immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (PML-IRIS) following antiretroviral therapy.

HIV encephalopathy (HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder) reflects the direct effect of the virus. Imaging shows symmetric, confluent, non-enhancing periventricular and deep white-matter T2/FLAIR hyperintensity, which characteristically spares the U-fibres, together with cerebral atrophy (ventricular and sulcal enlargement) out of proportion to age. There is no mass effect.

The contrast for the exam: PML is asymmetric, involves U-fibres, and is non-enhancing without significant atrophy; HIV encephalopathy is symmetric, spares U-fibres, and is dominated by atrophy. Both lack enhancement, so symmetry, U-fibre involvement and the degree of atrophy are the practical discriminators. CSF JC virus PCR confirms PML.

### Answer 3 — Cryptococcal and CMV CNS infection [5+5]

Cryptococcal infection (Cryptococcus neoformans) most often produces a meningitis in advanced HIV. CT may be normal or show only hydrocephalus. The characteristic parenchymal finding is the gelatinous pseudocyst: clusters of dilated perivascular (Virchow-Robin) spaces filled with organisms and mucoid material, typically in the basal ganglia, following CSF signal on all MRI sequences and producing a "soap-bubble" appearance; these are usually non-enhancing. Leptomeningeal enhancement reflects meningitis, and a discrete enhancing mass with oedema represents a true cryptococcoma. Hydrocephalus is a common complication. CSF India ink and cryptococcal antigen confirm the diagnosis.

CMV affects the CNS at very low CD4 counts. The classic imaging pattern is ventriculitis/ependymitis: a thin periventricular T2/FLAIR hyperintense rim with ependymal enhancement lining the ventricular margins, often with ventriculomegaly and sometimes subependymal nodularity. CMV may also cause an encephalitis (diffuse white-matter and grey-matter signal change) and a polyradiculomyelitis. Periventricular ependymal enhancement in an AIDS patient should prompt consideration of CMV. Both infections require correlation with CD4 count, CSF studies and the broader clinical picture, and both may coexist with other opportunistic processes.

### Answer 4 — Congenital CMV vs toxoplasmosis [6+4]

Congenital infections produce intracranial calcification, and the pattern combined with the associated malformation is the key to diagnosis. Cytomegalovirus, the commonest congenital infection, characteristically causes periventricular calcification. Because CMV injures the germinal matrix and disturbs neuronal migration, it is associated with malformations of cortical development — lissencephaly, pachygyria and polymicrogyria — together with microcephaly. Additional clues are anterior temporal white-matter abnormality with temporal-pole cysts, cerebellar hypoplasia, ventriculomegaly, germinolytic subependymal cysts and delayed myelination. The earlier in gestation the fetus is infected, the more severe the migrational and destructive consequences; later infection produces gliosis and atrophy rather than malformation.

Congenital toxoplasmosis classically produces scattered, random parenchymal calcification (cortex, basal ganglia, and periventricular regions may all be involved) rather than a strictly periventricular pattern, and crucially is associated with hydrocephalus, typically from aqueductal obstruction by the inflammatory process, producing macrocephaly. It is not primarily a disorder of neuronal migration. Chorioretinitis is a strong clinical association.

Imaging strategy: CT (or SWI on MRI) best demonstrates the calcification pattern, while MRI best shows the malformation and parenchymal injury. The discriminating summary: CMV gives periventricular calcification with a migration anomaly and microcephaly (small head); toxoplasmosis gives scattered calcification with hydrocephalus (often large head). Correlation with maternal serology and neonatal testing confirms the agent.

### Answer 5 — Imaging approach to suspected neonatal TORCH [4+6]

The TORCH spectrum (toxoplasma, others including syphilis/VZV/parvovirus/Zika, rubella, CMV, herpes) should be suspected in a neonate with microcephaly, seizures, hepatosplenomegaly, chorioretinitis, sensorineural deafness or intrauterine growth restriction. Imaging is targeted at detecting calcification, malformation and parenchymal destruction, and at narrowing the causative agent.

The practical first-line investigation is cranial ultrasound through the open fontanelle: it is portable, radiation-free and bedside-feasible in a sick neonate. Sought findings include echogenic calcific foci, lenticulostriate vasculopathy (branching echogenic lines in basal ganglia/thalami), ventriculomegaly, germinolytic cysts and white-matter echogenicity.

CT is the most sensitive modality for calcification and helps assign the pattern — periventricular (CMV) versus scattered parenchymal (toxoplasmosis) — but its radiation burden in a neonate must be weighed; SWI on MRI is an increasingly used radiation-free alternative for mineralisation.

MRI is the definitive characterising study: it demonstrates malformations of cortical development (lissencephaly, polymicrogyria in CMV), temporal-pole cysts, cerebellar hypoplasia, delayed myelination, hydrocephalus (toxoplasmosis), and the destructive/cystic encephalomalacia of neonatal herpes.

Interpretation should integrate the calcification pattern, the associated malformation, head size (microcephaly with CMV/rubella versus macrocephaly with toxoplasmosis hydrocephalus) and the timing principle (earlier insult causing malformation, later insult causing atrophy/gliosis), all correlated with serology and confirmatory laboratory testing.
