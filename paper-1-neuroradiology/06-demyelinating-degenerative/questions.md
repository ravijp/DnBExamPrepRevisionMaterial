1. Describe the characteristic MRI features of multiple sclerosis and explain the concept of dissemination in space and time as used in diagnosis. [6+4]
2. A 6-year-old child presents with encephalopathy two weeks after a viral illness. Discuss the imaging differential between ADEM and multiple sclerosis, with supporting features for each. [5+5]
3. Outline the imaging spectrum of NMOSD and MOGAD and explain why distinguishing these from multiple sclerosis is clinically important. [6+4]
4. A 40-year-old HIV-positive patient develops progressive neurological deficit. Describe the MRI features of progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy and how you would differentiate it from its mimics. [5+5]
5. Discuss the imaging patterns of common neurodegenerative and atypical parkinsonian disorders, naming the key sign for each. [10]

## Complete answers

**1. MRI features of MS and dissemination in space/time. [6+4]**

Multiple sclerosis is a chronic inflammatory demyelinating disease imaged primarily with MRI (T2/FLAIR, pre/post-gadolinium T1, DWI, increasingly SWI). Plaques are T2/FLAIR hyperintense and characteristically ovoid, oriented perpendicular to the ventricular margin along deep medullary veins — Dawson fingers — best seen on sagittal FLAIR at the callososeptal interface. Lesions favour four characteristic locations: periventricular, juxtacortical/cortical, infratentorial (brainstem, cerebellar peduncles), and spinal cord (typically short-segment, peripheral, under two vertebral segments). Active plaques enhance; the classic pattern is open-ring (incomplete-ring) enhancement, with the open margin facing grey matter, a feature favouring demyelination over abscess or tumour. Chronic disease shows callosal and cord atrophy; SWI may show a central vein sign and iron-rim lesions.

Dissemination in space (DIS) means demonstrating lesions in two or more of the four characteristic CNS locations, establishing that disease is multifocal. Dissemination in time (DIT) means demonstrating lesions of different ages — either the appearance of a new lesion on follow-up imaging, or the simultaneous presence of enhancing and non-enhancing lesions on a single scan (an enhancing lesion is recent while a non-enhancing one is older). Demonstrating both DIS and DIT on MRI, integrated with clinical attacks, underpins the McDonald-criteria approach to diagnosis. The principle, rather than memorised numeric tables, is the examinable point.

**2. ADEM versus MS in a post-viral child. [5+5]**

ADEM is typically a monophasic post-infectious or post-vaccinial demyelinating illness, classically in a child with encephalopathy. MRI shows large, bilateral but asymmetric, fluffy and ill-defined T2/FLAIR lesions in subcortical and deep white matter, frequently involving deep grey nuclei (thalami, basal ganglia) and the brainstem. A key feature is that lesions appear of similar age, consistent with a single insult; enhancement is variable. The cord may be involved. CT is often normal.

Multiple sclerosis, by contrast, occurs in young adults with a relapsing course. Its lesions are discrete and ovoid, with periventricular Dawson fingers, juxtacortical and infratentorial plaques, and short-segment peripheral cord lesions. Critically, MS lesions are of differing ages (the basis of dissemination in time), so a mix of enhancing and non-enhancing lesions favours MS, while uniformly enhancing lesions of one age favour ADEM. Deep grey matter involvement and a monophasic course favour ADEM; new lesions over time and the characteristic distribution favour MS.

Supporting features for ADEM: young age, recent infection/vaccination, encephalopathy, large fluffy lesions, deep grey involvement, lesions of one age, usually no new lesions on follow-up. Supporting features for MS: young adult, relapsing-remitting clinical history, ovoid periventricular Dawson fingers, callososeptal lesions, and lesions of multiple ages. Clinical context and follow-up imaging are decisive, as a single scan can overlap. MOGAD should also be considered in a child with ADEM-like presentation.

**3. NMOSD and MOGAD imaging and clinical importance. [6+4]**

NMOSD (aquaporin-4 antibody) characteristically causes longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis, a cord lesion spanning three or more vertebral segments, often central or holocord, together with optic neuritis that is frequently posterior or chiasmal. Brain lesions cluster in AQP4-rich periependymal regions — around the third and fourth ventricles, the hypothalamus, and the area postrema, the last producing the area postrema syndrome of intractable vomiting and hiccups. MOGAD also produces optic neuritis (often anterior, with perineural and optic-nerve-sheath enhancement), ADEM-like brain lesions, and cord/conus involvement; many MOG brain lesions are reversible on follow-up.

These contrast with MS, where cord lesions are short-segment and peripheral and brain plaques are ovoid and periventricular (Dawson fingers). Distinguishing NMOSD/MOGAD from MS is clinically critical because the disease-modifying therapies used for MS (certain interferons and other agents) can worsen NMOSD, and management, prognosis, and antibody testing differ. Imaging that shows longitudinally extensive cord disease, optic chiasmal involvement, or periependymal/area-postrema lesions should prompt antibody testing rather than a default MS diagnosis. Recognition therefore directly changes treatment and prevents harm.

**4. PML in HIV and differentiation. [5+5]**

Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy is a JC virus infection of oligodendrocytes occurring in profound immunosuppression (advanced HIV/AIDS, natalizumab therapy). MRI shows asymmetric, confluent, subcortical white-matter T2/FLAIR hyperintensity that involves the subcortical U-fibres, with a predilection for parieto-occipital white matter and the middle cerebellar peduncles. Characteristically there is no mass effect and no enhancement; lesions progress steadily and do not respect vascular territories. DWI may show restricted diffusion at the advancing leading edge. The appearance of new enhancement suggests immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS), often after antiretroviral therapy is started.

Differentiation: HIV encephalopathy produces symmetric periventricular white-matter change that tends to spare the U-fibres and lacks the asymmetry and progression of PML. Osmotic demyelination is symmetric and central in the pons with a different clinical setting (rapid sodium correction). Acute infarction respects vascular territories and shows restricted diffusion throughout the core. CNS lymphoma and toxoplasmosis are mass lesions with enhancement and mass effect, unlike non-enhancing PML. The triad of an immunosuppressed patient with asymmetric, U-fibre-involving, non-enhancing, non-mass-producing white-matter disease is the key to PML; clinical history and JC virus PCR confirm it.

**5. Neurodegenerative and atypical parkinsonian patterns. [10]**

Imaging of dementia and atypical parkinsonism is read off regional atrophy and a few specific signs; MRI (with coronal T1 angled to the hippocampus and midsagittal views) is the dominant modality, with CT used to exclude surgical causes and PET/SPECT as problem-solving adjuncts.

Alzheimer disease shows disproportionate medial temporal lobe and hippocampal atrophy with widening of the choroid fissure and dilated temporal horns, progressing to parietotemporal cortical atrophy; FDG-PET shows temporoparietal and posterior cingulate hypometabolism. Frontotemporal dementia shows focal frontal and/or anterior temporal lobar atrophy, often asymmetric with knife-edge gyri, and frontotemporal hypometabolism. Normal-pressure hydrocephalus shows communicating ventriculomegaly disproportionate to sulcal atrophy, with tight high-convexity sulci and widened Sylvian fissures (the DESH pattern) and a narrowed (acute) callosal angle; an aqueductal flow void may be seen.

Among atypical parkinsonian syndromes, progressive supranuclear palsy shows midbrain tegmental atrophy producing the hummingbird (penguin) sign on midsagittal images and a reduced midbrain-to-pons ratio (verify exact value). Multiple system atrophy of cerebellar type shows the pontine hot-cross-bun sign (cruciform T2 hyperintensity from degeneration of transverse pontocerebellar fibres) and middle cerebellar peduncle atrophy, while the parkinsonian type shows putaminal atrophy with a hyperintense rim and T2-hypointense putamen. Naming the key sign for each — hippocampal atrophy (Alzheimer), frontotemporal atrophy (FTD), DESH (NPH), hummingbird (PSP), hot-cross-bun (MSA) — is the high-yield examination point.
