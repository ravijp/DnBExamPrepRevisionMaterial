## Complete answers

### Answer 1 (Classification and location-based aetiology) [6+4]

Intracranial haemorrhage is first classified by **compartment**. **Intra-axial** bleeds are intraparenchymal (IPH) and intraventricular (IVH). **Extra-axial** bleeds are epidural (extradural), subdural, and subarachnoid; epidural is biconvex and arterial (middle meningeal artery), subdural is crescentic and venous (bridging veins), and subarachnoid fills the cisterns and sulci. This compartmental approach is the most useful first step because the location dictates the differential and the management.

For **intraparenchymal** haemorrhage, location strongly predicts cause. **Hypertensive** haemorrhage is **deep and central**: the putamen/external capsule, thalamus, pons and cerebellar dentate. It arises from rupture of small perforating vessels (lenticulostriate and thalamoperforators) damaged by chronic hypertension, classically with Charcot-Bouchard type microaneurysms. On SWI the associated chronic microbleeds also lie in a deep distribution.

**Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA)** produces **lobar, peripheral** haemorrhage in the **elderly**, characteristically **sparing the deep grey nuclei**, and is often recurrent. It reflects amyloid-beta deposition in cortical and leptomeningeal vessels, and is accompanied by multiple peripheral cortical microbleeds and superficial siderosis on SWI in a normotensive patient.

Thus a deep bleed in a known hypertensive points to hypertensive aetiology, whereas a peripheral lobar bleed in an elderly normotensive patient, especially if recurrent, points to amyloid. Other parenchymal causes to exclude are an underlying tumour (disproportionate oedema, enhancement, multiplicity), a vascular malformation, and haemorrhagic transformation of infarct.

### Answer 2 (MRI ageing of haematoma) [7+3]

The MRI appearance of a parenchymal haematoma evolves predictably as haemoglobin degrades, driven by changes in magnetic susceptibility and whether the iron compound is intracellular or extracellular.

- **Hyperacute (first day)** — **oxyhaemoglobin**, intracellular. T1 iso to slightly low, T2 high (bright).
- **Acute (~1-3 days)** — **deoxyhaemoglobin**, intracellular and paramagnetic. T1 iso to low, T2 low (dark).
- **Early subacute (~3-7 days)** — **intracellular methaemoglobin**. T1 becomes **bright** (the key sign), T2 remains dark.
- **Late subacute (~1 week onward)** — **extracellular methaemoglobin** after red-cell lysis. Both T1 and T2 are bright.
- **Chronic (months onward)** — **haemosiderin and ferritin** in macrophages. Both T1 and T2 are low, leaving a characteristic **dark haemosiderin rim**.

(In vivo timings are approximate and overlap — verify exact windows.) The two most testable points are the **T1-bright early-subacute clot** (extracellular and intracellular methaemoglobin) and the **chronic haemosiderin dark rim**.

**SWI / GRE (T2\*):** susceptibility-weighted imaging is exquisitely sensitive to the paramagnetic breakdown products. Old blood, microbleeds and the haemosiderin rim **bloom** dark and appear larger than their true size. SWI detects microbleeds (peripheral in amyloid, deep in hypertensive), superficial siderosis, and the blooming popcorn core of a cavernoma. A caveat is that calcium also blooms, so phase imaging is needed to separate calcium from blood.

### Answer 3 (Aneurysmal SAH work-up) [4+6]

In a patient with thunderclap headache, the **first investigation is non-contrast CT (NCCT)**, which is highly sensitive for acute subarachnoid blood in the first hours and shows hyperdense material in the basal cisterns, sylvian and interhemispheric fissures and sulci. The distribution hints at the source: anterior interhemispheric suggests an anterior communicating artery aneurysm, and sylvian blood suggests a middle cerebral or posterior communicating aneurysm. If CT is negative but suspicion is high, **lumbar puncture** for xanthochromia is performed, because CT sensitivity falls as blood washes out over days.

The **Fisher concept** relates the **amount and distribution of cisternal blood on CT to the risk of vasospasm**: thicker clot and the presence of intraventricular blood predict a higher vasospasm risk (the modified Fisher scale formalises this; the exact grade cut-offs should be verified rather than quoted from memory).

To localise the aneurysm, **CTA** is fast and excellent for planning, while **DSA remains the gold standard**, defining neck morphology, dome-to-neck ratio and daughter sacs, and allowing endovascular treatment (coiling). **If CTA is negative after a definite SAH, DSA is mandatory, and is repeated if the first study is negative.** An exception is **perimesencephalic non-aneurysmal SAH**, where blood is confined to the prepontine/perimesencephalic region with a normal angiogram and a benign, presumed venous, prognosis.

**Vasospasm** causing delayed cerebral ischaemia typically develops in a delayed window (around days 4-14, peaking near day 7 — verify exact window). It is screened with transcranial Doppler, assessed with CT perfusion, and confirmed and treated at DSA.

### Answer 4 (EDH vs SDH vs SAH on CT) [10]

All three are best assessed on **non-contrast CT**, where acute blood is hyperdense.

**Extradural (epidural) haematoma (EDH):** a **biconvex (lentiform)** collection between the skull and dura. It is classically **arterial**, from middle meningeal artery injury, often associated with a temporoparietal fracture and a lucid interval. It **does not cross sutures** (the dura is tightly attached there) but **can cross the midline / dural folds**. A swirl sign indicates active bleeding.

**Subdural haematoma (SDH):** a **crescentic** collection between dura and arachnoid. It is **venous** (torn bridging veins), common in the elderly, alcoholics and in non-accidental injury in infants. It **crosses sutures** but **does not cross the midline** (limited by the falx). Density changes with age: acute is hyperdense, and the **subacute phase becomes isodense** to brain (a classic trap — look for mass effect, sulcal effacement and grey-white interface displacement), and chronic is hypodense. Mixed density suggests acute-on-chronic or active bleeding.

**Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH):** hyperdense blood within the **CSF spaces** — basal cisterns, sylvian and interhemispheric fissures and sulci, rather than a focal lens or crescent. Trauma is the commonest cause overall; aneurysm rupture is the commonest non-traumatic cause. The distribution suggests the aneurysm location, and complications include hydrocephalus, rebleeding and vasospasm.

| Feature | EDH | SDH | SAH |
|---|---|---|---|
| Shape | Biconvex | Crescentic | Fills cisterns/sulci |
| Source | Arterial (MMA) | Venous (bridging) | Aneurysm/trauma |
| Crosses sutures | No | Yes | n/a |
| Crosses midline | Yes | No | n/a |

### Answer 5 (Vascular malformations and roles of SWI/DSA) [6+4]

**AVM (pial):** a nidus of tangled abnormal vessels with **enlarged feeding arteries and early-draining veins**. On MRI it shows serpentine **flow voids**; DSA demonstrates the shunt and any associated flow-related or intranidal aneurysms. It is a high-flow lesion with a significant bleed risk, graded for surgical risk by a scheme combining size, eloquence of adjacent brain and venous drainage (component concepts; verify exact points).

**Dural AV fistula:** a direct shunt from arteries into a dural sinus or vein, often adjacent to a sinus. The danger feature is **cortical venous reflux**, which raises bleed risk. It is a DSA diagnosis.

**Cavernoma (cavernous malformation):** a low-flow, **angiographically occult** lesion with a **popcorn / mulberry** mixed-signal core and a **complete haemosiderin rim** that **blooms** on SWI. It causes recurrent small bleeds and frequently coexists with a DVA.

**Developmental venous anomaly (DVA):** a **caput medusae** of dilated medullary veins converging on a single enlarged collecting vein, seen on the venous phase. It is **benign and drains normal brain, so it must not be resected**.

**Capillary telangiectasia:** a low-flow lesion favouring the **pons**, showing faint brush-like enhancement and subtle SWI signal, with negligible bleed risk.

**Roles:** **SWI** detects the blooming of cavernomas, the caput medusae of DVAs, and capillary telangiectasias, and characterises associated microbleeds. **DSA** is the gold standard for the high-flow lesions (AVM, dAVF), defining angioarchitecture and enabling embolisation. **Cavernomas and capillary telangiectasias are angiographically occult**; a **DVA is NOT occult** — it shows the classic **"caput medusae"** of dilated medullary veins draining into a collecting vein on the venous phase.
