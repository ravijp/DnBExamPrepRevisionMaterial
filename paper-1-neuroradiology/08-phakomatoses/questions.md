## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — NF1 features; OPG and plexiform NF on MRI [6+4]

Neurofibromatosis type 1 (von Recklinghausen disease) is an autosomal dominant disorder caused by a neurofibromin gene mutation on chromosome 17. Its imaging features span the CNS, bone and peripheral nerves.

CNS findings: optic pathway glioma (low-grade pilocytic astrocytoma — bilateral optic nerve involvement is essentially pathognomonic); focal areas of signal intensity (FASI / "NF bright spots" / UBOs) in globi pallidi, thalami, brainstem and cerebellar white matter; other gliomas (notably brainstem); and a generally increased intracranial tumour risk. Skeletal findings: sphenoid wing dysplasia producing the "bare/empty orbit" sign and pulsatile exophthalmos, posterior vertebral body scalloping from dural ectasia, lateral thoracic meningoceles, ribbon ribs, scoliosis and long-bone pseudoarthrosis (classically tibia). Peripheral findings: cutaneous and plexiform neurofibromas, dumbbell spinal neurofibromas widening neural foramina, with risk of malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumour.

On MRI, optic pathway glioma shows fusiform enlargement, elongation and kinking of the optic nerve, chiasm or tracts; the lesion is T2-hyperintense with variable enhancement and is usually indolent. A plexiform neurofibroma is a transspatial, infiltrative nerve-sheath mass ("bag of worms"), T1-isointense and T2-hyperintense, classically demonstrating the target sign — central low T2 signal with peripheral high signal. New rapid growth, heterogeneity or restricted diffusion/FDG avidity raises concern for malignant transformation. FASI, by contrast, show no mass effect or enhancement and tend to regress in adulthood, and should not be mistaken for tumour.

### Answer 2 — NF1 vs NF2; MISME [6+4]

Both neurofibromatoses are autosomal dominant but are genetically and phenotypically distinct. NF1 results from a neurofibromin mutation on chromosome 17 and is far commoner; NF2 results from a merlin/schwannomin mutation on chromosome 22.

NF1 is dominated by peripheral nerve, cutaneous and skeletal disease: cafe-au-lait macules, axillary/inguinal freckling, Lisch nodules (iris hamartomas), cutaneous and plexiform neurofibromas, sphenoid wing dysplasia, scoliosis and pseudoarthrosis. Its characteristic CNS lesions are optic pathway glioma and FASI. NF2, in contrast, has minimal skin and bone disease and is dominated by multiple intracranial and intraspinal tumours, with posterior subcapsular cataracts as an associated finding.

The defining lesion of NF2 is bilateral vestibular (acoustic) schwannomas — avidly enhancing internal auditory canal/cerebellopontine angle masses with an "ice-cream-on-cone" shape. The MISME complex summarises the NF2 tumour spectrum: Multiple Inherited Schwannomas, Meningiomas and Ependymomas. Thus an NF2 patient may show bilateral vestibular schwannomas, additional cranial/spinal nerve schwannomas, multiple dural-based meningiomas (often with a dural tail and possible calcification), and spinal cord ependymomas.

Imaging distinction therefore rests on pattern recognition: a young patient with bilateral CPA masses, multiple meningiomas or spinal ependymomas points to NF2, whereas optic pathway glioma, plexiform neurofibroma, FASI and sphenoid wing dysplasia point to NF1. A useful shorthand is "NF1 = peripheral/cafe-au-lait" versus "NF2 = central/MISME."

### Answer 3 — Tuberous sclerosis CT and MRI; lesion needing follow-up [7+3]

Tuberous sclerosis complex is an autosomal dominant hamartomatous disorder (*TSC1*/hamartin on chromosome 9 or *TSC2*/tuberin on chromosome 16). Four characteristic intracranial lesions are described.

On CT, the disease is calcification-friendly. Subependymal nodules line the lateral ventricular walls along the caudothalamic groove and calcify with age, producing a "candle-guttering" appearance. Cortical and subcortical tubers may also calcify. A soft-tissue or partly calcified mass at the foramen of Monro represents a subependymal giant cell astrocytoma (SEGA), which may obstruct CSF flow and cause hydrocephalus.

On MRI, cortical/subcortical tubers are expanded gyri that are T2/FLAIR-hyperintense in older children and adults; in neonates they show reversed signal (T1-hyperintense, T2-hypointense) because of incomplete myelination, and generally do not enhance. Subependymal nodules project into the ventricle, may calcify and may enhance. Radial migration lines (radial bands) are linear T2/FLAIR-hyperintense bands running from ventricle to cortex along neuronal migration paths. SEGA is an enhancing mass at or near the foramen of Monro.

The lesion that mandates follow-up is the subependymal nodule near the foramen of Monro, because of its potential to enlarge into a SEGA. Serial imaging is warranted for an interval-enlarging subependymal lesion or one causing ventricular obstruction, since a growing SEGA can produce obstructive hydrocephalus and may require surgical or medical (mTOR inhibitor) treatment.

### Answer 4 — Sturge-Weber imaging and pathophysiology [6+4]

Sturge-Weber syndrome (encephalotrigeminal angiomatosis) is a sporadic vascular phakomatosis (somatic *GNAQ* mutation) characterised by a facial port-wine stain in the trigeminal V1 (and often V2) distribution, an ipsilateral leptomeningeal (pial) angioma, and ocular involvement (glaucoma, choroidal angioma).

Imaging: plain skull films may show late gyriform parallel-line ("tram-track" or "railroad-track") calcification, usually parieto-occipital. CT is more sensitive for this gyriform cortical/subcortical calcification and also shows ipsilateral cerebral hemiatrophy with compensatory calvarial thickening and skull base elevation, and an enlarged ipsilateral choroid plexus. MRI best demonstrates the underlying pial angioma as leptomeningeal enhancement on post-contrast T1, together with hemiatrophy, accelerated/abnormal myelination, an enlarged and avidly enhancing ipsilateral choroid plexus, and prominent deep/medullary collateral veins with a paucity of normal superficial cortical veins. SWI/GRE is sensitive for both the calcium (blooming) and the abnormal venous drainage; FDG-PET shows hypometabolism in the affected hemisphere and aids epilepsy surgical planning.

Pathophysiological basis: the leptomeningeal angioma is associated with absent or insufficient normal superficial cortical venous drainage, so blood is diverted through deep medullary and subependymal collateral veins. The resulting chronic venous stasis and cortical hypoxia/ischaemia produce progressive cortical laminar necrosis with dystrophic gyriform calcification, neuronal loss and the hemiatrophy seen on imaging. The enlarged choroid plexus reflects collateral venous drainage through the deep system. These changes underpin the clinical seizures, hemiparesis and developmental delay.

### Answer 5 — VHL manifestations; haemangioblastoma appearance [5+5]

Von Hippel-Lindau disease is an autosomal dominant tumour-suppressor disorder (*VHL* gene, chromosome 3) predisposing to multiple benign and malignant lesions; surveillance is therefore whole-body.

CNS manifestations centre on haemangioblastomas, which may arise in the cerebellum, brainstem, spinal cord (and retina, as retinal haemangioblastomas). They are frequently multiple, and multiplicity in a young patient should prompt a search for VHL. Endolymphatic sac tumours of the temporal bone are also associated.

Systemic manifestations form an important checklist: clear cell renal cell carcinoma (a leading cause of mortality), phaeochromocytoma, pancreatic cysts and serous cystadenomas/neuroendocrine tumours, renal and epididymal/broad-ligament cysts. Imaging surveillance therefore combines brain and whole-spine MRI with abdominal imaging and ophthalmological screening.

Classic imaging appearance of haemangioblastoma: the prototypical lesion is a well-defined posterior fossa or spinal cyst with an avidly enhancing mural nodule that abuts the pial surface. The nodule is the solid neoplastic component and may show flow voids from prominent feeding and draining vessels, reflecting the lesion's vascularity; the cyst wall itself usually does not enhance. Solid (non-cystic) haemangioblastomas also occur and enhance intensely. On MRI the nodule is T1-hypo/isointense and T2-hyperintense with strong enhancement. The differential for a posterior fossa cyst-plus-nodule includes pilocytic astrocytoma (younger child, larger less-vascular nodule) and metastasis (older patient, known primary, surrounding oedema); pia-abutting location, flow voids, multiplicity and the VHL context favour haemangioblastoma.
