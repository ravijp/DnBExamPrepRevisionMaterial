## Practice questions

*Note: the following are practice questions only — they are DNB-style but uncited and have no recorded NBE past-paper origin.*

1. Classify sellar and suprasellar masses by compartment of origin, and describe the imaging protocol you would use to evaluate a suspected pituitary microadenoma. [4+6]

2. A 45-year-old presents with bitemporal hemianopia. Describe the MRI appearances of a pituitary macroadenoma, and explain how you assess cavernous sinus invasion. [6+4]

3. Discuss the imaging features that distinguish craniopharyngioma from a Rathke cleft cyst, including the role of CT and MRI. [6+4]

4. A child presents with central diabetes insipidus and a suprasellar mass. Give the differential diagnosis and the imaging features that help you narrow it. [4+6]

5. Write short notes on: (a) pituitary apoplexy; (b) the empty sella and the infundibulum sign; (c) why an aneurysm must be excluded before biopsy of a sellar mass. [4+3+3]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 [4+6]

**Classification (4).** Sellar-region masses are best grouped by compartment of origin. *Intrasellar*: pituitary adenoma (micro/macro), Rathke cleft cyst, pituitary hyperplasia, hypophysitis, metastasis, intrasellar aneurysm. *Suprasellar*: craniopharyngioma, meningioma (tuberculum/diaphragma sellae), germinoma, hypothalamic/optic-chiasm glioma, Langerhans cell histiocytosis, hamartoma of the tuber cinereum, aneurysm, and a suprasellar extension of an adenoma. The mnemonic SATCHMO (sellar-extension/sarcoid, aneurysm, teratoma/germ-cell, craniopharyngioma, hypothalamic glioma/hamartoma/histiocytosis, meningioma/metastasis, optic glioma) aids recall. *Parasellar/cavernous sinus*: meningioma, schwannoma, cavernous ICA aneurysm, cavernous haemangioma, perineural spread, metastasis. Patient age strongly weights the list (child: craniopharyngioma, glioma, germinoma, LCH; adult: macroadenoma, meningioma, Rathke cleft cyst).

**Microadenoma protocol (6).** MRI is the modality of choice using a high-field magnet with a small field of view and thin sections. The key sequence is **dynamic contrast-enhanced coronal T1**: a small bolus of gadolinium is given and rapid sequential coronal images acquired. The normal gland enhances early and briskly, whereas a microadenoma enhances more slowly, so it appears as a **relatively hypoenhancing focus** in the early phase (it may equilibrate or become hyperintense on delayed images — hence timing matters). Pre-contrast sagittal and coronal T1, coronal T2, and delayed post-contrast images complete the study. Indirect signs are sought: focal upward convexity of the gland surface, depression or erosion of the sellar floor, and deviation of the infundibulum away from the lesion. The bright posterior pituitary should be identified. Functioning microadenomas (prolactinoma, ACTH-secreting tumour in Cushing disease) are the usual indication; correlation with hormonal assays is essential.

### Answer 2 [6+4]

**Macroadenoma MRI (6).** A macroadenoma (>= 10 mm) is an intrasellar mass that expands the sella and grows superiorly through the diaphragma sellae. The constriction at the diaphragm produces the classic **"snowman", "figure-of-8" or "dumbbell"** configuration. It is usually isointense to grey matter on T1 and T2, enhances heterogeneously after contrast, and frequently contains cystic, necrotic or haemorrhagic components (the latter may show T1 hyperintensity or susceptibility blooming). Superior extension elevates and compresses the **optic chiasm**, the anatomical correlate of the patient's bitemporal hemianopia. Calcification is rare, which helps separate it from craniopharyngioma and meningioma. The sella is widened/ballooned, unlike meningioma, and the lesion arises from the gland rather than displacing it.

**Cavernous sinus invasion (4).** Lateral spread into the cavernous sinus is assessed with the **Knosp concept** on coronal post-contrast imaging. Tangent lines are drawn relative to the cross-sections of the intracavernous and supraclinoid ICA (commonly described as medial, intercarotid/cross-sectional and lateral tangents), and increasing extension of tumour beyond these lines correlates with rising probability of invasion. The strongest sign is **encasement of the cavernous ICA**, particularly when tumour surrounds more than about half the artery circumference, which predicts true invasion and incomplete surgical resectability. Loss of the normal cavernous sinus margin and asymmetric bulging also suggest invasion. (Exact grade thresholds vary between sources — describe the principle rather than over-quoting numbers.)

### Answer 3 [6+4]

**Distinguishing features (6).** **Craniopharyngioma** arises along the craniopharyngeal duct and is usually suprasellar with frequent intrasellar extension. The common *adamantinomatous* type (children, also adults) is the triad of a **cystic, calcified and enhancing** mass: the cyst fluid is often T1 hyperintense ("motor-oil" or machinery-oil fluid, rich in cholesterol and protein), there are enhancing solid/nodular components and a frequently enhancing cyst wall, and calcification is common. The *papillary* type (adults) is more often solid and non-calcified. **Rathke cleft cyst** is a benign developmental remnant of Rathke's pouch lying classically in the **pars intermedia** (between anterior and posterior lobes), intra- and/or suprasellar. It is **non-enhancing**, typically non-calcified, of variable signal (often T1 hyperintense from proteinaceous fluid), and may contain a small non-enhancing **intracystic nodule**. A thin peripheral rim of enhancement, when present, usually represents compressed displaced gland rather than a true enhancing wall — a key discriminator from cystic craniopharyngioma.

**Role of CT and MRI (4).** MRI characterises soft tissue, cyst content, the relationship to the gland, chiasm and hypothalamus, and the presence of enhancing solid tissue — favouring craniopharyngioma over the non-enhancing Rathke cleft cyst. CT is complementary and superior for **calcification**, which strongly favours adamantinomatous craniopharyngioma (calcification is rare in Rathke cleft cyst). CT also assesses bony sellar changes. In practice CT confirms calcium and MRI provides everything else.

### Answer 4 [4+6]

**Differential (4).** A child with central diabetes insipidus (DI) and a suprasellar mass should prompt consideration of: **germinoma** (midline, often with DI, may be suprasellar and/or pineal), **Langerhans cell histiocytosis** (thickened enhancing infundibulum, DI), **craniopharyngioma**, **hypothalamic/optic-chiasm glioma**, **hamartoma of the tuber cinereum**, **hypophysitis**, and less commonly metastasis or sarcoid. The combination of central DI with a thickened enhancing stalk and an absent posterior pituitary bright spot points strongly toward germinoma or LCH.

**Narrowing features (6).** Look first at the **posterior pituitary bright spot**: its absence supports central DI. A **thickened, avidly enhancing infundibulum** (> ~3 mm) suggests germinoma, LCH or hypophysitis; **germinoma** is typically a solid avidly enhancing midline mass that **restricts on DWI** and is exquisitely radiosensitive, while **LCH** is suggested by accompanying lytic skull lesions and other systemic disease. **Craniopharyngioma** is identified by a cystic, calcified, enhancing mass with motor-oil cyst fluid — CT confirms the calcification. **Hypothalamic/optic glioma** shows fusiform enlargement of the chiasm or optic pathways (consider NF1) with variable enhancement. **Hamartoma of the tuber cinereum** is a non-enhancing isointense pedunculated mass at the tuber cinereum associated with gelastic seizures and precocious puberty that does not change over time. Tumour markers (beta-hCG, AFP) and CSF cytology help confirm germ-cell tumours, and biopsy is reserved after vascular exclusion.

### Answer 5 [4+3+3]

**(a) Pituitary apoplexy (4).** This is acute haemorrhagic and/or ischaemic infarction of a pituitary tumour, usually a pre-existing macroadenoma, and is a neuro-endocrine emergency. The patient presents with thunderclap headache, sudden visual loss, ophthalmoplegia (cavernous sinus cranial nerves) and hypopituitarism (notably acute adrenal insufficiency requiring steroids). MRI shows an enlarged sellar mass with **blood products of varying age** — T1 hyperintensity of subacute methaemoglobin, susceptibility blooming on SWI, sometimes a fluid–fluid level — with reduced or peripheral enhancement of the infarcted portion. CT may show acute hyperdensity and is useful in the emergency setting.

**(b) Empty sella and infundibulum sign (3).** An empty sella is an enlarged sella filled with CSF, the pituitary flattened against the floor. *Primary* form results from a deficient diaphragma sellae allowing CSF pulsation (often incidental, linked to idiopathic intracranial hypertension); *secondary* follows surgery, radiotherapy, apoplexy or Sheehan syndrome. The **infundibulum sign** — a midline stalk descending through the CSF to reach the flattened gland — confirms that the CSF-filled sella is not a cystic mass.

**(c) Aneurysm before biopsy (3).** A parasellar or suprasellar ICA aneurysm can mimic a solid sellar mass. Clues include a **flow void** and pulsation artefact on MRI, laminated thrombus of mixed signal, a curvilinear calcified wall on CT, and avid luminal filling on CTA/MRA/DSA. Because biopsy of an aneurysm is catastrophic, vascular sequences must always be interrogated and an aneurysm excluded before any sellar mass is sampled.
