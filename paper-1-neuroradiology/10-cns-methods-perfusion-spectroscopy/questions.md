3. Describe the role of MR perfusion (DSC, DCE and ASL) in the assessment of brain tumours. How do perfusion and spectroscopy help to distinguish recurrent tumour from radiation necrosis? [6+4]

4. Write short notes on MR spectroscopy of the brain: principle, the main metabolites and their peak positions, and the spectral patterns of tumour and pyogenic abscess. [10]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — CT versus MRI in acute stroke; core versus penumbra [6+4]

Non-contrast CT (NCCT) is the universal first investigation in suspected acute stroke. Its primary purpose is rapid exclusion of haemorrhage (which appears hyperdense) before thrombolysis, and detection of any established large infarct or contraindication. Early ischaemic signs on NCCT are subtle: loss of grey-white differentiation, insular ribbon sign, sulcal effacement and a hyperdense vessel sign. CT angiography (CTA), performed in the same sitting, identifies the site of large-vessel occlusion, demonstrates the circle of Willis and cervical vessels, and assesses collaterals, enabling thrombectomy triage. CT perfusion (CTP) tracks an iodinated bolus to generate CBF, CBV and MTT/Tmax maps and quantifies core and penumbra.

MRI offers superior sensitivity: DWI becomes positive within minutes to hours, far earlier than CT, showing the infarct as bright on DWI with low ADC. FLAIR shows established oedema and helps estimate lesion age (DWI-positive but FLAIR-negative suggests a hyperacute, treatable lesion). GRE/SWI detects haemorrhage, and MR perfusion mirrors CTP. MRI is, however, slower, less available and harder in unstable patients.

Core versus penumbra (4 marks): the infarct core is irreversibly injured tissue, shown by markedly reduced CBF and reduced CBV (and restricted diffusion on MRI). The penumbra is hypoperfused but potentially salvageable tissue, shown by prolonged MTT and Tmax with relatively preserved or only mildly reduced CBV. The clinically relevant target is the perfusion-diffusion (or CBF-CBV) mismatch — a large penumbra with a small core favours reperfusion therapy, whereas a large completed core predicts limited benefit and higher haemorrhagic risk.

### Answer 2 — Diffusion-weighted imaging [4+6]

Principle (4 marks): DWI is a T2-based sequence with added diffusion-sensitising gradients that dephase moving water protons. Freely diffusing water loses signal, while water whose motion is restricted retains signal and appears bright on the DWI (high b-value) image. The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) is calculated from images at different b-values and displayed as a map. True restricted diffusion is bright on DWI AND dark (low) on the ADC map. This pairing is essential because a lesion that is bright on DWI but bright/normal on ADC reflects T2 shine-through — high signal carried over from the underlying long T2, not genuine restriction. Always confirm restriction on the ADC map.

Lesions with true restriction and the reason (6 marks):
- Acute infarction — cytotoxic (intracellular) oedema reduces extracellular water mobility; this is the earliest reliable sign of stroke, positive before CT change.
- Pyogenic abscess — viscous, cellular pus restricts, distinguishing it from necrotic/cystic tumour, whose central necrosis usually does not restrict.
- Epidermoid cyst — its keratinous solid contents restrict, distinguishing it from an arachnoid cyst, which follows CSF and does not restrict (and is incompletely suppressed on FLAIR).
- Hypercellular tumours — lymphoma, medulloblastoma and other small-round-blue-cell tumours and high-grade gliomas have densely packed cells with reduced extracellular space.
- Others — acute demyelination, herpes encephalitis, cortical ribboning of Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease, and hyperviscous or haemorrhagic states.

DWI thus adds tissue-characterisation and time information that conventional sequences cannot.

### Answer 3 — MR perfusion and spectroscopy in tumours; recurrence vs necrosis [6+4]

Perfusion techniques (6 marks). Dynamic susceptibility contrast (DSC) is a T2*/GRE technique tracking a gadolinium bolus; the transient signal drop yields relative cerebral blood volume (rCBV), the principal perfusion biomarker. Elevated rCBV reflects tumour neovascularity and correlates with higher glioma grade; it also helps target biopsy to the most vascular (most malignant) region and helps evaluate peritumoural infiltration. Dynamic contrast-enhanced (DCE) imaging is T1-based and models contrast leakage to give permeability parameters (Ktrans, Ve, Vp), reflecting blood-brain barrier integrity and complementing DSC in grading and response assessment. Arterial spin labelling (ASL) magnetically labels arterial blood as an endogenous tracer, giving cerebral blood flow without any contrast — valuable in renal impairment and serial paediatric studies.

Recurrence versus radiation necrosis (4 marks). After radiotherapy, both recurrent tumour and radiation necrosis can enhance and cause oedema, so conventional imaging often cannot separate them. Recurrent/progressive tumour typically shows elevated DSC rCBV and elevated DCE permeability, with MR spectroscopy showing raised choline and raised Cho/Cr (and Cho/NAA) reflecting active cellular proliferation. Pure radiation necrosis shows low rCBV and low metabolite levels, often with prominent lipid-lactate from tissue breakdown. In practice the picture is frequently mixed and thresholds vary by technique and institution (verify exact values), so a combined, multi-parametric interpretation alongside serial follow-up is recommended rather than reliance on any single ratio.

### Answer 4 — MR spectroscopy of the brain [10]

Principle: MRS exploits the small differences in resonance frequency (chemical shift, expressed in parts per million) of protons in different metabolites to produce a spectrum of peaks whose areas reflect metabolite concentration. It can be performed as single-voxel (one region, cleaner spectrum) or multi-voxel chemical-shift imaging (spatial distribution across a lesion). Acquisition uses different echo times; lactate is best identified at an intermediate TE where its doublet inverts below the baseline.

Main metabolites and approximate peaks:
- N-acetylaspartate (NAA) ~2.0 ppm — neuronal/axonal marker; reduced when neurons are lost or displaced.
- Choline (Cho) ~3.2 ppm — cell-membrane turnover; raised with proliferation (tumour, active demyelination).
- Creatine (Cr) ~3.0 ppm — relatively stable internal reference.
- Lactate ~1.3 ppm — anaerobic metabolism (necrosis, ischaemia, high-grade tumour, abscess).
- Lipid ~0.9-1.3 ppm — membrane breakdown/necrosis.

Tumour pattern: elevated Cho, reduced NAA and a raised Cho/NAA ratio; lipid and lactate indicate necrosis and higher grade. A rising Cho/NAA in peritumoural tissue favours infiltrative glioma over purely vasogenic metastatic oedema.

Pyogenic abscess pattern: the cavity shows cytosolic amino acids (valine, leucine, isoleucine), acetate, succinate and lactate, typically with low choline — a profile that helps distinguish abscess from necrotic tumour. MRS is therefore a non-invasive metabolic adjunct that complements diffusion and perfusion, though spectra must be interpreted with attention to voxel placement and contamination.

### Answer 5 — MRI sequence roles; fMRI, DTI, CSF-flow [6+4]

Standard sequences (6 marks):
- T1-weighted: best for anatomy; fat, subacute methaemoglobin, melanin and proteinaceous material are bright; CSF dark. It is the baseline for post-contrast comparison.
- T2-weighted: most pathology (oedema, gliosis, most tumours) is bright; CSF bright.
- FLAIR: T2 with CSF nulled, so juxta-ventricular and cortical lesions (MS plaques, cortical infarct, subarachnoid blood or meningitis) become conspicuous.
- DWI/ADC: detects restricted diffusion (acute infarct, abscess, epidermoid, hypercellular tumour); confirm on ADC to exclude T2 shine-through.
- SWI/GRE: susceptibility-sensitive, showing blooming of blood products, calcium and iron; SWI is more sensitive than GRE for microbleeds, cavernomas and small veins.
- Post-contrast T1: enhancement indicates blood-brain barrier breakdown; the pattern (ring, solid, leptomeningeal, ependymal) refines the differential.

Advanced techniques (4 marks): Functional MRI uses the blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) signal — neuronal activation alters local oxy/deoxy-haemoglobin and T2* signal — to map eloquent motor, language and visual cortex before surgery. Diffusion tensor imaging measures the directionality of water diffusion; fractional anisotropy is high in organised white-matter tracts, and tractography reconstructs major bundles (corticospinal tract, arcuate fasciculus) to show whether a mass displaces, infiltrates or disrupts them. CSF-flow imaging uses phase-contrast (cine) MRI to quantify and visualise flow at the aqueduct or foramen magnum, assessing aqueductal patency, obstructive versus communicating hydrocephalus, Chiari I to-and-fro flow, and shunt/third-ventriculostomy function.
