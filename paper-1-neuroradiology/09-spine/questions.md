## Practice questions

*Note: the following are practice questions, written in DNB style. They are uncited and are not drawn from any recorded NBE past paper.*

1. Describe the three-compartment approach to localising a spinal mass on MRI, with the imaging signs that separate extradural, intradural-extramedullary and intramedullary lesions, and give the principal differential for each compartment. [6+4]

2. A 45-year-old woman presents with progressive thoracic myelopathy. MRI shows an intradural-extramedullary enhancing mass. Discuss the imaging differential for an IDEM mass and the features that distinguish schwannoma, meningioma and drop metastasis. [5+5]

3. Outline your approach to the imaging diagnosis of an intramedullary cord lesion. Compare the MRI features of ependymoma, astrocytoma and haemangioblastoma. [4+6]

4. Classify the causes of myelopathy as compressive and non-compressive. Describe the MRI features that help characterise non-compressive (intrinsic) cord lesions by lesion length and cross-sectional location. [4+6]

5. Discuss the imaging of cord infarction and the role of MRI versus CT myelography in the evaluation of suspected cord compression. [6+4]

## Complete answers

### Answer 1 [6+4]

The first decision in any spinal mass is compartment localisation, because the compartment generates the differential. The lesion is assigned to one of three spaces by reading the cord margin and the subarachnoid (CSF) space.

An **extradural** lesion lies outside the dura and pushes the entire thecal sac away. The ipsilateral CSF space is effaced, the cord is displaced to the far side, and there is frequently associated bone disease (vertebral destruction, sclerosis, pedicle erosion) or disc disease. An **intradural-extramedullary (IDEM)** lesion lies inside the dura but outside the cord. Its signature is a widened CSF cap (meniscus) at the upper and lower poles of the mass, with the cord displaced and compressed against the opposite canal wall and contralateral CSF narrowing. An **intramedullary** lesion lies within the cord, which is fusiformly expanded, with symmetric narrowing of the CSF columns on both sides.

Compartment-specific differentials (4 marks): **Extradural** — metastasis (commonest), disc herniation, epidural abscess, epidural haematoma, lymphoma, myeloma/primary bone tumour. **IDEM** — schwannoma and neurofibroma (nerve sheath), meningioma, drop/CSF-disseminated metastases, myxopapillary ependymoma of the filum, dermoid/epidermoid. **Intramedullary** — ependymoma (central, adult), astrocytoma (eccentric, paediatric), haemangioblastoma, metastasis, and non-neoplastic mimics (demyelination, infarct, syrinx).

MRI with multiplanar T1, T2 and post-gadolinium sequences is the primary modality; CT myelography is the validated fallback when MRI is contraindicated, applying the same three-compartment CSF logic to the intrathecal contrast column.

### Answer 2 [5+5]

An IDEM mass is intradural but extrinsic to the cord: on MRI it produces CSF caps at its poles and shifts the cord to the opposite wall. The principal differential is nerve sheath tumour (schwannoma, neurofibroma), meningioma, drop metastasis, myxopapillary ependymoma of the conus/filum, paraganglioma, and dermoid/epidermoid or arachnoid cyst.

Distinguishing the common three (5 marks):

**Schwannoma** occurs in any adult and is multiple in NF2. It is typically eccentric to the cord and arises from a nerve root, often extending through an enlarged neural foramen as a dumbbell mass. It is T2 hyperintense, avidly enhancing, prone to cystic and haemorrhagic change, and may show a target sign (central low, peripheral high T2). Foraminal widening on CT/XR supports it.

**Meningioma** is classically a middle-aged woman with a thoracic lesion. It has a broad dural base, is often T2 iso- to slightly hypointense, enhances homogeneously and frequently shows a dural tail. Calcification (best seen on CT) favours meningioma over schwannoma.

**Drop metastasis** implies CSF dissemination from a CNS primary (medulloblastoma, ependymoma, GBM, germinoma) or a systemic cancer with leptomeningeal spread. Imaging shows nodular leptomeningeal enhancement coating the cord and cauda equina nerve roots ("sugar-coating"), often multiple, with predilection for the dependent lumbar thecal sac. CSF cytology is confirmatory.

The combination of foraminal dumbbell shape and target sign points to schwannoma; a dural tail with a broad thoracic dural base points to meningioma; multifocal root-coating enhancement with a known primary points to drop metastases.

### Answer 3 [4+6]

An intramedullary lesion expands the cord and narrows the surrounding CSF symmetrically. The approach (4 marks): confirm the lesion is truly within the cord; assess length of involvement and cross-sectional position; characterise on T1, T2 and post-gadolinium; look for cysts, haemorrhage and flow voids; and screen for syndromic associations (VHL, NF2). Most cord tumours are T1 iso/hypointense, T2 hyperintense and enhance, so the differentiating features are pattern, position and ancillary signs. Always consider non-neoplastic mimics (demyelination, infarct, inflammation) before committing to tumour.

Comparison (6 marks):

**Ependymoma** is the commonest cord tumour in adults. It is characteristically central and well-circumscribed, enhances, and classically shows a low-T2 haemosiderin cap at its poles with associated tumoural or reactive cysts and syrinx. The cellular cervical ependymoma is the usual subtype; myxopapillary ependymoma is a separate filum/conus lesion.

**Astrocytoma** is the commonest cord tumour in children and young adults. It is typically eccentric, infiltrative and ill-defined, often spanning a longer segment, with patchy enhancement and less clear margins than ependymoma; haemorrhage is less typical.

**Haemangioblastoma** is a strongly enhancing nodule, usually abutting the pial or dorsal cord surface, with a disproportionately large cyst and prominent serpentine flow voids from feeding vessels. Multiplicity raises von Hippel-Lindau disease, prompting a search for retinal, cerebellar, renal and pancreatic lesions.

A central tumour with a polar haemosiderin cap suggests ependymoma; an eccentric infiltrative lesion in a child suggests astrocytoma; an intensely enhancing peripheral nodule with a large cyst and flow voids suggests haemangioblastoma.

### Answer 4 [4+6]

Myelopathy is divided along a compressive versus non-compressive axis (4 marks). **Compressive** myelopathy results from an extrinsic structure indenting the cord: degenerative canal stenosis and disc herniation, epidural or intradural tumour, epidural abscess, epidural haematoma, and trauma with fracture or retropulsed fragment. MRI shows the offending structure and focal cord signal change at that level (myelomalacia/gliosis as focal T2 hyperintensity in chronic compression). **Non-compressive** myelopathy has no extrinsic compressor and reflects intrinsic cord disease, grouped as demyelinating, vascular, inflammatory/infective, metabolic/toxic, neoplastic and paraneoplastic.

Characterising intrinsic lesions by length and location (6 marks):

- **MS demyelination** — short-segment (under two vertebral bodies), peripheral and dorsolateral, occupying less than half the cord cross-section; acute plaques enhance; brain lesions support it.
- **NMOSD / MOG-associated disease** — longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis (LETM) of three or more segments, central or holocord, with cord swelling; area postrema and optic nerve involvement are clues.
- **Transverse myelitis** (idiopathic/post-infectious) — central T2 hyperintensity over one to several segments with cord expansion and variable patchy enhancement.
- **B12 deficiency / subacute combined degeneration** — symmetric dorsal (and sometimes lateral) column T2 hyperintensity in the cervical and upper thoracic cord, the "inverted V" or "rabbit-ears" sign on axial images, usually non-enhancing; copper deficiency and nitrous oxide abuse mimic it.
- **Dural AV fistula** — older patient with gradual myelopathy, long-segment central cord oedema with relative peripheral sparing and dorsal perimedullary serpiginous flow voids; the diagnosis not to miss.

CSF analysis (oligoclonal bands, aquaporin-4 and MOG antibodies, cytology) and serology are essential adjuncts.

### Answer 5 [6+4]

**Cord infarction** (6 marks) presents with acute, often painless, neurological deficit. The anterior spinal artery territory is most affected because it supplies the anterior two-thirds of the cord and has watershed vulnerability. On axial T2, infarction produces bilateral anterior grey-matter hyperintensity described as the "owl-eye" or "snake-eye" pattern. Sagittally there is a longitudinally oriented central/anterior cord signal abnormality with mild cord swelling. Diffusion-weighted imaging may show restriction acutely and supports the diagnosis, although cord DWI is technically demanding; enhancement is usually minimal early and may appear subacutely. An associated vertebral body endplate infarct is a helpful clue. Causes include aortic disease or surgery, profound hypotension, embolism (including fibrocartilaginous embolism) and vasculitis. The key differential is transverse myelitis and dural AV fistula, separated by clinical tempo, the grey-matter-restricted pattern and DWI.

**MRI versus CT myelography in cord compression** (4 marks): MRI is the primary modality. It directly shows the compressing structure, multiplanar extent, marrow signal, the soft-tissue and epidural component, and intrinsic cord signal change, and it differentiates tumour, disc, abscess and haematoma without ionising radiation or intrathecal contrast. CT myelography is reserved for when MRI is contraindicated (non-conditional pacemaker or implant, severe claustrophobia) or non-diagnostic (extensive hardware artefact). Intrathecal contrast outlines the thecal sac so the three-compartment logic still applies, and it demonstrates a complete block (cup-shaped column termination) and CSF leaks; its limitations are invasiveness, radiation, and poorer intrinsic cord characterisation. CT alone remains the choice for bony detail and acute trauma.
