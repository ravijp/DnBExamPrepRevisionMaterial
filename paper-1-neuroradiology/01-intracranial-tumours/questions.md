## Complete answers

### Answer 1 (Approach to a solitary mass; intra- vs extra-axial) [6+4]
A solitary intracranial mass should be analysed through an ordered checklist rather than by snap pattern recognition. First localise the lesion to a compartment (supra- vs infratentorial) and, crucially, determine whether it is intra-axial (arising within parenchyma) or extra-axial (arising from meninges, nerve, bone or ventricle). Next factor in the patient's age, then characterise margins, calcification, haemorrhage, necrosis, enhancement, diffusion and perfusion. Age and compartment together reshape the differential: a posterior fossa mass in a child is a primary tumour (medulloblastoma, pilocytic astrocytoma, ependymoma, brainstem glioma) whereas in an adult it is metastasis or haemangioblastoma; a supratentorial adult mass is most often glioma or metastasis.

The intra- versus extra-axial distinction is the highest-yield decision. Extra-axial signs are: a CSF cleft separating the mass from brain; inward buckling of the grey-white interface rather than its expansion; cortical grey matter displaced toward the ventricle; pial vessels swept around the lesion; a broad dural base with a dural tail; and bone reaction such as hyperostosis or remodelling. Intra-axial lesions instead expand the cortex, efface the grey-white margin and lack a CSF cleft. This single distinction redirects the differential entirely: an extra-axial mass points to meningioma or schwannoma, whereas an intra-axial mass points to glioma, metastasis or lymphoma. Finally, assessing single versus multiple lesions matters — multiplicity in an adult strongly favours metastases (or lymphoma/abscess/phakomatosis), while solitary lesions broaden the primary differential.

### Answer 2 (Meningioma) [7+3]
Meningioma is the prototypical extra-axial tumour. On CT it is typically a well-defined, broad dural-based mass that is iso- to hyperdense to grey matter and enhances avidly and homogeneously after contrast; calcification (psammomatous) is common, and adjacent bone may show hyperostosis. CT also demonstrates any aggressive or permeative bony change in atypical cases. On MRI the lesion is iso- to grey matter on both T1 and T2, enhances intensely and homogeneously, and shows a dural tail — a tapering enhancing thickened dura extending from the mass margin. A CSF cleft and a peripheral vascular rim confirm its extra-axial origin. There may be a broad dural base and reactive parenchymal oedema. Cystic, calcified and en-plaque variants exist, and rare aggressive subtypes show brain invasion and irregular margins.

Imaging signs confirming extra-axial location are the CSF/vascular cleft, inward cortical buckling, displaced pial vessels and the broad dural base with dural tail. A note on the differential: the dural tail is suggestive but not specific — dural metastases, lymphoma, sarcoidosis, tuberculosis and other dural-based processes can mimic meningioma. Multiplicity raises neurofibromatosis type 2. Avid homogeneous enhancement, a calcified psammomatous matrix and hyperostosis favour meningioma, whereas aggressive bone destruction, marked irregularity or systemic disease favour metastasis or an inflammatory mimic.

### Answer 3 (Butterfly ring-enhancing mass) [4+6]
A heterogeneous mass with thick irregular ring enhancement, central necrosis and a butterfly configuration crossing the corpus callosum in a 55-year-old is glioblastoma until proven otherwise. The principal differentials are: primary CNS lymphoma (which can also cross the callosum but is usually more homogeneously enhancing in the immunocompetent and ring-enhancing only in immunocompromised patients), solitary metastasis, tumefactive demyelination (typically an open or incomplete ring), and an abscess. Each has features that overlap on conventional sequences, so advanced MR is decisive.

Advanced MR contributes in three ways. Diffusion (DWI/ADC): glioblastoma shows restricted diffusion in its cellular rim but a non-restricting necrotic centre, whereas an abscess shows a markedly restricting (low ADC) central cavity — the single most useful discriminator from necrotic tumour; lymphoma restricts diffusion densely throughout. Perfusion (DSC, rCBV): glioblastoma shows markedly elevated rCBV in the solid enhancing tumour reflecting neovascularity, metastasis shows high rCBV within the lesion but characteristically low rCBV in the surrounding non-enhancing region (because peritumoural signal is pure oedema, not infiltrating tumour as in GBM), and lymphoma shows only modest rCBV despite high cellularity. Spectroscopy: tumour shows elevated choline and reduced NAA (high Cho/NAA), with a lipid-lactate peak indicating necrosis in high-grade lesions; an abscess shows cytosolic amino-acid, acetate and succinate peaks. Combining these techniques with the conventional appearance allows confident characterisation and guides biopsy targeting.

### Answer 4 (Paediatric posterior fossa) [4+6]
In a child, a posterior fossa mass is most likely a primary tumour, and the four core entities are medulloblastoma, pilocytic astrocytoma, ependymoma and brainstem (diffuse intrinsic pontine) glioma. The approach combines lesion location (midline vs hemispheric vs intraventricular vs intrinsic brainstem), CT density, diffusion behaviour and enhancement pattern, and includes whole-neuraxis imaging when seeding is a concern. Hydrocephalus from fourth-ventricular obstruction is common to several of these.

| Tumour | Location | CT density | DWI/ADC | Enhancement | Key clue |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Medulloblastoma | Midline, roof of 4th ventricle (vermis) | Hyperdense (cellular) | Restricts (low ADC) | Variable | CSF/leptomeningeal seeding; image whole spine |
| Pilocytic astrocytoma | Cerebellar hemisphere | Low (cystic) | No restriction (high ADC) | Avidly enhancing mural nodule | Cyst with enhancing mural nodule |
| Ependymoma | Floor of 4th ventricle | Mixed, often calcified | Variable | Heterogeneous | "Plastic" — extrudes through Luschka/Magendie |
| Brainstem (DIPG) glioma | Pons (intrinsic) | Low, expansile | Variable | Often minimal early | Engulfs the basilar artery |

The discriminators that earn marks: medulloblastoma is dense and restricts diffusion and seeds the CSF; pilocytic is a benign-behaving cyst-plus-nodule that does not restrict; ependymoma is the squeezed-out fourth-ventricular tumour with calcification; and brainstem glioma diffusely expands the pons and surrounds the basilar artery, frequently without early enhancement.

### Answer 5 (Advanced MR for grading and recurrence) [5+5]
(a) Glioma grading. Conventional MRI suggests grade through enhancement, necrosis and oedema, but advanced techniques quantify the underlying biology. Perfusion (DSC) measures relative cerebral blood volume: high-grade gliomas display markedly elevated rCBV reflecting angiogenesis, whereas low-grade gliomas show low rCBV — making perfusion the most useful grading adjunct. Spectroscopy demonstrates the metabolic shift of malignancy: choline rises with membrane turnover and NAA falls with neuronal loss, so a rising Cho/NAA ratio tracks grade, and a lipid-lactate peak signals necrosis typical of high-grade disease. Diffusion shows low ADC in hypercellular high-grade regions. Two pitfalls deserve mention: oligodendrogliomas may show elevated rCBV despite lower grade, and lymphoma is highly cellular (low ADC) yet only modestly perfused, so techniques must be interpreted together rather than in isolation (verify any quoted numeric ratio or cut-off).

(b) Recurrence versus post-treatment change. After chemoradiation, new or enlarging enhancement may represent pseudoprogression or radiation necrosis rather than true tumour. Recurrent tumour tends to show elevated rCBV on perfusion and elevated choline on spectroscopy, reflecting viable hypervascular hypercellular tissue. Radiation necrosis shows low rCBV and lacks elevated choline, and may show heterogeneous or diminishing metabolites over time. Diffusion findings are variable and supportive rather than definitive. No single parameter or threshold is reliable in isolation; the robust approach combines perfusion, spectroscopy and diffusion with serial follow-up imaging, and in selected centres amino-acid PET. The reporting conclusion should integrate all of these with the treatment timeline.
