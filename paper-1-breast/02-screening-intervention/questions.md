## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — Screening principles, density and supplemental screening [6+4]

Mammographic screening aims to detect breast cancer before it is clinically evident, shifting the stage at diagnosis to earlier, more treatable disease and reducing breast-cancer mortality. Organised programmes define a target population by age and a screening interval, screening asymptomatic women with full-field digital mammography in standard craniocaudal and mediolateral oblique projections. The MLO view is the most informative single view as it includes the axillary tail and pectoral muscle. Screening detects malignancy through masses, architectural distortion, developing asymmetries and, importantly, microcalcifications, the latter being a signature of early in-situ and invasive disease for which mammography is irreplaceable.

Screening must be framed as a balance of benefits and harms. Longer intervals reduce cost and radiation but increase interval cancers (those presenting symptomatically between rounds). Broader or younger screening increases recall, false positives and overdiagnosis. Common programmes screen approximately every two years within a defined age band, with some recommending annual screening in higher-risk groups (verify exact age and interval for the relevant jurisdiction).

Sensitivity is high in fatty breasts but falls progressively in heterogeneously dense and extremely dense breasts, because cancer and normal fibroglandular tissue have similar X-ray attenuation, so a cancer may be masked by overlapping dense tissue. Dense tissue is additionally a modest independent risk factor for breast cancer — a double hit of reduced sensitivity and increased risk. This justifies supplemental screening: whole-breast ultrasound for average-risk dense breasts (detects mammographically occult cancers, no radiation, but increases biopsies) and contrast-enhanced MRI for high-risk women, MRI being the most sensitive modality for invasive disease.

### Answer 2 — Digital breast tomosynthesis [6+4]

Digital breast tomosynthesis acquires multiple low-dose projection images as the X-ray tube moves through a limited arc over the compressed breast; these projections are reconstructed into a stack of thin (approximately 1 mm) tomographic slices. The governing principle is the separation of overlapping fibroglandular tissue plane by plane, which reduces the tissue-overlap masking that limits conventional two-dimensional mammography.

This principle yields two complementary benefits. First, improved cancer detection: by removing superimposed tissue, true masses and especially architectural distortion become conspicuous, with the greatest incremental benefit in dense breasts where masking is worst. Second, reduced recall rate: many recalls from 2D mammography are caused by summation or pseudo-lesions, where overlapping normal tissue mimics a mass; on tomographic slices these resolve into normal tissue, so unnecessary recalls fall.

In practice DBT is frequently paired with a synthesised 2D image reconstructed from the tomographic dataset, which reproduces a conventional 2D mammogram without a separate exposure and thereby limits radiation dose. A clinically important caveat is that DBT is most advantageous for masses and architectural distortion; assessment of fine microcalcifications still relies on conventional or synthesised 2D mammography with magnification views, as calcification characterisation is not the primary strength of tomographic slices. DBT therefore functions both as a screening tool, improving the detection-to-recall ratio in dense breasts, and as a problem-solving tool that clarifies whether a 2D finding represents a real lesion or simply overlapping tissue.

### Answer 3 — BI-RADS density and assessment categories [5+5]

BI-RADS describes breast density qualitatively in four categories reflecting the extent of fibroglandular tissue and its potential to obscure findings: (a) almost entirely fatty, with high mammographic sensitivity; (b) scattered fibroglandular density, with largely preserved sensitivity; (c) heterogeneously dense, where dense areas may obscure small masses; and (d) extremely dense, where sensitivity is markedly reduced and masking risk is highest. Density is a perceptual judgement of masking potential, not a precise percentage measurement.

Separately, the BI-RADS final assessment categories communicate the level of suspicion and the recommended action: 0 incomplete (needs additional imaging or prior comparison); 1 negative; 2 benign; 3 probably benign (short-interval follow-up given low malignancy likelihood); 4 suspicious (often subdivided 4a/4b/4c, requiring tissue diagnosis); 5 highly suggestive of malignancy (biopsy and appropriate action); and 6 known biopsy-proven malignancy. It is important to distinguish the density categories (a–d) from these assessment categories (0–6).

Density influences screening strategy because reduced sensitivity in categories c and d, combined with the modestly increased cancer risk associated with dense tissue, supports supplemental screening. Average-risk women with dense breasts may be offered whole-breast ultrasound or benefit from tomosynthesis, which reduces masking. High-risk women are best served by contrast-enhanced MRI, the most sensitive modality. Density reporting also informs patient counselling. Where exact density-prevalence percentages or risk multipliers are quoted, these should be verified against the current atlas edition.

### Answer 4 — Image-guided biopsy and localisation [7+3]

Image-guided breast biopsy is the pathology limb of triple assessment, and the guidance modality is selected according to how the lesion is best visualised. Ultrasound-guided core biopsy is first choice whenever the lesion is sonographically visible, such as most masses, complicated cysts and suspicious nodes, because it is real-time, fast and uses no ionising radiation. Core needle biopsy, frequently vacuum-assisted, is preferred over fine-needle aspiration cytology because it provides histological architecture, distinguishes invasive from in-situ disease and yields receptor status for treatment planning.

Stereotactic (X-ray-guided) biopsy is the technique of choice for microcalcifications and other lesions seen only on mammography. Vacuum-assisted devices improve calcification yield, and specimen radiography is performed to confirm that representative calcifications have actually been retrieved. MRI-guided biopsy is reserved for lesions seen only on MRI with no mammographic or sonographic correlate, typically after a targeted second-look ultrasound has failed to identify the lesion.

A marker clip is commonly deployed at the biopsy site so that a small, fully sampled, or neoadjuvant-treated lesion can be relocated later. For non-palpable lesions requiring surgery, pre-operative localisation guides the surgeon: wire (hookwire) localisation has long been standard, while seed-based and other wire-free markers are increasingly used and usefully decouple placement from theatre scheduling (verify device specifics locally). Selection is summarised simply: seen on US, use US core; calcifications or mammography-only, use stereotactic; MRI-only, use MRI-guided.

### Answer 5 — Triple assessment and concordance [4+6]

Triple assessment is the cornerstone of breast lesion work-up and combines three independent strands: clinical assessment (history and palpation), imaging (mammography with or without ultrasound and MRI, chosen by age and clinical context), and pathology (image-guided needle biopsy, with core biopsy preferred over cytology for definitive histology). Integrating all three strands maximises diagnostic accuracy and minimises both missed cancers and unnecessary surgery.

Concordance is the explicit radiological judgement of whether the histology result agrees with the level of imaging suspicion, and it is the radiologist's responsibility. A concordant benign result, where a benign histology adequately explains a benign or probably benign imaging appearance, allows routine or short-interval follow-up. A discordant result occurs when the histology does not explain the imaging — for example, a BI-RADS 5 lesion highly suggestive of malignancy that returns a benign core. Discordance must be assumed to represent sampling error and managed by repeat biopsy or surgical excision; a benign result that does not explain a suspicious image is never accepted at face value.

Borderline or B3 lesions add further nuance. Findings such as atypical ductal hyperplasia, radial scar, lobular neoplasia and papillary lesions carry an upgrade risk to malignancy on excision and frequently warrant surgical or vacuum-assisted excision despite a benign-appearing core, because adjacent malignancy may have been missed. The practical message is that the biopsy result must always be interpreted against the imaging, and any mismatch or borderline diagnosis triggers escalation rather than reassurance.
