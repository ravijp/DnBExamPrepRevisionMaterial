## Complete answers

### Answer 1 — BI-RADS categories 0-6 and management [6+4]

The Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System (ACR) gives every examination a single final assessment category that maps directly to management, ensuring uniform reporting and clear next steps.

- **Category 0 — Incomplete.** The assessment cannot be finalised on current data; additional imaging (spot compression, magnification, tomosynthesis, ultrasound) or comparison with prior films is required. This is principally a screening category and does NOT itself imply benign or malignant. Management: recall for further workup.
- **Category 1 — Negative.** Nothing to report; symmetric tissue, no mass, distortion or suspicious calcification. Management: routine screening. Likelihood of malignancy essentially 0%.
- **Category 2 — Benign.** A definitely benign finding is described (e.g. simple cyst, involuting calcified fibroadenoma, lipoma, intramammary node, surgical clips). Management: routine screening. Essentially 0% malignancy.
- **Category 3 — Probably benign.** A finding with a very low but non-zero malignancy risk (no more than about 2%; verify exact value), expected to be stable — for example a non-palpable circumscribed oval mass, a focal asymmetry, or a small group of round/punctate calcifications. Management: short-interval follow-up (commonly 6 months, then periodic surveillance to establish stability) rather than immediate biopsy.
- **Category 4 — Suspicious.** Malignancy risk above category 3 and below category 5 (more than ~2% up to <95%; verify). Subdivided 4A (low), 4B (moderate), 4C (high) to convey graded suspicion. Management: tissue diagnosis (image-guided core biopsy).
- **Category 5 — Highly suggestive of malignancy.** Classic malignant features (e.g. spiculated high-density mass, fine-linear-branching segmental calcifications); risk at least ~95% (verify). Management: biopsy and appropriate definitive action.
- **Category 6 — Known biopsy-proven malignancy.** Used when imaging an already-proven cancer (staging, neoadjuvant monitoring). Management: appropriate definitive action/treatment.

Key examination points: category 0 = incomplete (not benign); category 3 obliges short-interval follow-up and should not be overused; clinical-imaging discordance overrides a low category and warrants biopsy.

### Answer 2 — Mammographic signs of malignancy vs benign [5+5]

Mammographic abnormalities fall into masses, calcifications, architectural distortion and asymmetries; each has malignant and benign forms.

**Malignant signs.** The archetypal malignant mass is **spiculated**, irregular in shape, of high density, with associated architectural distortion. Indistinct or microlobulated margins are also suspicious. **Calcifications** are the key to in-situ disease: suspicious morphologies are amorphous, coarse heterogeneous, **fine pleomorphic**, and **fine-linear or fine-linear-branching (casting)**, and a **linear or segmental distribution** (following a duct system) markedly increases concern — the classic pattern of ductal carcinoma in situ. **Architectural distortion** without a surgical/biopsy explanation and a **new or developing focal asymmetry** are suspicious. Secondary signs include skin or nipple retraction, skin/trabecular thickening, and abnormal axillary nodes (enlarged, rounded, dense, with loss of the fatty hilum).

**Benign signs.** A **circumscribed oval mass** suggests a cyst or fibroadenoma. Benign calcifications have characteristic morphology: **coarse "popcorn"** (involuting fibroadenoma); **rim/eggshell** (fat necrosis, oil cyst, cyst wall); **vascular tram-track** parallel lines; **large rod-like secretory** calcifications of duct ectasia; **dystrophic** post-treatment calcification; and **milk-of-calcium** that layers dependently, giving a teacup appearance on the true-lateral view. A **fat-containing (lucent or mixed-density) mass** (lipoma, oil cyst, hamartoma, galactocele) is reassuringly benign, as fat density is not produced by cancer.

In practice, spot-magnification views characterise calcification number and morphology, and tomosynthesis confirms or dismisses distortion and summation asymmetries. The final synthesis is expressed as a BI-RADS category that determines whether the patient is followed up or biopsied.

### Answer 3 — Ultrasound benign vs malignant solid mass [6+4]

Ultrasound is the primary tool for characterising a palpable lump (especially in young or dense breasts), separating cystic from solid, and guiding biopsy. A simple cyst — anechoic, thin imperceptible wall, posterior acoustic enhancement, no internal vascularity — is benign (BI-RADS 2) and needs no further action unless symptomatic.

For **solid masses**, a structured lexicon distinguishes benign from malignant.

- **Orientation** is the single most useful sign: a **wider-than-tall (parallel)** lesion respects tissue planes and favours benignity, whereas a **taller-than-wide** lesion that grows across planes is suspicious.
- **Margins:** circumscribed margins favour benignity; **spiculated, angular, microlobulated or indistinct** margins are malignant features, and an echogenic halo/rim is suspicious.
- **Shape and echotexture:** oval/round, homogeneous, mildly hypoechoic with gentle macrolobulation suggests a fibroadenoma; marked hypoechogenicity and heterogeneity are concerning.
- **Posterior features:** **posterior enhancement** reassures, whereas **posterior acoustic shadowing** suggests malignancy (though some benign lesions shadow).
- **Other malignant pointers:** internal microcalcifications, ductal extension, architectural distortion, and increased disorganised internal flow on Doppler. Suspicious axillary nodes show eccentric cortical thickening, rounded shape and loss of the fatty hilum.

**Elastography** adds tissue-stiffness information: malignant lesions are typically stiffer (harder) than benign ones, so strain or shear-wave elastography can up- or down-grade suspicion. It is an adjunct only — it supplements the greyscale (B-mode) lexicon and local cut-off values should be verified rather than relied on as a single number.

The features are synthesised into a BI-RADS category; any solid mass that is not classically benign, or discordance with clinical findings, proceeds to image-guided core biopsy.

### Answer 4 — Breast MRI indications and kinetic curves [5+5]

Dynamic contrast-enhanced breast MRI is the most sensitive modality for invasive cancer but is less specific, so it is used selectively for problem-solving, staging and high-risk screening.

**Indications.**
- **High-risk screening** of women with a high lifetime risk (e.g. BRCA mutation carriers and equivalent risk groups — verify local thresholds), usually as an adjunct to mammography.
- **Extent-of-disease assessment** in newly diagnosed cancer: detecting multifocal, multicentric and contralateral disease before surgical planning.
- **Neoadjuvant chemotherapy monitoring** of treatment response.
- **Implant integrity** evaluation using silicone-sensitive sequences (the "linguine sign" indicates intracapsular rupture).
- **Occult primary** search in a patient presenting with axillary nodal metastatic adenocarcinoma and negative conventional imaging.
- **Problem-solving** of equivocal mammographic/US findings and assessment of suspected recurrence versus post-treatment scar.

**Kinetic (time-signal-intensity) curves.** After contrast, enhancing lesions are characterised by their initial uptake and their delayed-phase behaviour:
- **Type I — persistent/progressive:** signal continues to rise through the delayed phase; most often **benign**.
- **Type II — plateau:** an initial rise followed by a flat plateau; **intermediate/indeterminate**.
- **Type III — washout:** rapid early uptake then a fall in signal during the delayed phase; **most suspicious for malignancy**.

Interpretation always combines kinetics with morphology (mass shape/margin/internal enhancement and non-mass-enhancement distribution such as segmental or clumped patterns). Morphology takes precedence: a morphologically suspicious lesion is biopsied even with a type I curve, and a type III curve in a clearly benign-appearing lesion does not mandate biopsy on kinetics alone. Findings are reported with an MRI BI-RADS category.

### Answer 5 — Approach to a breast lump by age; gynaecomastia vs male cancer [6+4]

All breast lumps undergo **triple assessment** (clinical examination, imaging, and tissue sampling where indicated), with concordance required; discordance overrides a benign read.

**Imaging by age.** First-line imaging is chosen by breast density and pre-test probability.
- **Under ~30, and pregnant or lactating women:** **ultrasound first** — the breast is dense and glandular, the commonest lesion is a fibroadenoma, and radiation is best avoided; mammography is added only if there is clinical or sonographic suspicion of malignancy.
- **~30-39:** ultrasound, with mammography added if findings are suspicious — a transitional group where density is often still high.
- **40 and over:** **mammography first** (with targeted ultrasound) — cancer probability is higher and mammographic sensitivity improves as the breast involutes to fat.

A simple cyst needs no further action. A solid mass that is not classically benign, any suspicious mammographic feature, or a clinically suspicious lump despite reassuring imaging proceeds to image-guided **core biopsy**.

**Gynaecomastia vs male breast cancer.** Gynaecomastia is benign ductal/stromal proliferation, typically **subareolar, central, often bilateral and tender**, appearing mammographically as a **flame-shaped or fan-like density radiating from the nipple** (nodular and dendritic patterns), without discrete malignant features; causes include physiological states, drugs, liver disease and hypogonadism. **Male breast cancer**, by contrast, is usually a **hard, eccentric (not subareolar), unilateral** mass with malignant margins, possible pleomorphic calcifications and skin/nipple changes. The **eccentric location and malignant morphology** are the key discriminators; pseudogynaecomastia shows only fat without glandular density. Suspicious findings in a male breast are worked up exactly as in women, with biopsy of any eccentric solid mass.
